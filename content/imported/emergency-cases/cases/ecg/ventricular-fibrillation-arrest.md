# Ventricular Fibrillation Arrest

## Clinical Vignette
A collapsed pulseless patient has chaotic ECG activity without organized QRS complexes.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**No effective cardiac output; death without immediate resuscitation.**

## Key ED Clues
Pulselessness, unresponsiveness, chaotic rhythm, no organized QRS.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Start high-quality CPR and defibrillation pathway immediately according to local ACLS; search reversible causes after initial rescue actions.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Rhythm recognition, glucose/electrolytes when feasible, post-ROSC ECG and cause workup.

## Common Trap
**Trying to interpret fine details of the ECG before shocking a clear VF arrest.**

## Assessment Question
What is the defining feature of VF clinically?

A. Organized pulse.
B. Pulseless chaotic rhythm requiring immediate defibrillation pathway.
C. Normal QRS before every P.
D. Stable narrow tachycardia.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Wellens Pattern After Pain Resolves

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with recent chest pain is now pain-free but ECG shows deeply inverted or biphasic T waves in V2–V3.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Critical proximal LAD lesion with high risk of large anterior MI.**

## Key ED Clues
Pain resolved, minimal enzymes possible, characteristic anterior T-wave changes, usually preserved R waves and no major ST elevation.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Recognize as high-risk ACS equivalent, avoid stress testing, involve cardiology and follow local ACS pathway.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Serial ECG/troponin, cardiology evaluation, coronary imaging/intervention pathway per local system.

## Common Trap
**Discharging because pain is gone and troponin is low.**

## Assessment Question
What is the major trap in Wellens syndrome?

A. Overreacting to a harmless normal ECG.
B. Sending for stress testing or discharge because symptoms improved.
C. Treating as ankle sprain.
D. Ignoring all T waves in chest leads.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Hyperkalemia ECG Collapse

## Clinical Vignette
A dialysis or renal failure patient has weakness, bradycardia, wide QRS, peaked T waves, sine-wave pattern, or cardiac arrest.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Sudden malignant dysrhythmia from membrane instability.**

## Key ED Clues
Peaked T waves, PR prolongation, QRS widening, bradyarrhythmias, sine wave, renal failure, potassium-raising meds.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Place on monitor, stabilize myocardium and shift/remove potassium per local hyperkalemia pathway, do not wait for repeat lab if ECG is classic and patient unstable.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, potassium repeat, renal function, blood gas, medication review.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for lab confirmation while the ECG is screaming hyperkalemia.**

## Assessment Question
What is the most dangerous ECG progression in hyperkalemia?

A. Flat baseline with normal vitals.
B. QRS widening toward sine-wave pattern.
C. Isolated sinus arrhythmia.
D. Short PR only.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Wide Complex Tachycardia Stable vs Unstable

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has a wide-complex tachycardia and may be stable, hypotensive, altered, ischemic, or in heart failure.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Degeneration to arrest or incorrect drug choice in undifferentiated wide tachycardia.**

## Key ED Clues
Wide QRS tachycardia; instability signs include hypotension, AMS, shock, ischemic chest pain, acute heart failure.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Treat instability first using synchronized cardioversion pathway when indicated; if stable, assume VT until proven otherwise and avoid AV-nodal-blocker traps in uncertain rhythms.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, electrolytes, ischemia/toxin review, prior ECG if available, expert consultation.

## Common Trap
**Treating an undifferentiated wide-complex rhythm like simple SVT.**

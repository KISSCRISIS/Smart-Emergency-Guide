# Complete Heart Block with Instability

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has bradycardia, syncope, hypotension, chest pain, or heart failure with AV dissociation on ECG.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Low cardiac output, syncope, ischemia, or progression to arrest.**

## Key ED Clues
P waves and QRS complexes march independently, slow escape rhythm, wide escape is more dangerous, symptoms of poor perfusion.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Monitor, pads on, treat reversible causes, follow bradycardia/pacing pathway per local ACLS, involve cardiology early.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, electrolytes including potassium, ischemia workup, medication review, Lyme/infection context when relevant.

## Common Trap
**Calling it first-degree block because P waves are present.**

## Assessment Question
What ECG concept defines complete heart block?

A. Long PR with every P conducted.
B. AV dissociation between P waves and QRS complexes.
C. ST elevation only.
D. Irregularly irregular rhythm without P waves.

**Correct Answer: B**

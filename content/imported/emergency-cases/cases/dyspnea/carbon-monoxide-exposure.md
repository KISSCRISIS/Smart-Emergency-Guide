# Carbon Monoxide Exposure

## Clinical Vignette
Multiple patients from the same location develop headache, nausea, dizziness, syncope, chest pain, or confusion with a normal pulse oximeter reading.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypoxic cellular injury, myocardial ischemia, neurologic injury, fetal harm in pregnancy.**

## Key ED Clues
Shared exposure, winter/heater/fire context, cherry-red skin is unreliable, pulse oximetry may be falsely normal.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Remove from exposure, give high-concentration oxygen, assess neurologic/cardiac/pregnancy risk, consult poison center/hyperbaric pathway when indicated.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Carboxyhemoglobin level, ECG/troponin when symptomatic or high risk, lactate, pregnancy assessment.

## Common Trap
**Trusting the pulse oximeter to rule out CO poisoning.**

## Assessment Question
Why can pulse oximetry be misleading in CO poisoning?

A. It always reads low.
B. It may read falsely normal because it cannot reliably distinguish oxyhemoglobin from carboxyhemoglobin.
C. It measures potassium only.
D. It detects cyanide.

**Correct Answer: B**

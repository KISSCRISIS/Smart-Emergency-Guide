# Severe Asthma Near Respiratory Failure

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with asthma is exhausted, speaking single words, has silent chest or rising CO2 signs, and poor response to initial bronchodilator therapy.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Respiratory arrest from fatigue and dynamic hyperinflation.**

## Key ED Clues
Silent chest, altered mental status, inability to speak, accessory muscle fatigue, hypoxemia, hypercapnia, previous intubation history.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Escalate early: continuous monitoring, aggressive bronchodilator pathway, steroids per protocol, magnesium/ventilatory support considerations, and prepare for difficult ventilation if intubation becomes necessary.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Peak flow if feasible, blood gas only when it changes escalation, chest x-ray if complication suspected, ECG and electrolytes when severe.

## Common Trap
**Being reassured by less wheezing when the patient is actually too tired to move air.**

## Assessment Question
Which asthma finding is most ominous?

A. Loud wheeze with full sentences.
B. Mild cough only.
C. Silent chest with exhaustion and altered mental status.
D. Normal chest x-ray.

**Correct Answer: C**

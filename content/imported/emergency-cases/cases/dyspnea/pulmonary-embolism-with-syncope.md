# Pulmonary Embolism with Syncope

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with sudden dyspnea or chest pain collapses or syncopizes, especially with DVT risk or cancer/pregnancy.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Massive/submassive PE with RV failure.**

## Key ED Clues
Syncope, hypotension, hypoxemia, tachycardia, RV strain, DVT signs, risk factors.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Support ABCs, bedside echo/DVT ultrasound if unstable, escalate local PE response pathway when high risk.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, troponin/BNP, CTA if stable, POCUS when unstable, D-dimer only when low/intermediate appropriate.

## Common Trap
**Treating syncope as benign vasovagal without explaining dyspnea/hypoxemia.**

Which PE presentation is highest risk?

A. Mild pleuritic pain only.
B. Syncope or shock with RV strain.
C. Chronic cough only.
D. Normal breathing.

**Correct Answer: B**

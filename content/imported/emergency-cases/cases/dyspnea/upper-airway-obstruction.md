# Upper Airway Obstruction

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has stridor, drooling, muffled voice, neck swelling, facial burns, or rapidly progressive airway edema.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Complete airway obstruction during delay or failed intubation.**

## Key ED Clues
Stridor, inability to handle secretions, tripod position, expanding neck hematoma, burns/inhalation injury, deep neck infection signs.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Do not agitate unnecessarily; mobilize airway expertise early, prepare primary and backup airway plans, and keep surgical airway readiness when deterioration is likely.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Imaging only if stable; bedside nasopharyngeal/laryngoscopic assessment by skilled provider when appropriate.

## Common Trap
**Sending an unstable stridor patient to CT without airway planning.**

## Assessment Question
Which finding suggests upper airway obstruction rather than lower airway wheeze?

A. Expiratory wheeze only.
B. Inspiratory stridor with drooling or muffled voice.
C. Bilateral ankle edema.
D. Chronic cough.

**Correct Answer: B**

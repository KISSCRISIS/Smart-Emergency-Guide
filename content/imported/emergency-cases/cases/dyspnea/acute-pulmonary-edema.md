# Acute Pulmonary Edema

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with severe dyspnea, crackles, hypertension or known heart failure develops frothy sputum and hypoxemia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypoxemic respiratory failure and cardiogenic shock if pump failure progresses.**

## Key ED Clues
Diffuse crackles, orthopnea, severe hypertension or low-output state, B-lines on ultrasound, pulmonary vascular congestion.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Sit upright, support oxygenation/ventilation, reduce preload/afterload per protocol when appropriate, identify ACS/arrhythmia triggers, and monitor closely.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, chest x-ray, lung/cardiac POCUS, troponin, BNP as adjunct, electrolytes/renal function.

## Common Trap
**Giving large fluids to every dyspneic patient before deciding whether lungs are wet or dry.**

## Assessment Question
What bedside finding strongly supports pulmonary edema?

A. Diffuse B-lines with respiratory distress.
B. Isolated unilateral wheeze.
C. Normal lung ultrasound with pleuritic pain.
D. Localized calf pain only.

**Correct Answer: A**

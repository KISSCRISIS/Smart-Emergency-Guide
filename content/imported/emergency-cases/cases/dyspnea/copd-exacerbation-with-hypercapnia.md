# COPD Exacerbation with Hypercapnia

## Clinical Vignette
An older smoker with COPD presents with increased dyspnea, sputum change, wheeze, and somnolence or CO2 retention signs.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypercapnic respiratory failure, arrhythmia, or missed pneumonia/PE/heart failure mimic.**

## Key ED Clues
Pursed-lip breathing, wheeze, accessory muscle use, somnolence, asterixis, elevated CO2 on gas.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Support ventilation, use bronchodilator/steroid pathway, consider noninvasive ventilation when appropriate, search for trigger, and reassess mental status closely.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
VBG/ABG for ventilation, chest x-ray, ECG, viral testing when relevant, labs to find infection/metabolic triggers.

## Common Trap
**Treating every COPD dyspnea as COPD only and missing PE, pneumonia, pneumothorax, or ACS.**

A. Mild wheeze only.
B. Hypercapnic respiratory failure.
C. Normal sputum color.
D. Chronic cough history.

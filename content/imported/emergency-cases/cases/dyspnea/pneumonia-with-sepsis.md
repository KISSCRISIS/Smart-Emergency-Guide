# Pneumonia with Sepsis

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has fever, cough, dyspnea, hypoxemia, confusion, hypotension, or elevated lactate in the setting of suspected lung infection.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Septic shock and respiratory failure.**

## Key ED Clues
Infectious symptoms plus organ dysfunction: altered mentation, hypoxemia, hypotension, poor perfusion, rising lactate.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Recognize sepsis physiology, give oxygen/ventilatory support, obtain cultures when this does not delay antibiotics, start local antimicrobial pathway, and reassess perfusion.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Chest x-ray, CBC/CMP, lactate, blood cultures per local process, viral testing, POCUS for effusion when useful.

## Common Trap
**Calling it “just pneumonia” while ignoring shock physiology.**

## Assessment Question
Which feature makes pneumonia a resuscitation problem?

A. Cough with normal vitals.
B. Hypotension or altered mental status with suspected infection.
C. Mild sore throat.
D. Normal oxygen saturation only.

**Correct Answer: B**

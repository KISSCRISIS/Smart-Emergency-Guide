# Anaphylaxis with Airway Threat

## Clinical Vignette
A patient develops acute urticaria, wheeze, hypotension, vomiting, or lip/tongue swelling after food, medication, sting, or unknown trigger.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Airway obstruction and distributive shock.**

## Key ED Clues
Rapid multi-system involvement, hypotension, bronchospasm, angioedema, GI symptoms, exposure history may be absent.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Treat clinically; use IM epinephrine per local protocol, airway readiness, oxygen, fluids when shocked, adjuncts after epinephrine, and observe for recurrence according to policy.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
No test should delay treatment; consider tryptase only later/supportive context.

## Common Trap
**Using antihistamines first while delaying epinephrine in true anaphylaxis.**

# Ectopic Pregnancy

## Clinical Vignette
A reproductive-age patient has abdominal/pelvic pain, vaginal bleeding, syncope, shoulder pain, or shock.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Ruptured ectopic pregnancy causing hemorrhagic shock.**

## Key ED Clues
Positive pregnancy test, unilateral pelvic pain, bleeding, peritoneal signs, syncope, free fluid.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Pregnancy test early, stabilize if shocked, bedside ultrasound, urgent OB/GYN involvement when rupture or instability suspected.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Urine/serum hCG, transvaginal ultrasound when stable, CBC/type and screen, Rh status per local pathway.

## Common Trap
**Assuming contraception or patient history rules out pregnancy.**

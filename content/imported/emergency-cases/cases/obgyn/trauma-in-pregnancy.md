# Trauma in Pregnancy

## Clinical Vignette
A pregnant trauma patient presents after MVC/fall/assault with abdominal pain, contractions, bleeding, or decreased fetal movement.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Maternal hemorrhage, placental abruption, fetal compromise; maternal stabilization is fetal resuscitation.**

## Key ED Clues
Pregnancy stage, abdominal trauma, uterine tenderness, vaginal bleeding, contractions, seatbelt injury, shock.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Prioritize maternal ABCs, left uterine displacement when appropriate, trauma/OB involvement, fetal monitoring according to gestational age and local pathway.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Trauma imaging based on maternal need, Rh status, ultrasound/FAST, fetal assessment per OB.

## Common Trap
**Avoiding necessary maternal imaging solely because of pregnancy.**

## Assessment Question
What is the core principle in pregnant trauma?

A. Ignore the mother and assess fetus only.
B. Maternal stabilization comes first and usually best protects the fetus.
C. Never image a pregnant trauma patient.
D. Discharge all if fetal heart tones present.

**Correct Answer: B**

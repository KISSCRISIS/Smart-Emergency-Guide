# Ovarian Torsion

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has sudden unilateral pelvic pain, nausea/vomiting, adnexal mass, pregnancy, or intermittent severe pain.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Ovarian ischemia and loss if detorsion is delayed.**

## Key ED Clues
Sudden unilateral pain, vomiting, adnexal tenderness/mass, Doppler flow may be present despite torsion.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Control pain, pregnancy test, urgent pelvic ultrasound, early OB/GYN involvement when suspicion is high.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Pregnancy test, pelvic ultrasound with Doppler, CBC/urinalysis as adjuncts.

## Common Trap
**Ruling out torsion because Doppler flow is seen.**

Can normal Doppler flow completely exclude torsion?

A. Yes, always.
B. No, torsion can still be present if clinical suspicion is high.

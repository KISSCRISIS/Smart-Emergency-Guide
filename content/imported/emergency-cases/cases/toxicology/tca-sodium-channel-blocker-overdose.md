# TCA / Sodium Channel Blocker Overdose

## Clinical Vignette
A patient after overdose presents with coma, seizure, hypotension, wide QRS, terminal R in aVR, or ventricular dysrhythmia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Malignant dysrhythmia and refractory shock from sodium-channel blockade.**

## Key ED Clues
Wide QRS, hypotension, seizures, anticholinergic signs, aVR abnormalities.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Airway and seizure control, sodium bicarbonate pathway per local/toxicology guidance, continuous ECG, poison center consultation.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG serially, electrolytes, glucose, co-ingestion screen, acid-base status.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for a drug level instead of treating ECG toxicity.**

## Assessment Question
Which ECG clue is most concerning in TCA-like overdose?

A. Narrow QRS with normal vitals.
B. Wide QRS with hypotension or seizure.
C. Sinus bradycardia after sleep.
D. Isolated PAC.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Serotonin Syndrome

## Clinical Vignette
A patient taking serotonergic drugs develops agitation, hyperthermia, diarrhea, tremor, clonus, or hyperreflexia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, and cardiovascular collapse.**

## Key ED Clues
Inducible/spontaneous clonus, hyperreflexia, agitation, diaphoresis, GI symptoms, recent medication change or interaction.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stop serotonergic agents, supportive care, sedation/cooling pathway per local protocol, manage severe hyperthermia aggressively.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Medication review, temperature, CK, electrolytes, renal function, ECG, tox co-ingestion as indicated.

## Common Trap
**Confusing clonus/hyperreflexia with NMS rigidity and missing serotonergic trigger.**

## Assessment Question
Which exam finding strongly supports serotonin syndrome?

A. Lead-pipe rigidity without reflexes.
B. Clonus and hyperreflexia with serotonergic exposure.
C. Isolated pinpoint pupils.
D. Dry skin and absent bowel sounds only.

**Correct Answer: B**

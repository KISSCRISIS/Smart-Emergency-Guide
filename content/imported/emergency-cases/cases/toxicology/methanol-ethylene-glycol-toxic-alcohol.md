# Methanol / Ethylene Glycol Toxic Alcohol

## Clinical Vignette
A patient presents with intoxication, high anion gap metabolic acidosis, visual symptoms, renal injury, or calcium oxalate crystals after suspicious alcohol ingestion.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Severe acidosis, blindness, renal failure, and death.**

## Key ED Clues
Gap-gap pattern, osmolar gap early, visual complaints with methanol, renal injury/crystals with ethylene glycol.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Call poison center, block toxic metabolism per protocol, correct acidosis, arrange dialysis when indicated by toxicology guidance.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Blood gas, electrolytes, osmolar gap, toxic alcohol levels if available, renal function, urinalysis, lactate/ketones to avoid mimics.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for confirmatory level when the syndrome is classic and patient is deteriorating.**

A. Normal gap and mild headache only.
B. High anion gap acidosis after suspicious alcohol exposure with visual or renal clues.
C. Isolated rash.
D. Pure respiratory alkalosis only.

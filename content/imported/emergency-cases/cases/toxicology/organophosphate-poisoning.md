# Organophosphate Poisoning

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has pesticide/nerve agent exposure with salivation, lacrimation, bronchorrhea, wheeze, vomiting, diarrhea, miosis, fasciculations, or seizures.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Respiratory failure from secretions, bronchospasm, muscle weakness, and seizures.**

## Key ED Clues
Wet patient, pinpoint pupils, bronchorrhea, fasciculations, cholinergic crisis, multiple exposed patients possible.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Decontaminate safely, protect staff, prioritize airway/ventilation, atropine/pralidoxime/benzodiazepine pathways per local/toxicology protocol.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Clinical diagnosis; cholinesterase levels later if available; ECG, gas, electrolytes.

## Common Trap
**Under-treating secretions because the heart rate is not fast or because pupils are the focus.**

# Cyanide Toxicity in Smoke Inhalation

## Clinical Vignette
A smoke-inhalation patient has coma, shock, severe lactic acidosis, or cardiovascular collapse after enclosed-space fire.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cellular inability to use oxygen despite delivery, causing rapid cardiovascular collapse.**

## Key ED Clues
Enclosed fire, soot, altered mental status, hypotension, high lactate, concurrent CO exposure possible.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Give high-concentration oxygen, manage airway/burns, consider cyanide antidote pathway per local protocol, treat coexisting CO.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Lactate, blood gas, carboxyhemoglobin, ECG/troponin when indicated, burn assessment.

## Common Trap
**Assuming normal pulse oximetry excludes inhalational poisoning.**

# Salicylate Toxicity

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has tinnitus, vomiting, tachypnea, fever, confusion, or mixed respiratory alkalosis/metabolic acidosis after aspirin exposure.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cerebral/pulmonary edema, severe acidosis, and death if acidemia worsens CNS penetration.**

## Key ED Clues
Tinnitus, hyperventilation, anion gap acidosis, respiratory alkalosis, rising salicylate level, altered mental status.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Call poison center, correct hypoglycemia/electrolytes, alkalinization/dialysis decisions through toxicology protocol, avoid intubation unless absolutely necessary and expert-supported.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Serial salicylate levels, blood gas, electrolytes, glucose, renal function, chest x-ray if respiratory symptoms.

## Common Trap
**Intubating without matching the patient’s high minute ventilation, worsening acidosis.**

## Assessment Question
Why is intubation dangerous in severe salicylate poisoning?

A. It always cures acidosis.
B. Loss of compensatory hyperventilation can worsen acidemia and toxicity if ventilation is not matched.
C. It removes aspirin from the stomach.
D. It lowers salicylate levels directly.

**Correct Answer: B**

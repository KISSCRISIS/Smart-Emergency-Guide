# Lithium Toxicity

## Clinical Vignette
A patient on lithium has tremor, ataxia, vomiting, diarrhea, confusion, seizures, renal dysfunction, or drug interaction/dehydration.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Neurotoxicity, seizures, dysrhythmia, and prolonged toxicity due to renal handling.**

## Key ED Clues
Coarse tremor, ataxia, GI symptoms, confusion, chronic therapy, dehydration, renal impairment, interacting medications.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stop lithium, support volume/renal perfusion as appropriate, check levels serially, consult poison center/nephrology for severe cases and dialysis decisions.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Lithium level, renal function, electrolytes, ECG, pregnancy when relevant, co-ingestion screen.

## Common Trap
**Treating the lithium number alone without considering chronicity and neurologic symptoms.**

## Assessment Question
What makes lithium toxicity particularly concerning?

A. Only skin itching.
B. Neurologic symptoms such as ataxia/confusion with renal impairment or chronic use.
C. Normal exam.
D. Isolated sore throat.

**Correct Answer: B**

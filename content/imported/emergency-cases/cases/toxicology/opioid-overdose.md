# Opioid Overdose

## Clinical Vignette
A patient is found with coma, slow respirations, pinpoint pupils, or drug paraphernalia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Respiratory arrest and hypoxic brain injury.**

## Key ED Clues
Bradypnea, hypoxia, miosis, response to naloxone, co-ingestions possible.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Prioritize ventilation and oxygenation; use naloxone titrated to restore breathing per local protocol, monitor for recurrence.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, ECG, co-ingestion labs when indicated, acetaminophen/salicylate screen when unknown overdose.

## Common Trap
**Focusing on waking the patient fully instead of restoring adequate ventilation.**

## Assessment Question
What is the primary endpoint when reversing opioid toxicity?

A. Make the patient agitated.
B. Restore adequate ventilation and airway protection.
C. Normalize the pupils only.
D. Discharge immediately after one response.

**Correct Answer: B**

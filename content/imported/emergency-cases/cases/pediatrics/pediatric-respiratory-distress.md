# Pediatric Respiratory Distress

## Clinical Vignette
A child has tachypnea, retractions, nasal flaring, grunting, wheeze, stridor, or altered mental status.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Respiratory failure from fatigue or upper/lower airway obstruction.**

## Key ED Clues
Work of breathing, mental status, feeding ability, cyanosis, silent chest, stridor.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Assess appearance, work of breathing, and circulation; support oxygenation/ventilation; identify upper vs lower airway; avoid agitation in critical upper airway obstruction.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Pulse oximetry, glucose if altered, chest x-ray only when it changes management, viral/other tests by syndrome.

## Common Trap
**Using oxygen saturation alone to judge severity while work of breathing is severe.**

## Assessment Question
Which sign is most concerning in pediatric breathing?

A. Playful child with mild cough.
B. Decreasing mental status or exhaustion with respiratory distress.
C. Normal appetite.
D. Single sneeze.

**Correct Answer: B**

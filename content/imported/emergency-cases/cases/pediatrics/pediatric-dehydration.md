# Pediatric Dehydration

## Clinical Vignette
A child with vomiting/diarrhea has poor intake, lethargy, dry mucosa, delayed capillary refill, decreased urine, or sunken eyes.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypovolemic shock and electrolyte/glucose abnormalities.**

## Key ED Clues
Mental status, perfusion, tears, urine output, mucous membranes, weight change, tachycardia.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Classify severity, check glucose when lethargic, use oral/IV rehydration pathway based on perfusion and ability to drink, search for surgical mimics.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, electrolytes when severe/prolonged, stool tests only when indicated, abdominal evaluation for red flags.

## Common Trap
**Assuming all vomiting/diarrhea is benign gastroenteritis.**

## Assessment Question
What sign suggests severe dehydration/shock?

A. Normal play and normal urine.
B. Lethargy with poor perfusion.
C. One loose stool.
D. Mild thirst only.

**Correct Answer: B**

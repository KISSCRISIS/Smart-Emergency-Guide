# Pediatric Sepsis

## Clinical Vignette
A child has fever or hypothermia with poor perfusion, lethargy, respiratory distress, mottling, or hypotension late.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Rapid progression to shock and cardiac arrest.**

## Key ED Clues
Poor feeding, lethargy, delayed capillary refill, mottling, tachypnea, parental concern, hypotension is late.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Recognize poor perfusion early, support airway/breathing/circulation, start sepsis pathway per local pediatric protocol, reassess frequently.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, lactate if poor perfusion, cultures/labs, source-based imaging/testing.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for hypotension before calling it serious.**

## Assessment Question
Why is hypotension a late sign in pediatric shock?

A. Children compensate with tachycardia and vasoconstriction before BP falls.
B. It appears first in all cases.
C. It never occurs.
D. It only occurs in adults.

**Correct Answer: A**

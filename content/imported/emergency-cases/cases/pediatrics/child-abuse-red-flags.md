# Child Abuse Red Flags

## Clinical Vignette
A child presents with inconsistent history, delay in care, patterned bruises, injuries at different healing stages, or injury incompatible with development.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Ongoing harm, missed intracranial/abdominal injury, unsafe discharge.**

## Key ED Clues
Bruises on torso/ears/neck, frenulum injury, burns with pattern, multiple fractures, caregiver story changes, sentinel injuries.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Prioritize medical stabilization, document objectively, evaluate occult injuries per local child protection pathway, involve safeguarding team.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Imaging/labs guided by age/injury, skeletal survey pathway when indicated, head/abdominal evaluation if concern.

## Common Trap
**Accepting a vague explanation for a non-mobile infant’s bruise.**

## Assessment Question
Which finding is most suspicious?

A. Bruising in a non-mobile infant.
B. Small shin bruise in running toddler.
C. Mild scrape with consistent witnessed fall.
D. Old vaccine mark.

**Correct Answer: A**

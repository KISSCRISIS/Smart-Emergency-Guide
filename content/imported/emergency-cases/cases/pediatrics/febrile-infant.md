# Febrile Infant

## Clinical Vignette
A young infant presents with fever, poor feeding, lethargy, irritability, respiratory symptoms, or parental concern.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Invasive bacterial infection or sepsis with subtle signs.**

## Key ED Clues
Age-specific risk, poor feeding, lethargy, abnormal perfusion, apnea, temperature instability.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Use age-based local febrile infant pathway, assess appearance/perfusion, obtain indicated cultures/labs, and treat/observe according to risk.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Urine testing, blood tests/cultures, CSF testing depending on age/risk/local pathway, viral testing as adjunct.

## Common Trap
**Treating a young infant like an older child with simple viral fever.**

## Assessment Question
Why are febrile infants high risk?

A. They always look toxic.
B. Serious infection can present subtly and age changes workup.
C. Fever is never important.
D. Viral testing rules out bacterial disease completely.

**Correct Answer: B**

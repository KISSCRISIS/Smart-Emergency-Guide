# Pediatric Seizure

## Clinical Vignette
A child presents after seizure or ongoing convulsion with fever, trauma, toxin access, metabolic risk, or no return to baseline.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Status epilepticus, hypoglycemia, CNS infection, trauma, or toxic ingestion.**

## Key ED Clues
Prolonged seizure, focal deficit, age extremes, meningismus, head trauma, persistent AMS, medication/toxin access.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Protect airway/oxygenation, check glucose, stop active seizure per local protocol, identify febrile simple vs complex vs dangerous causes.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, electrolytes when indicated, infection/toxin/trauma workup, imaging if focal/trauma/persistent AMS.

## Common Trap
**Calling every febrile seizure benign before checking red flags.**

## Assessment Question
Which feature makes a pediatric seizure higher risk?

A. Brief generalized seizure with rapid full recovery in typical age.
B. Persistent altered mental status or focal neurologic deficit.
C. Child playing normally after event.
D. Known simple febrile seizure pattern only.

**Correct Answer: B**

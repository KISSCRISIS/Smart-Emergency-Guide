# Cardiogenic Shock

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with chest pain, dyspnea, pulmonary edema, cool extremities, altered mentation, or hypotension after MI/arrhythmia/heart failure worsens.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Pump failure causing systemic hypoperfusion and pulmonary edema.**

## Key ED Clues
Cool clammy skin, crackles, JVD, new murmur, ischemic ECG, low EF on bedside echo, poor response to fluids.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Avoid automatic large fluid boluses, identify ACS/arrhythmia/mechanical complication, support perfusion with local vasoactive pathway, involve cardiology/ICU early.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, troponin, bedside echo, chest x-ray/lung ultrasound, lactate, renal/liver perfusion labs.

## Common Trap
**Treating cardiogenic shock like dehydration and flooding the lungs.**

## Assessment Question
Which bedside clue supports cardiogenic shock?

A. Warm flushed skin after allergen exposure.
B. Cool extremities with pulmonary edema and poor LV function.
C. Isolated diarrhea with dry mucosa.
D. Urticaria only.

**Correct Answer: B**

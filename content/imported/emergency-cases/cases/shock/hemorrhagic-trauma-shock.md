# Hemorrhagic Trauma Shock

## Clinical Vignette
A trauma patient is pale, tachycardic, hypotensive, confused, or has positive FAST/pelvic instability/penetrating injury.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Exsanguination and traumatic cardiac arrest.**

## Key ED Clues
Mechanism plus poor perfusion, abdominal distension, pelvic instability, long-bone bleeding, positive FAST, anticoagulant use.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Control external bleeding, activate trauma team/massive transfusion pathway according to local protocol, minimize delays to hemorrhage control, prevent hypothermia.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
FAST, chest/pelvis imaging when appropriate, type and cross, coagulation context, lactate/base deficit.

## Common Trap
**Over-resuscitating with crystalloid while definitive hemorrhage control is delayed.**

## Assessment Question
What is the central priority in hemorrhagic shock?

A. Pain control only.
B. Rapid hemorrhage control with balanced resuscitation pathway.
C. Routine CT before stabilization in all cases.
D. Oral fluids.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Undifferentiated Shock

## Clinical Vignette
A patient arrives cold, confused, tachycardic, hypotensive, or poorly perfused without an obvious cause.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cardiac arrest from untreated obstructive, distributive, cardiogenic, or hemorrhagic shock.**

## Key ED Clues
Altered mentation, mottling, weak pulses, low urine output, lactate elevation, narrow or wide pulse pressure, abnormal POCUS findings.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Start resuscitation while categorizing shock: pump, tank, pipes, obstruction. Use monitor, IV/IO access, oxygenation, focused ultrasound, ECG, and targeted therapy.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, bedside echo/lung/IVC/FAST when appropriate, lactate, blood gas, CBC/CMP, type and screen, cultures when infection suspected.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for the full lab panel before treating poor perfusion.**

## Assessment Question
What is the best first cognitive step in undifferentiated shock?

A. Pick one diagnosis and ignore others.
B. Treat poor perfusion while sorting shock into pump/tank/pipes/obstruction.
C. Wait for CT before any resuscitation.
D. Give all patients the same fluid amount regardless of exam.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Adrenal Crisis

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with chronic steroid use, adrenal disease, sepsis, trauma, or vomiting presents with hypotension, weakness, abdominal pain, hyponatremia, or hyperkalemia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Refractory shock from cortisol deficiency.**

## Key ED Clues
History of steroid dependence/adrenal insufficiency, unexplained hypotension, GI symptoms, hypoglycemia, electrolyte abnormalities.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Treat shock supportively, give stress-dose steroid pathway per local protocol when suspected, correct hypoglycemia/electrolytes, identify trigger.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, electrolytes, cortisol/ACTH if this does not delay treatment, infection evaluation.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for cortisol result before treating a highly suspicious crashing patient.**

## Assessment Question
When should adrenal crisis enter the differential?

A. Shock in a chronic steroid user with vomiting and electrolyte disturbance.
B. Mild ankle sprain.
C. Isolated sore throat.
D. Simple headache with normal vitals.

**Correct Answer: A**

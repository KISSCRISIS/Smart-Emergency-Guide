# Obstructive Shock from Tamponade

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with trauma, malignancy, renal failure, or recent procedure has hypotension, JVD, dyspnea, and muffled heart sounds or ultrasound effusion.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Mechanical obstruction to cardiac filling.**

## Key ED Clues
Pericardial effusion with right-sided chamber collapse, pulsus paradoxus, tachycardia, narrow pulse pressure, shock.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Recognize obstructive physiology, avoid unnecessary delay, obtain bedside echo, support preload temporarily if needed, and mobilize definitive drainage expertise.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Bedside echocardiography is key; ECG may show low voltage/electrical alternans but should not delay care.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for CT confirmation in a crashing tamponade patient.**

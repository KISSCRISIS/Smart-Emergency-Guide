# Septic Shock

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with suspected infection has hypotension, altered mental status, high lactate, or signs of poor perfusion despite initial measures.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Progressive vasodilatory shock, organ failure, and death from delayed source control/antimicrobial therapy.**

## Key ED Clues
Fever or hypothermia, suspected source, hypotension, confusion, tachypnea, mottling, elevated lactate.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Recognize sepsis early, support airway/breathing/circulation, obtain cultures when feasible, start antimicrobial and fluid/vasopressor pathway per local protocol, identify source control need.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Lactate, cultures, CBC/CMP/coagulation, imaging guided by source, urine studies, POCUS to assess fluid tolerance and alternative shock.

## Common Trap
**Calling sepsis “infection only” and missing organ dysfunction.**

## Assessment Question
Which feature distinguishes uncomplicated infection from septic shock concern?

A. Localized sore throat only.
B. Poor perfusion or hypotension in suspected infection.
C. Mild runny nose.
D. Normal mentation and normal perfusion.

**Correct Answer: B**

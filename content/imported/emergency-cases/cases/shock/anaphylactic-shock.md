# Anaphylactic Shock

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has hypotension after exposure with rash, wheeze, GI symptoms, or airway swelling.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Distributive shock and airway collapse.**

## Key ED Clues
Rapid onset, multi-system involvement, urticaria may be absent, bronchospasm or angioedema, hypotension.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Give IM epinephrine per local protocol, position appropriately, oxygen, fluids if shocked, airway readiness, remove trigger when possible.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Clinical diagnosis; no lab should delay treatment.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for rash to appear before diagnosing anaphylaxis.**

## Assessment Question
Can anaphylaxis occur without skin findings?

A. No, rash is mandatory.
B. Yes, hypotension/airway/respiratory compromise after exposure can be enough.
C. Only in children.
D. Only after antibiotics.

**Correct Answer: B**

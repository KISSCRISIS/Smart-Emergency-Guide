# Head Injury on Anticoagulants

## Clinical Vignette
An anticoagulated patient falls and has headache, vomiting, confusion, neurologic deficit, or even deceptively mild symptoms.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Intracranial hemorrhage with delayed recognition.**

## Key ED Clues
Anticoagulant/antiplatelet exposure, age, loss of consciousness, vomiting, severe headache, abnormal neuro exam.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Assess ABCs and neuro status, obtain imaging per local head injury pathway, plan reversal if bleeding, and give clear observation/disposition plan.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
CT head, coagulation/medication assessment, repeat neuro exams.

## Common Trap
**Reassurance because the fall was “minor.”**

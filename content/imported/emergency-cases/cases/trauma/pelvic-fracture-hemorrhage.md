# Pelvic Fracture Hemorrhage

## Clinical Vignette
A high-energy trauma patient has pelvic pain/instability, hypotension, leg length discrepancy, perineal bruising, or urethral blood.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Massive retroperitoneal bleeding.**

## Key ED Clues
Unstable pelvis, shock, high-energy mechanism, blood at meatus, perineal ecchymosis.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Apply pelvic stabilization per trauma protocol, activate hemorrhage pathway, avoid repeated pelvic rocking, involve trauma/IR/orthopedics.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Pelvic x-ray, FAST may be negative despite pelvic bleeding, CT if stable, type and cross.

## Common Trap
**Repeatedly checking pelvic stability and worsening bleeding.**

## Assessment Question
Why can FAST be falsely reassuring in pelvic hemorrhage?

A. Pelvic bleeding may be retroperitoneal rather than free intraperitoneal fluid.
B. FAST always detects all bleeding.
C. Pelvic fractures never bleed.
D. Only CT brain matters.

**Correct Answer: A**

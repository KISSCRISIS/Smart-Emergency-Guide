# Primary Survey Polytrauma

## Clinical Vignette
A severely injured patient arrives after high-energy trauma with airway risk, respiratory distress, bleeding, altered mental status, or multiple injuries.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Death from missed ABC threat before diagnostic workup.**

## Key ED Clues
Airway compromise, tension pneumothorax, massive hemorrhage, shock, neurologic decline, hypothermia.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Use structured primary survey, treat life threats as found, activate trauma resources, prevent hypothermia, and reassess after every intervention.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
FAST, chest/pelvis imaging when appropriate, labs/type and cross, CT only when stable enough.

## Common Trap
**Jumping to CT before airway, breathing, and hemorrhage are controlled.**

What is the purpose of the primary survey?

A. Find every minor injury.
B. Identify and treat immediate life threats first.
C. Complete discharge paperwork.
D. Delay resuscitation until CT.

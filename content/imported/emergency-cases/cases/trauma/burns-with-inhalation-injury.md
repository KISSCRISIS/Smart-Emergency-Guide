# Burns with Inhalation Injury

## Clinical Vignette
A burn patient from enclosed fire has facial burns, soot, hoarseness, stridor, cough, or altered mental status.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Progressive airway edema and inhalational poisoning.**

## Key ED Clues
Soot in mouth/nose, singed hairs, hoarse voice, enclosed-space fire, carbonaceous sputum, CO/cyanide features.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Assess airway early, give high-concentration oxygen, prepare for early definitive airway if edema risk, treat CO/cyanide concerns per protocol.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Carboxyhemoglobin, lactate, blood gas, chest imaging when needed, bronchoscopy later if indicated.

## Common Trap
**Waiting until swelling is advanced and intubation becomes impossible.**

## Assessment Question
What burn feature should trigger early airway concern?

A. Isolated small hand burn.
B. Hoarseness or soot after enclosed-space fire.
C. Old healed scar.
D. Sunburn only.

**Correct Answer: B**

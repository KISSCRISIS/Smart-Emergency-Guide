# Spinal Cord Injury

## Clinical Vignette
A trauma patient has weakness, sensory level, priapism, hypotension with bradycardia, or midline spine pain.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Permanent neurologic injury and neurogenic shock.**

## Key ED Clues
Motor/sensory deficit, spinal tenderness, high-risk mechanism, hypotension with bradycardia, loss of rectal tone.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Maintain spinal precautions, support perfusion/oxygenation, image appropriately, involve spine/neurosurgery.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
CT spine pathway, MRI when cord/ligament concern, neuro exam documentation.

## Common Trap
**Assuming hypotension in trauma is neurogenic before excluding bleeding.**

What is a key pitfall in suspected neurogenic shock after trauma?

A. Ignoring hemorrhage as a more common cause of shock.
B. Checking neurologic exam.
C. Maintaining spinal precautions.
D. Calling for help.

# Flail Chest / Pulmonary Contusion

## Clinical Vignette
A trauma patient has chest wall instability, severe pain, hypoxemia, bruising, or worsening respiratory distress after blunt chest trauma.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Respiratory failure from pain, contusion, and impaired ventilation.**

## Key ED Clues
Paradoxical chest motion, rib fractures, hypoxemia, pulmonary contusion may worsen over time.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Support oxygenation/ventilation, treat pain safely, monitor for deterioration, search for pneumothorax/hemothorax and associated injuries.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Chest x-ray/CT when stable, lung ultrasound, blood gas if severe, continuous respiratory reassessment.

## Common Trap
**Focusing on rib fracture count while missing respiratory mechanics.**

# Blunt Abdominal Trauma

## Clinical Vignette
A patient after MVC/fall/assault has abdominal pain, seatbelt sign, hypotension, distracting injury, or altered mental status.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Occult intra-abdominal hemorrhage or hollow viscus injury.**

## Key ED Clues
Seatbelt sign, abdominal tenderness, hypotension, positive FAST, lower rib/pelvic injuries, unreliable exam.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Resuscitate, use FAST in unstable patients, obtain CT when stable, involve trauma surgery early when bleeding or peritonitis suspected.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
FAST, CT abdomen/pelvis when stable, CBC trend, lactate/base deficit, type and screen.

## Common Trap
**Normal early abdominal exam in an unreliable patient falsely reassuring.**

## Assessment Question
Which finding after blunt trauma is a red flag for abdominal injury?

A. Seatbelt sign with abdominal tenderness.
B. Small isolated finger abrasion.
C. Normal appetite only.
D. Chronic mild back pain.

**Correct Answer: A**

# Sepsis Delirium in Elderly

## Clinical Vignette
An older patient presents with confusion, weakness, fall, anorexia, or functional decline without a clear complaint.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Occult sepsis and shock missed because presentation is atypical.**

## Key ED Clues
Delirium, tachypnea, hypothermia or fever, hypotension, urinary/lung/skin source clues, medication vulnerability.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Treat delirium as a symptom; search for infection/metabolic/drug causes, assess perfusion, and start sepsis pathway if organ dysfunction is present.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Glucose, electrolytes, CBC/CMP, urinalysis/culture when indicated, chest imaging, lactate if poor perfusion.

## Common Trap
**Dismissing confusion as dementia without looking for acute illness.**

## Assessment Question
In an elderly patient, what does acute confusion often represent?

A. Always baseline dementia.
B. A possible sign of infection, metabolic problem, drug effect, or hypoperfusion.
C. Normal aging only.
D. A psychiatric diagnosis until proven otherwise.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Heat Stroke

## Clinical Vignette
A patient exposed to heat/exertion presents with hyperthermia, confusion, seizure, collapse, or multiorgan injury.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cellular heat injury, rhabdomyolysis, DIC, liver failure, and death.**

## Key ED Clues
High core temperature, CNS dysfunction, exertional or environmental exposure, hot skin may be dry or sweaty.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Begin rapid cooling immediately while supporting ABCs; do not wait for lab confirmation.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Core temperature, CK, electrolytes, renal/liver/coagulation tests, glucose, ECG.

## Common Trap
**Confusing heat exhaustion with heat stroke when CNS dysfunction is present.**

## Assessment Question
What defines heat stroke clinically?

A. Heat exposure with normal mental status only.
B. Hyperthermia plus CNS dysfunction.
C. Mild thirst after exercise.
D. Sunburn only.

**Correct Answer: B**

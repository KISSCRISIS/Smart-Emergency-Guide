# Intracranial Hemorrhage

## Clinical Vignette
A patient presents with sudden severe headache, vomiting, neurologic deficit, seizure, depressed consciousness, trauma, or anticoagulant use.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Herniation, expanding hematoma, airway loss, and death.**

## Key ED Clues
Thunderclap headache, focal deficit, high blood pressure, anticoagulants, trauma, declining GCS, unequal pupils.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Protect airway when needed, avoid hypotension/hypoxia, obtain emergent neuroimaging, reverse anticoagulation per local protocol, involve neurosurgery/ICU early.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Noncontrast CT head, coagulation profile, medication history, CTA if aneurysmal SAH/dissection concern.

## Common Trap
**Treating severe sudden headache as migraine without considering hemorrhage.**

## Assessment Question
What headache feature is a major red flag for hemorrhage?

A. Gradual mild headache for years.
B. Sudden worst headache with vomiting or neurologic deficit.
C. Headache relieved by sleep only.
D. Tension after long workday.

**Correct Answer: B**

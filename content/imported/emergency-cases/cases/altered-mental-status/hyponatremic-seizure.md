# Hyponatremic Seizure

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with vomiting, polydipsia, diuretics, endurance exercise, SIADH risk, or chronic illness seizes and has low sodium.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cerebral edema and recurrent seizures from acute severe hyponatremia.**

## Key ED Clues
Seizure, headache, vomiting, confusion, low serum sodium, acute water excess or medication trigger.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stabilize seizure and airway, correct sodium carefully through local protocol, avoid overly rapid correction in chronic cases.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Serum sodium/osmolality, urine studies later, glucose correction, medication review.

## Common Trap
**Correcting chronic hyponatremia too aggressively or ignoring symptomatic acute hyponatremia.**

## Assessment Question
What makes hyponatremia an emergency?

A. Mild chronic low sodium without symptoms.
B. Seizure or severe neurologic symptoms.
C. Normal mental status.
D. Incidental lab abnormality only.

**Correct Answer: B**

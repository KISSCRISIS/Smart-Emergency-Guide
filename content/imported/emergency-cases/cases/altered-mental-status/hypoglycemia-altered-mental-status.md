# Hypoglycemia Altered Mental Status

## Clinical Vignette
A confused, diaphoretic, seizing, or comatose patient may have low bedside glucose regardless of history.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Brain injury or death from unrecognized reversible neuroglycopenia.**

## Key ED Clues
Diaphoresis, tremor, seizure, focal deficits mimicking stroke, diabetes meds or poor intake, but any patient can be affected.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Check bedside glucose early in every AMS; correct per local protocol and reassess neurologic status.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Point-of-care glucose, electrolytes, medication review, infection/renal/liver evaluation if recurrent or unexplained.

## Common Trap
**Skipping glucose because the patient has no known diabetes.**

# Meningitis / Encephalitis

## Clinical Vignette
A febrile patient has headache, neck stiffness, altered mental status, seizure, rash, or immunocompromise.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Sepsis, cerebral edema, seizures, and neurologic injury from delayed antimicrobial therapy.**

## Key ED Clues
Fever with AMS, meningismus, petechial rash, seizure, focal deficit, immunosuppression.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stabilize, start local empiric antimicrobial/antiviral pathway when suspected, obtain cultures if feasible without delay, image before LP when indicated.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Blood cultures, CT head if red flags before LP, CSF studies when safe, glucose/electrolytes, lactate if septic.

## Common Trap
**Delaying treatment until lumbar puncture is completed when LP is unsafe or delayed.**

What is the main danger in suspected meningitis with delayed LP?

A. Early empiric treatment may be delayed unnecessarily.
B. The rash always rules it out.
C. Normal neck movement excludes it.
D. Head CT cures it.

**Correct Answer: A**

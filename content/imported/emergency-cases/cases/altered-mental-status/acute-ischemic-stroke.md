# Acute Ischemic Stroke

## Clinical Vignette
A patient develops sudden facial droop, arm weakness, speech difficulty, visual loss, neglect, ataxia, or severe neurologic deficit.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Irreversible brain injury if reperfusion-eligible stroke is not identified quickly.**

## Key ED Clues
Sudden focal deficit, last-known-well matters, glucose mimic excluded, large vessel signs, anticoagulant history.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Activate local stroke pathway, check glucose, assess airway/protection, obtain urgent brain/vascular imaging per protocol, determine eligibility for reperfusion interventions.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Noncontrast CT/CTA pathway per local stroke system, glucose, ECG, labs that do not delay imaging when appropriate.

## Common Trap
**Delaying stroke pathway because symptoms improved partially or because patient is young.**

# Esophageal Rupture After Vomiting

## Clinical Vignette
Severe chest pain follows forceful vomiting or instrumentation, with toxicity, subcutaneous emphysema, pleural effusion, or sepsis.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Mediastinitis and septic shock from esophageal perforation.**

## Key ED Clues
Pain after vomiting, fever/toxicity, crepitus, pleural findings, pneumomediastinum.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Keep patient NPO, resuscitate, give broad supportive sepsis care per protocol, obtain urgent imaging and surgical consultation.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Chest x-ray, CT chest with appropriate contrast pathway, labs for sepsis and operative planning.

## Common Trap
**Calling it gastritis or musculoskeletal pain because the ECG is not ischemic.**

What historical clue should trigger concern for esophageal rupture?

A. Chest pain after forceful vomiting with systemic toxicity.
B. Chest pain that lasts seconds with anxiety.
C. Pain only with shoulder movement.
D. Burning epigastric pain relieved by antacid.

**Correct Answer: A**

# Pericarditis vs Cardiac Tamponade

## Clinical Vignette
A patient reports sharp pleuritic chest pain improved by leaning forward, or presents with hypotension, muffled heart sounds, JVD, and dyspnea.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Tamponade physiology causing obstructive shock.**

## Key ED Clues
Diffuse ST elevation/PR depression suggests pericarditis; hypotension, JVD, pulsus paradoxus, and large effusion suggest tamponade.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Separate inflammatory pericarditis from shock physiology, place on monitor, use bedside echo early, and involve procedural/specialty support when tamponade is suspected.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, troponin, inflammatory context, bedside cardiac ultrasound, chest x-ray as supportive.

## Common Trap
**Treating tamponade as simple pericarditis and missing obstructive shock.**

## Assessment Question
Which change turns pericarditis into a resuscitation problem?

A. Pain improves with sitting forward.
B. Diffuse ST elevation without instability.
C. Hypotension with large pericardial effusion and JVD.
D. Low-grade fever only.

**Correct Answer: C**

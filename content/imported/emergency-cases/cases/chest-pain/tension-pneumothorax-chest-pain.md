# Tension Pneumothorax Chest Pain

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with sudden pleuritic chest pain, severe dyspnea, unilateral absent breath sounds, hypotension, or trauma/ventilation context deteriorates rapidly.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Obstructive shock from intrathoracic pressure collapsing venous return.**

## Key ED Clues
Unilateral breath sound reduction, respiratory distress, hypotension, distended neck veins, tracheal shift late, mechanical ventilation risk.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
If unstable and classic, do not wait for imaging; perform local emergency decompression procedure and reassess.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Clinical diagnosis in crashing patient; lung ultrasound/chest x-ray only if stable enough.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for chest x-ray in a crashing classic tension pneumothorax.**

A. Wait for CT confirmation.
B. Treat immediately according to local emergency decompression protocol.

# Aortic Dissection Mimicking ACS

## Clinical Vignette
A patient with abrupt tearing chest/back pain, pulse asymmetry, neurologic symptoms, or severe hypertension appears similar to ACS.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Aortic rupture, tamponade, stroke, or catastrophic bleeding if anticoagulation is given blindly.**

## Key ED Clues
Maximal pain at onset, migrating pain, pulse/blood pressure differential, neurologic deficit, new aortic regurgitation, widened mediastinum may be present but is not required.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stabilize, control physiology per local protocol, avoid reflex anticoagulation until dissection is considered, obtain urgent definitive imaging when stable enough, involve surgery/vascular early.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG to assess mimics, CTA aorta when appropriate, bedside echo for tamponade/aortic root clues, chest x-ray as supportive not exclusionary.

## Common Trap
**Assuming every chest pain with ECG changes is ACS and giving anticoagulation before considering dissection.**

## Assessment Question
Which clue most strongly pushes chest pain toward aortic dissection rather than uncomplicated ACS?

A. Gradual burning pain after meals.
B. Abrupt maximal pain with neurologic deficit or pulse asymmetry.
C. Pain that improves with rest only.
D. Isolated low-grade fever.

**Correct Answer: B**

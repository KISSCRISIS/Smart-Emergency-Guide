# Unstable Angina / NSTEMI

## Clinical Vignette
A patient has ischemic chest pain without ST elevation but with dynamic ECG changes, elevated troponin, or high-risk features.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Progression to occlusion MI, arrhythmia, shock, or missed ACS.**

## Key ED Clues
Pressure-like pain, exertional/rest symptoms, diaphoresis, dynamic ST depression/T inversion, troponin rise, high-risk history.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Risk stratify, monitor, treat ACS per local protocol, repeat ECG/troponin, consult cardiology for high-risk features.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Serial ECGs and troponins, basic labs, chest x-ray when useful, echo if instability.

## Common Trap
**Discharging after one normal ECG/troponin when symptoms are high risk.**

A. Dynamic ECG changes or troponin rise.
B. Pain lasting one second only.
C. Normal shoe size.
D. Chronic stable rash.

**Correct Answer: A**

# Pulmonary Embolism Chest Pain

## Clinical Vignette
A patient presents with pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, syncope, hemoptysis, or risk factors such as malignancy, pregnancy, surgery, or immobility.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Obstructive shock from massive PE or sudden decompensation from right ventricular failure.**

## Key ED Clues
Pleuritic pain, unexplained hypoxemia, tachycardia, syncope, RV strain signs, DVT symptoms, risk factor context.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Risk stratify, support oxygenation and circulation, use local diagnostic pathway, escalate early when shock or RV failure is suspected.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
ECG, troponin/BNP for risk context, D-dimer only when appropriate, CTA pulmonary angiography or V/Q pathway, POCUS for RV strain/DVT when unstable.

## Common Trap
**Ruling out PE because the chest x-ray is normal or because chest pain is not central crushing pain.**

## Assessment Question
In suspected PE, which finding is most concerning for immediate deterioration?

A. Stable pleuritic pain with normal oxygenation.
B. Shock or syncope with evidence of RV strain.
C. Pain reproducible with palpation only.
D. Normal chest x-ray.

**Correct Answer: B**

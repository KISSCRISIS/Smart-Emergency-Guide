# STEMI / OMI Chest Pain

## Clinical Vignette
A middle-aged patient presents with crushing retrosternal chest pain, diaphoresis, nausea, and dynamic ST-segment changes on ECG.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**An occluded coronary artery causing ongoing myocardial necrosis and malignant dysrhythmia.**

## Key ED Clues
Contiguous ST elevation or OMI-equivalent pattern, reciprocal change, persistent ischemic pain, diaphoresis, shock or arrhythmia.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Move to monitored resuscitation area, obtain immediate ECG review, treat as time-sensitive ACS, activate local reperfusion pathway, manage instability, and reassess ECGs serially.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
12-lead ECG with repeat ECGs, troponin trend, bedside echo if shock, chest x-ray when it does not delay escalation, basic labs for procedural readiness.

## Common Trap
**Waiting for troponin before acting on a clear occlusion pattern.**

## Assessment Question
A patient has classic ischemic chest pain and ECG findings concerning for acute coronary occlusion. What is the most important ED mindset?

A. Wait for troponin confirmation before escalation.
B. Treat the ECG and patient as a time-sensitive occlusion syndrome while following local reperfusion protocol.
C. Discharge if pain improves after analgesia.
D. Repeat ECG only the next day.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Crashing Airway

## Clinical Vignette
A patient cannot maintain oxygenation, ventilation, airway protection, or has rapidly worsening anatomy/physiology.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypoxic arrest during delayed or poorly planned airway intervention.**

## Key ED Clues
Failed oxygenation, altered mental status, facial/neck trauma, severe shock, acidosis, airway edema, vomiting/bleeding.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Call for help, preoxygenate as possible, prepare primary and backup plans, optimize physiology before and during intubation, and have failed-airway plan ready.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Continuous monitoring, capnography confirmation, blood gas only if it does not delay rescue, post-intubation assessment.

## Common Trap
**Thinking intubation is just a tube problem; it is also a physiology problem.**

## Assessment Question
What makes an airway “crashing”?

A. Stable elective procedure.
B. Immediate threat to oxygenation, ventilation, protection, or rapidly worsening anatomy/physiology.
C. Mild sore throat.
D. Normal breathing.

**Correct Answer: B**

# Procedural Sedation Complication

## Clinical Vignette
During ED sedation, the patient develops hypoventilation, airway obstruction, hypotension, or laryngospasm.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Hypoxia, aspiration, hemodynamic collapse.**

## Key ED Clues
Snoring/obstruction, falling ETCO2 waveform quality, apnea, desaturation, hypotension, difficult airway risk.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Stop procedure if needed, airway repositioning, suction, ventilation support, reversal only when appropriate, prepare escalation.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Continuous pulse oximetry/capnography when available, vitals, airway assessment before sedation.

## Common Trap
**Assuming sedation is safe because the procedure is minor.**

# Post-Intubation Hypotension

## Clinical Vignette
A critically ill patient becomes hypotensive immediately after intubation or sedation.

## First 60-Second Impression
Ask immediately:
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is airway, breathing, circulation, disability, or exposure currently threatened?
- Does this case require a resuscitation bay, monitor, senior help, or specialty activation?

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
**Cardiac arrest from reduced venous return, medication effect, missed tension pneumothorax, or severe acidosis.**

## Key ED Clues
Shock before intubation, dehydration, sepsis, RV failure, high intrathoracic pressure, sedative sensitivity.

## First ED Actions — Conceptual, Not a Fixed Protocol
Anticipate before tube: resuscitate physiology, choose medications carefully per protocol, reassess after tube for breath sounds, pressure, ventilator settings, pneumothorax.

> SEG safety note: this page is for education and board-style clinical reasoning. It is not a bedside protocol. Use local hospital policy, senior supervision, and current guideline pathways for final treatment decisions.

## Essential Tests / Bedside Tools
Capnography, blood pressure cycling/arterial monitoring when available, lung ultrasound/chest x-ray if pneumothorax concern, blood gas if ventilation issue.

## Common Trap
**Celebrating tube placement and missing the blood pressure collapse.**

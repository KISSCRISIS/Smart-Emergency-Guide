## 1. Start With the Patient

ECG is never read alone.

Ask:
- Why was the ECG done?
- What symptom triggered it?
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is this chest pain, dyspnea, syncope, palpitations, altered mental status, overdose, electrolyte concern, or pre-op/incidental?
- Is there an old ECG?

## 2. First-Pass ECG System

Use the same order every time:

1. Rate
2. Rhythm
3. Axis
4. Intervals
5. QRS width
6. ST segment
7. T waves
8. QT concern
9. Compare with old ECG
10. Match ECG with clinical picture

---

## 3. Senior-Now Patterns

Call senior urgently for ECG concern with:

- STEMI or STEMI equivalent pattern
- wide complex tachycardia
- complete heart block
- unstable bradycardia
- hyperkalemia pattern concern
- sodium channel blocker pattern concern
- long QT with syncope/seizure concern
- Brugada pattern with syncope concern
- WPW with tachyarrhythmia concern
- ECG changes with shock, chest pain, syncope, severe dyspnea, or overdose

## 7. Syncope Red-Flag ECGs

Do not miss:
- Brugada pattern
- WPW/pre-excitation
- long QT
- HCM clues
- complete heart block
- bifascicular block with concerning story
- ischemia
- ventricular arrhythmia patterns

## 8. JMC / Exam Traps

- Reading ECG without clinical context.
- Calling ST elevation â€œSTEMIâ€ without considering mimics, but also not missing STEMI equivalents.
- Ignoring posterior MI when anterior leads show ST depression pattern.
- Ignoring ECG in overdose.
- Calling wide complex tachycardia SVT too quickly.
- Reassuring syncope patient with a dangerous ECG pattern.
- Missing hyperkalemia ECG concern in renal failure.
- Missing old ECG comparison.

## 9. Quick ECG Checklist

- Patient stable?
- Why was ECG done?
- Rate?
- Rhythm?
- QRS narrow or wide?
- ST elevation/depression?
- T-wave danger?
- QT concern?
- Old ECG?
- Symptoms match ECG?
- Need senior now?

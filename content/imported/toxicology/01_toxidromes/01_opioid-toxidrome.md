# Opioid Toxidrome

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Miosis, CNS depression, and respiratory depression are the classic triad. Death is usually from hypoventilation, airway loss, aspiration, or recurrence after short-acting reversal.

## ECG / Clinical Clues

Small pupils, slow breathing, somnolence/coma, bradycardia or hypotension in severe cases, response to naloxone but recurrence with long-acting agents.

## Why It Matters in the ED

Ventilation first, then targeted naloxone when clinically appropriate; monitor for recurrence and pulmonary complications.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway loss, respiratory failure, seizures, hyperthermia, shock, or dysrhythmia depending on the toxidrome.
- Delayed recurrence when the exposure is long-acting or mixed.

## Common Trap

Do not confuse “woke up after naloxone” with “safe to leave.”

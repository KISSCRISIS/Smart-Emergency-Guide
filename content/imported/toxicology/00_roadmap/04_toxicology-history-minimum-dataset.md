# Toxicology History: The Minimum Useful Dataset

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Core Concept

A toxicology history must be fast, practical, and repeated. Key elements are substance, dose/amount, time, route, co-ingestions, intent, formulation, access to medications, scene clues, and patient risk factors.

## High-Yield Recognition Clues
- Empty bottles, blister packs, patches, powders, plants, chemicals
- Delayed-release or long-acting formulations
- Occupational or laboratory exposure
- Pediatric exploratory ingestion
- Intentional self-harm or body packing

## ED Priorities
- Ask EMS/family to bring containers when safe.
- Document time as estimated and uncertain when needed.
- Clarify route: ingestion, inhalation, injection, dermal, ocular.
- Ask about chronic medications and renal/hepatic disease.
- Repeat history when mental status improves.

## Common Trap

A negative patient history does not rule out poisoning, especially in intentional overdose, body packing, assault, or impaired mental status.

A) Exact brand of patient shoes
B) Whether the product is extended-release or long-acting
C) Patient favorite food
D) Remote childhood illness only

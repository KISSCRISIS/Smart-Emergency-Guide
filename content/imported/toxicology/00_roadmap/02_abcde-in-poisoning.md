# ABCDE in the Poisoned Patient

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Core Concept

ABCDE is the universal resuscitation framework adapted to toxin-specific threats: airway loss from CNS depression, ventilation failure from opioids or cholinergics, shock from cardiotoxins, seizures from proconvulsants, and exposure/decontamination for dermal or inhalational toxins.

## High-Yield Recognition Clues
- Depressed mental status
- Respiratory depression or bronchorrhea
- Hypotension, bradycardia, wide QRS, or dysrhythmia
- Seizure, clonus, rigidity, or coma
- Odor, contaminated clothing, chemical burns

## ED Priorities
- Airway risk is about trajectory, not only current oxygen saturation.
- Ventilation matters: opioids and sedative-hypnotics kill through hypoventilation.
- Circulation: treat shock and rhythm while thinking sodium-channel blocker, calcium-channel blocker, beta-blocker, digoxin-like toxin, or potassium disturbance.
- Disability: check glucose, treat seizures, control temperature.
- Exposure: remove contaminated clothing and irrigate when indicated.

## Common Trap

Normal oxygen saturation can coexist with dangerous hypercapnia in opioid or sedative poisoning.

## Assessment Question

**Which poisoned patient needs immediate airway/ventilation attention even if the pulse oximeter is normal?**

A) Awake patient with mild nausea after vitamins
B) Somnolent patient with slow breathing after suspected opioid exposure
C) Stable patient with isolated rash
D) Patient with normal exam after a witnessed tiny taste exposure

**Correct Answer:** B

**Arabic reasoning:** الأوكسيميتر قد يبقى طبيعياً فترة، لكن التنفس البطيء يعني تراكم CO2 وخطر توقف التنفس.

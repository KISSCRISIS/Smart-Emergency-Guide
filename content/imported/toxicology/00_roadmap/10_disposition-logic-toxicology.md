# Disposition Logic in Toxicology

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Core Concept

Disposition depends on toxin, formulation, intent, symptoms, trajectory, ECG/labs, recurrence risk, comorbidities, and availability of reliable observation. “Looks better now” is not enough for long-acting or delayed toxins.

## High-Yield Recognition Clues
- Long-acting opioid or methadone
- Extended-release products
- Lithium, salicylates, acetaminophen, iron, toxic alcohols
- Persistent ECG abnormality
- Intentional self-harm
- Child with potentially lethal small exposure

## ED Priorities
- Assess recurrence risk after antidote wears off.
- Repeat ECG/labs when toxin evolves.
- Consider ICU for airway, shock, seizures, severe acidosis, dysrhythmia, or antidote infusion needs.
- Psychiatric and social safety assessment after medical stabilization when intentional.

## Common Trap

Discharging after transient naloxone response can be dangerous in long-acting opioid exposure.

## Assessment Question

**A child improves after naloxone but likely ingested a long-acting opioid. What is the safest disposition concept?**

A) Immediate discharge because response confirms cure
B) Observation/admission planning because recurrent respiratory depression is possible
C) No monitoring if pupils normalize
D) Activated charcoal despite somnolence

**Correct Answer:** B

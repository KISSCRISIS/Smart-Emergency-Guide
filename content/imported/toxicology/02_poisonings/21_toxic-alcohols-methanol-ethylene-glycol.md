# Toxic Alcohols: Methanol and Ethylene Glycol

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Methanol and ethylene glycol cause dangerous metabolites: methanol injures optic/CNS pathways; ethylene glycol causes glycolate/oxalate-related acidosis and renal injury.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- High anion gap metabolic acidosis
- Osmolar gap early
- Visual symptoms for methanol
- Renal injury/calcium oxalate crystals for ethylene glycol
- Ingestion may initially look like ethanol intoxication

## Why It Matters in the ED

Fomepizole and dialysis concepts are time-sensitive in significant cases; consult poison center/nephrology early.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Assuming the patient is just drunk when acidosis is evolving.

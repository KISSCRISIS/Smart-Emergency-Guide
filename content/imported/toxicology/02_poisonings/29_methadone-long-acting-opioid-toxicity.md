# Methadone / Long-Acting Opioid Toxicity

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Methadone is a long-acting opioid that can cause recurrent respiratory depression after naloxone response and may prolong QT.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Somnolence, miosis, respiratory depression
- Improves with naloxone then relapses
- Pediatric exposure serious
- QTc prolongation risk

## Why It Matters in the ED

Ventilation, naloxone strategy, and prolonged monitoring/admission concepts are central; watch for recurrence and QT issues.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Discharging after a single naloxone response.

## Assessment Question

**A toddler responds to naloxone but becomes somnolent again after suspected methadone exposure. Best concept?**

A) Recurrent toxicity requires ongoing monitoring/possible naloxone infusion pathway
B) Discharge because naloxone worked once
C) Activated charcoal despite somnolence
D) No ECG consideration ever

**Correct Answer:** A

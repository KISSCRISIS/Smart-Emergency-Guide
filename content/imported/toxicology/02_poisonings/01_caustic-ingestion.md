# Caustic Ingestion

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Caustic exposures injure tissue by chemical burns. Alkalis tend to penetrate deeply; acids may cause coagulative injury. Airway symptoms can occur even without visible oral burns.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Stridor, drooling, dysphonia, dysphagia
- Chest/abdominal pain or vomiting
- No oral burns does not rule out esophageal injury
- Intentional ingestion = higher risk

## Why It Matters in the ED

Airway assessment is first. Endoscopic evaluation is often central after stabilization. Avoid neutralization and avoid charcoal because it does not solve the burn and may complicate evaluation.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Reassured by a normal mouth exam.

## Assessment Question

**A patient ingests an alkali and has stridor but no oral burns. After airway stabilization, what is the best next concept?**

A) Neutralize with acid
B) Activated charcoal for all caustics
C) Arrange GI evaluation/endoscopy pathway
D) Discharge because mouth is normal

**Correct Answer:** C

**Arabic reasoning:** الخطر الحقيقي داخل المريء والحنجرة، وليس شكل الفم فقط. الفحم والتعادل ليسا الحل.

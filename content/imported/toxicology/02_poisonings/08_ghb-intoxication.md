# GHB Intoxication

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

GHB causes abrupt CNS depression, often with rapid spontaneous recovery because of short duration; it may be used recreationally or in facilitated assault.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Sudden coma or deep sedation
- Rapid improvement can occur
- Bradycardia, hypoventilation may occur
- History often unreliable or absent

## Why It Matters in the ED

Support airway and ventilation. Do not be falsely reassured by rapid improvement; assess assault risk, co-ingestions, and safety.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Assuming rapid recovery means harmless exposure.

## Assessment Question

**Which feature is classic for GHB intoxication?**

A) Long persistent coma for days in all cases
B) Abrupt CNS depression with possible rapid recovery
C) Dry hot delirium
D) Severe salivation and diarrhea

**Correct Answer:** B

**Arabic reasoning:** GHB قصير المفعول نسبياً وقد يسبب تحسناً سريعاً بعد غيبوبة واضحة.

# Cardiac Glycoside / Digoxin Toxicity

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Cardiac glycosides inhibit Na/K-ATPase, producing GI symptoms, visual changes, hyperkalemia in acute toxicity, bradyarrhythmias, AV block, and ventricular ectopy.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Halos or visual disturbance
- Nausea/vomiting
- Bradycardia, AV block, ventricular ectopy
- Hyperkalemia in acute severe poisoning
- Toad/bufotoxin exposure can mimic digoxin

## Why It Matters in the ED

Digoxin-specific antibody fragments are the key antidote concept in severe toxicity. Hyperkalemia in acute cardiac glycoside poisoning is a marker of severity.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Treating ectopy alone while missing the systemic cardiac glycoside syndrome.

## Assessment Question

**A teen ingests “heart pills,” sees halos, has ventricular ectopy and hyperkalemia. What is the key antidote concept?**

A) Digoxin-specific antibody fragments
B) Naloxone infusion
C) Vitamin K
D) Fomepizole

**Correct Answer:** A

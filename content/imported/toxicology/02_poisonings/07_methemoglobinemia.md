# Methemoglobinemia

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Methemoglobinemia occurs when hemoglobin iron is oxidized and cannot carry oxygen normally, causing cyanosis with impaired oxygen delivery.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Cyanosis not improving as expected with oxygen
- Chocolate-brown blood
- Exposure to benzocaine, nitrates, dapsone, aniline dyes
- Pulse oximetry may be misleading

## Why It Matters in the ED

Methylene blue is the antidote concept for many acquired cases; verify contraindications and consult toxicology. Severe cases require aggressive support.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Trusting pulse oximetry alone or missing exposure history after topical anesthetics.

## Assessment Question

**A patient becomes cyanotic after benzocaine spray and blood appears chocolate-brown. What antidote concept fits?**

A) Methylene blue
B) Deferoxamine
C) Naloxone
D) Vitamin K

**Correct Answer:** A

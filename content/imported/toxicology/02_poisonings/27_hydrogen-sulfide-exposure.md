# Hydrogen Sulfide Exposure

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Hydrogen sulfide is a rapid knockdown gas with rotten-egg odor at low levels but olfactory fatigue at high levels; it can cause collapse, respiratory failure, and shock.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Rotten egg smell at scene
- Collapse after opening confined space/vehicle/sewer
- Mucous membrane irritation/gas eye
- Shock, coma, respiratory distress
- Secondary responder risk

## Why It Matters in the ED

Rescuer safety, removal from source, decontamination, high-flow oxygen, and expert-guided antidote considerations for severe cases.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Rushing into a contaminated scene and becoming the next patient.

## Assessment Question

**A responder collapses after opening a car door with rotten-egg odor. What is the first systems lesson?**

A) Scene safety and removal from exposure are critical
B) Give oral charcoal immediately
C) Ignore because odor means low risk
D) Discharge if awake

**Correct Answer:** A

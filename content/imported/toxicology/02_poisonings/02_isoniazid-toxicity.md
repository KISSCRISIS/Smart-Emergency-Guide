# Isoniazid Toxicity

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Isoniazid overdose causes refractory seizures by functional pyridoxine depletion and impaired GABA synthesis.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Status epilepticus after TB medication access
- Metabolic acidosis may occur
- Seizures may resist usual therapy until pyridoxine is given

## Why It Matters in the ED

Recognize the antidote early. Benzodiazepines support seizure control but pyridoxine addresses the mechanism.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Treating only with benzodiazepines and missing pyridoxine.

## Assessment Question

**Which antidote addresses the mechanism of isoniazid-induced seizures?**

A) Hydroxocobalamin
B) Pyridoxine
C) Naloxone
D) Vitamin K

**Correct Answer:** B

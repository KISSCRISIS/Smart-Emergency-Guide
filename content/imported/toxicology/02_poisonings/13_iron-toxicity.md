# Iron Toxicity

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Definition

Iron overdose is a serious pediatric and intentional ingestion risk. It causes GI injury, metabolic acidosis, shock, and systemic toxicity; tablets may be radiopaque.

## ECG / Clinical Clues
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea
- Radiopaque tablets
- Metabolic acidosis/lactate
- Shock in severe cases

## Why It Matters in the ED

Activated charcoal does not bind iron well. Whole-bowel irrigation and deferoxamine are concepts for selected significant cases with expert guidance.

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?

- Airway or ventilation failure.
- Seizure, hyperthermia, shock, malignant dysrhythmia, severe acidosis, or delayed organ injury depending on toxin.
- Recurrence after short-acting reversal or delayed absorption in long-acting/extended-release exposures.

## Common Board Trap

Giving charcoal and assuming it solves iron ingestion.

## Assessment Question

**A patient ingests many iron tablets visible on abdominal radiograph. Which statement is true?**

A) Activated charcoal reliably adsorbs iron
B) Whole-bowel irrigation may be considered in significant tablet burden
C) Naloxone is antidote
D) No systemic toxicity is possible

**Correct Answer:** B

**Arabic reasoning:** الحديد لا يمتصه الفحم جيداً وقد تظهر الحبوب بالأشعة، لذلك WBI مفهوم مهم في حالات مختارة.

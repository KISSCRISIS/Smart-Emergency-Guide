# Toxicology Mini Cases

> **SEG Safety Layer**
> This page is for emergency medicine learning and board-style pattern recognition. It is not a local treatment protocol. For real patients, stabilize ABCs, call your local poison center/toxicologist early, and follow local guidelines. Doses, infusion rates, dialysis thresholds, and disposition times must be verified locally before use.


## Mini Cases

### Case 1: The silent liver injury

**Stem:** A 22-year-old arrives after intentional acetaminophen ingestion. He feels well and initial exam is normal.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: normal exam does not exclude delayed hepatic injury. What can kill now/later: missed NAC decision and liver failure. Next thinking: timing, level, co-ingestions, NAC criteria, mental health safety.

### Case 2: The wet pesticide patient

**Stem:** A farm worker arrives coughing, wheezing, salivating, miotic, and weak after pesticide exposure.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: secretions + bronchospasm + weakness. What can kill now: respiratory failure from bronchorrhea/bronchospasm and nicotinic weakness. Next thinking: PPE/decontamination, airway, atropine/pralidoxime concepts, seizure control.

### Case 3: The ECG screams before the lab

**Stem:** A patient overdosed on unknown tablets and has seizure, hypotension, wide QRS, and terminal R in aVR.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: sodium-channel blockade pattern. What can kill now: ventricular dysrhythmia and shock. Next thinking: bicarbonate concept and aggressive resuscitation.

### Case 4: The returning opioid child

**Stem:** A toddler wakes after naloxone but becomes sleepy again one hour later.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: recurrence after short reversal. What can kill now: recurrent respiratory depression. Next thinking: long-acting opioid such as methadone, monitoring/admission, possible infusion pathway.

### Case 5: The painful feet and falling hair

**Stem:** A woman returns after GI illness with burning feet and alopecia.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: delayed metal poisoning pattern. What can kill later: neuropathy, respiratory failure, cardiac arrest in severe thallium. Next thinking: toxicology consultation and Prussian blue concept.

### Case 6: The raver with seizure

**Stem:** A young patient at a dance event is hyperthermic, agitated, diaphoretic, and severely hyponatremic.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: MDMA pattern with hyponatremia. What can kill now: hyperthermia, cerebral edema/seizure, rhabdomyolysis. Next thinking: sodium, cooling, sedation, complications.

### Case 7: The normal pulse ox trap

**Stem:** Several family members have headache and dizziness after using a generator indoors. Pulse ox looks normal.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: exposure cluster. What can kill now/later: CO hypoxia, cardiac ischemia, neurologic injury. Next thinking: high-concentration oxygen and carboxyhemoglobin.

### Case 8: The body packer with abdominal pain

**Stem:** Traveler has severe hypertension, tachycardia, chest pain, abdominal pain, and many packets on abdominal imaging.

**Teaching frame:** First thing you notice: symptomatic packet rupture/ischemia concern. What can kill now: massive sympathomimetic toxicity, bowel ischemia. Next thinking: urgent surgical/toxicology pathway.

# Tension Pneumothorax

## Definition
Air under pressure compromises ventilation and venous return.

## Why It Matters in the ED
This topic matters because it can rapidly progress to cardiac arrest, respiratory arrest, irreversible organ injury, or unsafe transfer if the first clinician misses the physiology. In SEG, the goal is to teach the learner to ask: **What can kill this patient now?**

## First 60-Second Impression
Look for airway risk, work of breathing, mental status, perfusion, skin temperature, pulse quality, blood pressure trend, oxygenation, and any monitor pattern that suggests impending collapse.

## Key ED Clues
- unilateral breath sound reduction
- shock
- respiratory distress
- tracheal shift late
- high airway pressure if ventilated

## What Can Kill the Patient Now?
- Loss of airway or ventilation
- Refractory shock or poor tissue perfusion
- Malignant rhythm or conduction failure
- Worsening hypoxemia or hypercapnia
- Delayed recognition of a reversible cause

## Bedside Tools
- Continuous vital sign trend, not a single measurement
- ECG and rhythm strip when circulation is involved
- Point-of-care glucose for altered mental status
- VBG/ABG when ventilation or acid-base status matters
- Lactate trend when shock or sepsis is suspected
- Focused POCUS when shock, dyspnea, or tamponade/pneumothorax/volume questions exist

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Call for help early if the patient is peri-arrest or deteriorating.
2. Support oxygenation and ventilation according to local practice.
3. Establish access and monitoring.
4. Treat immediately reversible life threats.
5. Reassess after each intervention and prepare destination planning.

## Common Trap
Waiting for imaging in a crashing patient with classic physiology.

## Assessment Question
**A patient with suspected tension pneumothorax is deteriorating in the ED. What is the safest first principle?**

A. Focus only on the final diagnosis before resuscitation.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, support ABCs, and reassess after interventions.
C. Delay bedside reassessment until all imaging returns.
D. Treat the most abnormal laboratory value without linking it to perfusion.

**Correct Answer:** B

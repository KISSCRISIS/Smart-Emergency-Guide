# Suction and the Contaminated Airway


## Definition
The contaminated airway contains blood, vomit, secretions, or debris that blocks visualization and threatens aspiration.

## Why It Matters in the ED
A dirty airway destroys first-pass success and can rapidly worsen hypoxemia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- vomiting/hematemesis
- facial trauma
- massive hemoptysis
- pulmonary edema with frothy secretions
- seizure with secretions

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Put suction in the hand before the laryngoscope.
2. Position to reduce aspiration when possible.
3. Have backup suction if high-volume contamination exists.
4. Consider early expert help for massive bleeding or distorted anatomy.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to suction and the contaminated airway?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

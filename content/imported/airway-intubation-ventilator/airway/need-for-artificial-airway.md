# Need for an Artificial Airway


## Definition
The decision to place an artificial airway is based on oxygenation failure, ventilation failure, airway protection failure, or anticipated clinical deterioration.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Waiting for complete collapse increases aspiration, hypoxic injury, and peri-intubation arrest.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- failure to oxygenate despite escalating support
- rising CO₂ with fatigue or altered mental status
- inability to clear secretions
- facial/neck pathology likely to worsen

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Classify the airway problem.
2. Prepare for intubation if support is failing or course is predictable.
3. Do not use gag reflex alone as the decision point.

## Common Trap
Overvaluing gag reflex; Rosen notes absence of gag reflex can occur in normal adults and does not map reliably to airway protection.

## Assessment Question
**Which finding is least reliable as a stand-alone reason to intubate?**

A. Absent gag reflex
B. Inability to phonate with obstruction
C. Pooling secretions with deterioration
D. Severe work of breathing with exhaustion

**Correct Answer:** A

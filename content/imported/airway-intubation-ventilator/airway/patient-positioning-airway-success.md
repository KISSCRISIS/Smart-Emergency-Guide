# Patient Positioning for Airway Success


## Definition
Positioning aligns anatomy and improves preoxygenation, laryngoscopy, BVM ventilation, and rescue options.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Bad positioning turns an otherwise manageable airway into a difficult airway.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- obesity requiring ramping
- infant with large occiput requiring shoulder roll
- trauma requiring cervical spine precautions
- hypoxemic patient who benefits from head-up position

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen/Tintinalli-supported concept: obese patients often do better in ramped positioning, aligning the external auditory meatus with the sternal notch.
- Pediatric infants may need shoulder support to overcome occiput-driven neck flexion.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Position before drugs if time allows.
2. Ramped or head-up for obese/hypoxemic adults when safe.
3. Use trauma precautions when cervical injury is possible.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to patient positioning for airway success?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

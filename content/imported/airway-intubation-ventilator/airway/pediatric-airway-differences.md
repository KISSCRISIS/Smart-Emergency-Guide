# Pediatric Airway Differences


## Definition
Children have faster desaturation, different anatomy, higher oxygen consumption, and different rescue considerations compared with adults.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Pediatric airway failure often becomes bradycardia and arrest from hypoxia.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- large occiput in infants
- high/anterior airway
- floppy epiglottis
- smaller reserve and rapid desaturation
- hypoxia-triggered bradycardia

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen-based concept: children are more likely to develop bradycardia in response to hypoxia and desaturate faster after paralysis.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Preoxygenate and prepare rescue ventilation meticulously.
2. Limit attempts and avoid hypoxic repetition.
3. Use age/size-appropriate equipment and local pediatric airway algorithm.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to pediatric airway differences?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

# Oral and Nasal Airway Adjuncts


## Definition
Adjuncts help keep the upper airway open during spontaneous breathing or BVM ventilation.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Adjuncts can convert poor mask ventilation into effective ventilation.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- tongue obstruction
- snoring respirations
- poor BVM chest rise despite good mask seal
- tolerance depends on mental status and reflexes

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Tintinalli-supported sizing reminder: oral airway sizing is commonly from the corner of the mouth to the angle of the mandible.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Choose adjunct based on consciousness, trauma, and nasal/oral contraindications.
2. Reassess chest rise and leak after insertion.
3. Remove if vomiting or intolerance occurs.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to oral and nasal airway adjuncts?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

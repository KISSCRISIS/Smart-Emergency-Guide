# Hyperoxia After Intubation


## Definition
Excess oxygen exposure after stabilization when FiO₂ remains unnecessarily high.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Oxygen is life-saving, but prolonged unnecessary hyperoxia may be harmful in several critical illness states.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- FiO₂ left at 1.0 after stabilization
- SpO₂ persistently 100% with stable patient
- post-arrest/ventilated patient
- no plan to titrate

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Use high FiO₂ during rescue, then titrate down when safe.
2. Target oxygenation per local disease-specific protocol.
3. Do not delay rescue oxygen because of hyperoxia concerns.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to hyperoxia after intubation?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

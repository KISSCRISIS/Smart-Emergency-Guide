# High-Pressure Alarm Approach


## Definition
High pressure means the ventilator meets resistance or poor compliance while trying to deliver a breath.

## Why It Matters in the ED
It may indicate mucus plug, bronchospasm, biting, kinked tube, pneumothorax, pulmonary edema/ARDS, or breath stacking.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- high peak pressure
- plateau high or normal helps distinguish compliance vs resistance
- sudden desaturation
- unequal breath sounds
- obstructive waveform

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Assess patient first, not just machine.
2. Check tube/circuit/biting/secretions.
3. Consider pneumothorax if sudden instability.
4. Compare peak and plateau when safe.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to high-pressure alarm approach?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

# CPAP vs BiPAP/NPPV


## Definition
CPAP provides one continuous pressure; BiPAP/NPPV provides inspiratory and expiratory pressure support to improve ventilation and oxygenation.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Choosing the right noninvasive support can avoid intubation, but using it in a failing airway can delay definitive care.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- COPD/hypercapnia: ventilation support often helpful
- cardiogenic pulmonary edema: pressure support/recruitment helpful
- altered mental status/shock/vomiting: higher failure risk
- emergent intubation need is a contraindication

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen-based concept: degree of respiratory distress is central to NPPV initiation, but need for emergent intubation is an absolute contraindication.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Assess work of breathing and mental status.
2. Start only when patient can tolerate/protect airway and local protocol supports it.
3. Reassess early for failure.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to cpap vs bipap/nppv?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

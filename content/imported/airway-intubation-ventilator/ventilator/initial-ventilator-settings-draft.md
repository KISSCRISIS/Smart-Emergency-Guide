# Initial Ventilator Settings Draft


## Definition
Initial settings translate the clinical problem into mode, tidal volume, rate, FiO₂, PEEP, inspiratory flow/time, and alarms.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Bad initial settings can cause hypoxemia, hypercapnia, auto-PEEP, hypotension, or ventilator-induced lung injury.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- PBW not actual body weight for tidal volume
- high peak pressure
- hypercapnia/acidosis
- oxygen requirement
- hemodynamic response

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Educational draft: common initial adult ED strategy often starts with assist-control mode, Vt 6–8 mL/kg predicted body weight, RR around 14–20/min, FiO₂ 1.0 then titrate down, PEEP around 5 cm H₂O.
- ARDS/lung injury draft: prioritize low tidal volume around 6 mL/kg PBW and plateau pressure target ≤30 cm H₂O when applicable.
- Obstructive disease draft: use lower rate/longer expiratory time to reduce breath stacking.
- Final settings must be adjusted to blood gas, mechanics, disease state, and local protocol.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Choose mode and Vt based on lung protection.
2. Set FiO₂/PEEP for oxygenation then titrate.
3. Set RR/minute ventilation for CO₂/acid-base needs.
4. Reassess with ETCO₂, blood gas, pressure alarms, and hemodynamics.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to initial ventilator settings draft?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

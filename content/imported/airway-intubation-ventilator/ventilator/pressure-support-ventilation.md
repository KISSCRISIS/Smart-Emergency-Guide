# Pressure Support Ventilation


## Definition
A spontaneous mode where the ventilator assists patient-triggered breaths with set pressure support.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Useful for supported spontaneous breathing and weaning, but not for apneic or severely unstable patients.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- patient must trigger breaths
- tidal volume depends on effort and mechanics
- monitor fatigue
- not definitive for absent respiratory drive

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Use only if patient has adequate drive and monitoring.
2. Watch RR, Vt, work of breathing, and gas exchange.
3. Escalate if fatigue develops.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to pressure support ventilation?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

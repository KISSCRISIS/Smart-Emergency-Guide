# Asthma/COPD Ventilation


## Definition
Ventilation strategy for obstructive disease prioritizes avoiding dynamic hyperinflation and intrinsic PEEP.

## Why It Matters in the ED
The patient may arrest from breath stacking and reduced venous return, even if oxygen looks acceptable.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- wheezing/silent chest
- high peak pressure
- auto-PEEP
- hypotension after intubation
- long expiratory flow not returning to baseline

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Educational obstructive draft: lower RR, adequate expiratory time, high inspiratory flow, permissive hypercapnia strategy when appropriate, and careful sedation/bronchodilation.
- Disconnecting from ventilator briefly may be life-saving in peri-arrest auto-PEEP according to local critical care practice.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Think exhalation first.
2. Check for breath stacking on waveform.
3. Treat bronchospasm and anxiety/synchrony.
4. Avoid excessive minute ventilation.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to asthma/copd ventilation?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

# Post-Intubation Sedation and Analgesia


## Definition
A plan for comfort, ventilator synchrony, analgesia, and prevention of awareness after intubation and paralysis.

## Why It Matters in the ED
The tube is not the finish line; inadequate sedation after paralysis is traumatic and unsafe.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- long-acting paralytic used
- tachycardia/hypertension or agitation
- ventilator dyssynchrony
- painful condition still active

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Educational draft: choose analgesia-first or sedative strategy based on local protocol, hemodynamics, and disease state.
- Avoid deep unnecessary sedation; monitor sedation depth when possible.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Start a post-intubation plan immediately after confirmation.
2. Treat pain and anxiety, not just movement.
3. Reassess blood pressure after sedatives.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to post-intubation sedation and analgesia?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

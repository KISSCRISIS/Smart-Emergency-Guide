# Trauma Airway


## Definition
Airway management in trauma balances oxygenation, cervical spine protection, hemorrhage control, shock physiology, and aspiration risk.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Trauma patients may die from hypoxia, hemorrhage, aspiration, or post-intubation collapse.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- facial trauma/bleeding
- c-spine risk
- shock or positive FAST
- chest injury
- TBI requiring controlled ventilation

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Control bleeding/suction.
2. Maintain spinal precautions as locally practiced.
3. Resuscitate hemorrhagic shock before induction when possible.
4. Prepare surgical airway if facial anatomy is destroyed.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to trauma airway?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

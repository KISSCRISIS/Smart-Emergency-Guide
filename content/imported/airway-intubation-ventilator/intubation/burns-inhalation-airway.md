# Burns and Inhalation Airway


## Definition
Airway risk after burns or inhalation injury may progress due to edema, soot, thermal injury, and toxic gases.

## Why It Matters in the ED
A manageable airway can become impossible as swelling progresses.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- facial burns
- soot in mouth/nose
- hoarseness/stridor
- enclosed-space fire
- progressive edema

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen/Tintinalli-supported medication caution: succinylcholine hyperkalemia risk becomes important after the early post-burn window, commonly taught around 5 days.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Assess early for airway progression.
2. Intubate before edema prevents access when indicated.
3. Check CO/cyanide patterns in fire victims according to toxicology protocol.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to burns and inhalation airway?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

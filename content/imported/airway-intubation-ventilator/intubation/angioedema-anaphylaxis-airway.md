# Angioedema and Anaphylaxis Airway


## Definition
Upper-airway swelling can rapidly threaten patency, especially tongue, floor-of-mouth, laryngeal, or progressive facial swelling.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Delayed airway planning may leave no oral route.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- tongue swelling
- voice change
- stridor
- drooling
- rapid progression
- ACE inhibitor or hereditary angioedema history

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Treat anaphylaxis immediately if present.
2. Call expert airway early for progressive swelling.
3. Consider awake strategy when obstruction is predicted.
4. Prepare front-of-neck backup.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to angioedema and anaphylaxis airway?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

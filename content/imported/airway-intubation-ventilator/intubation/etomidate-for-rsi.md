# Etomidate for RSI


## Definition
Etomidate is a commonly used induction agent with relative hemodynamic stability and no analgesic properties.

## Why It Matters in the ED
It is often considered when hypotension risk exists, but it does not treat pain and still requires post-intubation sedation/analgesia planning.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- shock patient requiring induction
- need to avoid major blood pressure drop
- adrenal suppression debate
- no analgesia

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Typical educational RSI induction draft: etomidate 0.3 mg/kg IV once.
- Use lower/modified dosing only according to local protocol and patient physiology.
- No analgesia: plan analgesia/sedation after tube confirmation.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Use with paralysis only when RSI plan is ready.
2. Prepare post-intubation sedation and analgesia.
3. Do not confuse hemodynamic stability with absence of risk.

## Common Trap
Assuming etomidate provides analgesia.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to etomidate for rsi?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

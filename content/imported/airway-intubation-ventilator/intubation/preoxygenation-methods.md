# Preoxygenation Methods


## Definition
Methods include nonrebreather mask, flush-rate oxygen, nasal cannula/apneic oxygenation, HFNC, NIV/CPAP, and BVM with PEEP depending on patient condition.

## Why It Matters in the ED
The goal is maximal oxygen reserve before apnea.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- standard NRB may be insufficient in shunt physiology
- NIV can help recruit alveoli before intubation
- agitation may limit tolerance
- trauma may limit positioning

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen-based draft: flush-rate nonrebreather oxygen can provide higher end-tidal oxygen than a standard 15 L/min nonrebreather.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Choose method based on hypoxemia severity and tolerance.
2. Use head-up/ramped position when safe.
3. Keep apneic oxygenation during attempt when feasible.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to preoxygenation methods?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

# Pre-Intubation Optimization


## Definition
Correction or mitigation of physiologic derangements before induction when time allows.

## Why It Matters in the ED
The sickest patients often die after intubation because shock and hypoxemia were not optimized.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- hypotension before RSI
- severe hypoxemia
- metabolic acidosis with high minute ventilation
- RV failure or pulmonary hypertension
- trauma hemorrhage

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen-based educational point: resuscitation with appropriate volume/blood before induction can reduce circulatory collapse in unstable trauma when time allows.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Optimize oxygen reserve.
2. Support circulation before induction.
3. Anticipate the effect of positive pressure on preload and RV function.
4. Choose induction strategy based on physiology.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to pre-intubation optimization?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

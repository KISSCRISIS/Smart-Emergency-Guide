# Propofol in Airway and Sedation


## Definition
Propofol is a short-acting sedative-hypnotic with antiemetic properties, no analgesia, and dose-dependent hypotension/apnea.

## Why It Matters in the ED
It can be excellent for selected stable patients but dangerous in preload-dependent shock.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- volume depletion
- elderly patient
- hypotension risk
- need for analgesic coadministration
- antiemetic effect

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Typical educational induction/sedation draft: titrate carefully; common ED bolus ranges vary by indication and patient physiology.
- Rosen-based concept: propofol has profound antiemetic properties but can cause respiratory depression, apnea, and hypotension; preload-dependent patients are particularly susceptible.
- Correct hypovolemia before propofol when possible.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Avoid automatic full-dose use in elderly/shock.
2. Pair with analgesia when pain is present.
3. Prepare airway support before administration.

## Common Trap
Using propofol like a benign sleep medicine in a volume-depleted patient.

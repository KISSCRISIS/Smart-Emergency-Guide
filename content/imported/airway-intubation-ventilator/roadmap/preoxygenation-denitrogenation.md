# Preoxygenation and Denitrogenation


## Definition
Preoxygenation replaces alveolar nitrogen with oxygen to extend safe apnea time during airway management.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Poor preoxygenation shortens the time to desaturation and increases peri-intubation arrest risk.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- hypoxemia before induction
- obesity or pregnancy
- pneumonia/ARDS/shunt physiology
- agitated patient unable to tolerate mask

## Rosen-Based Educational Draft
- Rosen-based educational point: flush-rate nonrebreather oxygen can improve end-tidal oxygen compared with standard 15 L/min flow.
- Head-up or ramped positioning can improve preoxygenation when not contraindicated.

**Final status:** Requires final local protocol/formulary verification before bedside or public clinical use.

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Position the patient for physiology and laryngoscopy.
2. Use the best tolerated oxygen delivery method.
3. Consider NIV/CPAP-style support when shunt physiology is severe and local protocol allows.
4. Do not rush induction before oxygen reserve is optimized unless immediate airway loss is occurring.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to preoxygenation and denitrogenation?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

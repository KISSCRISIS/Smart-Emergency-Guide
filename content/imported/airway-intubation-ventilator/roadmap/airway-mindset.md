# Airway Mindset in the Emergency Department


## Definition
A structured way to decide whether the patient needs oxygenation support, ventilation support, airway protection, or a definitive airway.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Airway failure kills quickly, but premature intubation without physiologic preparation can also precipitate collapse.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- altered mental status with inability to clear secretions
- exhaustion or rising work of breathing
- shock before intubation
- need for transport or procedure with unstable airway

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Ask: oxygenation problem, ventilation problem, protection problem, or anticipated course?
2. Prepare suction, oxygen, BVM, adjuncts, definitive airway plan, and backup plan.
3. Resuscitate before intubation when physiology allows.
4. Declare difficulty early rather than repeating failed attempts.

## Common Trap
Using absence of gag reflex as a stand-alone trigger for intubation. Rosen emphasizes that gag reflex is unreliable for airway protection.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to airway mindset in the emergency department?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

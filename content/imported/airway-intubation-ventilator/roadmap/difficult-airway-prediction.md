# Difficult Airway Prediction


## Definition
A pre-attempt screen for difficulty with laryngoscopy, BVM, supraglottic airway, or front-of-neck access.

## Why It Matters in the ED
The most dangerous airway is the one that becomes difficult after paralysis without a rescue plan.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- obesity, facial hair, edentulous anatomy
- short neck or limited mobility
- facial/airway trauma, angioedema, airway burns
- history of snoring/sleep apnea or prior difficult airway

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Predict difficulty across all rescue pathways, not just intubation.
2. Prepare backup device and surgical airway plan early.
3. Limit repeated attempts and declare failure early.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to difficult airway prediction?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

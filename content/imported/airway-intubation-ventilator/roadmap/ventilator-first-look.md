# Ventilator First Look


## Definition
A rapid check of oxygenation, ventilation, pressures, synchrony, and hemodynamics after connecting a patient to a ventilator.

## Why It Matters in the ED
A newly ventilated patient can crash from tube misplacement, tension pneumothorax, auto-PEEP, shock, or wrong settings.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- SpO₂ and ETCO₂ trend
- peak and plateau pressure pattern
- blood pressure after positive pressure
- patient-ventilator dyssynchrony
- high-pressure or low-pressure alarm

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Confirm tube and circuit first.
2. Check oxygenation and ventilation separately.
3. Assess hemodynamics and breath stacking.
4. Use DOPE/DOPES-type thinking for sudden deterioration.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to ventilator first look?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

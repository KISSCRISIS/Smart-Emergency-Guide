# Oxygenation vs Ventilation


## Definition
Oxygenation is getting oxygen into blood; ventilation is removing carbon dioxide through adequate alveolar gas exchange.

## Why It Matters in the ED
A patient can have acceptable SpO₂ but dangerous hypercapnia, or severe hypoxemia with initially normal CO₂.

## First 60-Second Impression
Look at mental status, work of breathing, oxygen saturation trend, capnography if available, hemodynamics, and whether the patient can protect and clear the airway.

## Key ED Clues
- SpO₂ trend and FiO₂ requirement for oxygenation
- ETCO₂, VBG/ABG, mental status, and respiratory pattern for ventilation
- COPD/asthma: ventilation failure can be the killer
- pneumonia/ARDS: oxygenation failure often dominates

## Conceptual First ED Actions
1. Separate the problem into oxygenation, ventilation, or both.
2. Use waveform capnography when an advanced airway is present.
3. Do not use pulse oximetry alone to judge ventilation.
4. Match support: oxygen, HFNC/NIV, BVM, intubation, ventilator.

## Common Trap
Treating the monitor number without asking whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, and perfusing.

## Assessment Question
**A patient requires emergency airway support. What is the safest first principle related to oxygenation vs ventilation?**

A. Delay airway planning until all laboratory tests return.
B. Identify the immediate physiologic threat, optimize oxygenation/ventilation, and use local protocol for definitive intervention.
C. Rely on one physical sign alone to decide all airway management.
D. Ignore hemodynamics once the tube is placed.

**Correct Answer:** B

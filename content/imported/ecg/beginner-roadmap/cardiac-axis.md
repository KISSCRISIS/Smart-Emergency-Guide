# Cardiac Axis

## Core Definition
The frontal-plane QRS axis is the average direction of ventricular depolarization. A practical beginner method uses Lead I and Lead II or Lead I and aVF to classify the axis as normal, left axis deviation, right axis deviation or extreme axis.

## ECG Recognition Clues
Lead I positive and Lead II positive usually suggests normal adult axis. Lead I positive with Lead II negative suggests left axis deviation. Lead I negative with Lead II positive suggests right axis deviation. Lead I negative and Lead II negative suggests extreme axis or possible lead error.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Axis abnormalities can suggest fascicular block, ventricular hypertrophy, pulmonary disease, previous infarction or lead reversal. In the ED, axis is a clue, not a diagnosis by itself.

## Common Trap
Do not diagnose disease from axis alone. Always check limb-lead placement and compare with previous ECG.

## Assessment Question
Lead I is positive and Lead II is negative. Which axis category is most likely?
A) Normal axis
B) Left axis deviation
C) Right axis deviation
D) Indeterminate ventricular rate

**Answer:** B) Left axis deviation

**Arabic Rationale:** Lead I يرى الكهرباء باتجاهه، بينما Lead II لا يراها باتجاهه؛ هذا يدفعك للتفكير بانحراف يساري، مع ضرورة استبعاد خطأ توصيل الأسلاك.

# Waveforms: P, QRS, ST, T, U

## Core Definition
Waveform analysis examines the shape, amplitude and direction of each ECG component: P wave, QRS complex, ST segment, T wave and U wave.

## ECG Recognition Clues
P wave abnormalities suggest atrial enlargement or ectopic atrial activity. Q waves can suggest infarction but may be normal in some leads. ST elevation/depression suggests injury or ischemia but has mimics. T wave inversion or peaking can signal ischemia, electrolyte abnormality or repolarization variants. Prominent U waves suggest hypokalemia or bradycardia-related repolarization changes.

## Why It Matters in the ED
Many life-threatening diagnoses live in waveform details rather than rate or rhythm: STEMI, Wellens, hyperkalemia, TCA toxicity and Brugada pattern.

## Common Trap
Do not read one lead in isolation. ECG meaning comes from pattern recognition across anatomically related leads.

## Assessment Question
Tall, narrow, tented T waves diffusely across multiple leads should immediately raise concern for which condition?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Isolated atrial enlargement
C) First-degree AV block
D) Lead I reversal only

**Answer:** A) Hyperkalemia

## Purpose

ECG Essentials teaches interns a safe, repeatable first-pass approach to emergency ECGs.

This source is for:

- recognizing danger early
- connecting the ECG to the patient
- avoiding false reassurance
- knowing when to call senior help
- preparing for JMC-style ECG traps
- documenting and handing over ECG concern clearly

This source is not:

- a full ECG Atlas
- a cardiology interpretation manual
- a cath-lab activation protocol
- a local hospital pathway
- a treatment algorithm
- a visual ECG library
- a replacement for senior or consultant review

**Intern rule:**  
ECG Essentials is for recognition and escalation, not independent cardiology-level interpretation.

## Safety Disclaimer

This is a general educational framework. ECG interpretation must be correlated with the patient and reviewed according to local policy, senior physician guidance, and consultant input when needed.

Interns should not make independent final diagnostic, disposition, reperfusion, rhythm-management, or high-risk treatment decisions based on this page alone.

---

## 1. Start With the Patient

ECG is never read alone.

Before reading the tracing, ask:

- Why was the ECG done?
- What symptom triggered it?
- Is the patient unstable?
- Is this chest pain, dyspnea, syncope, palpitations, altered mental status, overdose, electrolyte concern, or incidental finding?
- Is there an old ECG for comparison?
- Does the ECG match the clinical story?
- Is the patient changing while you are reading?

**Intern trap:**  
Reading the ECG as a pattern-recognition puzzle while ignoring the patient.

**Senior trigger:**  
Any ECG concern in a patient with shock, severe chest pain, syncope, severe dyspnea, seizure, overdose, or altered mental status.

---

## 2. First-Pass ECG System

Use the same sequence every time.

1. Confirm patient and ECG context
2. Check technical quality and artifact
3. Estimate rate
4. Identify rhythm broadly
5. Look at axis
6. Review intervals
7. Check QRS width
8. Review ST segment
9. Review T waves
10. Notice QT concern
11. Compare with old ECG if available
12. Match ECG with symptoms and stability
13. Decide: reassuring, concerning, or senior-now

**Intern focus:**  
A structured first pass prevents random reading and reduces missed danger.

**JMC trap:**  
Jumping directly to ST elevation or rhythm naming without checking context, QRS width, intervals, and the patient.

---

## 3. Senior-Now ECG Patterns

Call senior urgently for ECG concern with any of the following:

- STEMI or STEMI-equivalent concern
- posterior MI concern
- right ventricular MI concern
- Wellens-pattern concern
- De Winter-pattern concern
- diffuse ischemia / left-main concern
- wide-complex tachycardia
- ventricular tachycardia concern
- ventricular fibrillation or pulseless rhythm concern
- complete heart block
- severe bradycardia with symptoms
- hyperkalemia ECG concern
- sodium-channel blocker toxicity concern
- long QT with syncope, seizure, or concerning symptoms
- Brugada-pattern concern with syncope
- WPW / pre-excitation with tachyarrhythmia concern
- pericarditis versus MI uncertainty
- PE-related ECG clues in an unstable patient
- any ECG abnormality in overdose or unknown ingestion
- any ECG that does not fit a patient who looks sick

**Intern rule:**  
You do not need to name the exact final diagnosis before escalating a dangerous pattern.

- hyperacute T-wave concern

Intern framing:

### C. Rhythm

Topics:

- sinus tachycardia
- sinus bradycardia
- atrial fibrillation
- atrial flutter
- SVT
- wide-complex tachycardia
- ventricular tachycardia
- torsades-risk concept
- premature beats
- paced rhythm basics

Intern framing:

- Stability comes before rhythm labeling.
- Wide-complex tachycardia is a senior-now pattern.
- Fast rhythm plus shock, chest pain, syncope, dyspnea, or altered mental status needs urgent help.
- Do not call a wide rhythm “SVT” confidently as an intern.
- Do not ignore electrolyte or toxicology causes.

### D. Conduction

Topics:

- first-degree AV block
- second-degree AV block concept
- complete heart block
- right bundle branch block
- left bundle branch block
- bifascicular-block concern
- WPW / pre-excitation
- Brugada pattern

Intern framing:

- Heart block with symptoms is not a casual finding.
- Syncope plus conduction abnormality needs escalation.
- New bundle branch block with concerning symptoms needs senior review.
- WPW / pre-excitation with tachyarrhythmia concern is senior-now.
- Brugada-pattern concern with syncope is senior-now.

### E. Electrolytes / Toxicology

Topics:

- hyperkalemia ECG concern
- hypokalemia ECG concern
- calcium-related QT concept
- sodium-channel blocker ECG concern
- digoxin effect versus toxicity concept
- QT-prolonging medication concern

Intern framing:

- Do not treat the ECG as separate from renal disease, overdose, medication history, or symptoms.
- ECG abnormality in overdose is senior-now.
- QT concern with syncope, seizure, or multiple QT-risk medications needs escalation.
- Hyperkalemia ECG concern is an emergency-pattern recognition task, not a solo intern management task.

### F. Other Emergency Patterns

Topics:

- pericarditis pattern
- myocarditis concern
- pulmonary embolism ECG clues
- hypothermia ECG concept
- LVH strain pattern
- HCM-related red flags
- syncope red-flag ECGs

Intern framing:

- Pericarditis versus MI uncertainty needs review.
- ECG clues for PE do not rule in or rule out PE alone.
- Syncope with ECG abnormality should not be reassured quickly.
- HCM / Brugada / WPW / long QT concern in syncope requires senior review.

## 6. JMC / Intern ECG Traps

Common traps:

- reading ECG without clinical context
- reassuring chest pain because the ECG is not obviously dramatic
- calling ST elevation “STEMI” without considering mimics, while also missing STEMI equivalents
- missing posterior MI when the anterior leads show reciprocal-looking changes
- ignoring old ECG comparison
- ignoring ECG in overdose or unknown ingestion
- calling wide-complex tachycardia SVT too quickly
- reassuring syncope with a dangerous ECG pattern
- missing hyperkalemia ECG concern in renal failure
- treating QT concern as a minor incidental finding when the story is dangerous
- focusing on rhythm name while missing patient instability
- over-trusting a single automated ECG interpretation

## 7. Quick ECG Checklist

Before handing over an ECG, answer:

- Why was the ECG done?
- Is the patient stable?
- What is the main symptom?
- Is rate concerning?
- Is rhythm broadly regular or irregular?
- Is QRS narrow or wide?
- Is there ST elevation or depression concern?
- Are T waves concerning?
- Is QT concern present?
- Is there an old ECG?
- Does the ECG match the symptoms?
- Is this safe to observe, concerning, or senior-now?
- What is pending?
- Who needs to review it?

---

## 8. Handover Examples

Use short, risk-focused handover language.

### Chest pain ECG

“Chest pain patient with current stability, ECG concern, old ECG comparison status, working concern, and senior review need.”

### Syncope ECG

“Syncope patient with current stability, ECG red flag concern, baseline risk factors, and need for senior review.”

### Overdose ECG

“Overdose or unknown ingestion with ABCDE status, ECG concern, QT/QRS concern if present, toxidrome clues, and senior or poison-center plan.”

### Rhythm concern

“Patient with rhythm concern, stability status, QRS narrow or wide, symptoms, possible triggers, and senior-now decision.”

### Electrolyte concern

“Patient with electrolyte-risk context, symptoms, ECG concern, renal or medication risk, and need for senior review.”

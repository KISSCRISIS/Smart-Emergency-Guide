## Page Purpose

This page teaches the intern how to think safely before, during, and after ED medication use. It is not a drug dosing table and not a substitute for local drug references.

The intern goal is simple:

- Recognize high-risk medication situations.
- Check patient-specific risk factors before prescribing or administering.
- Confirm uncertain plans with a senior, nurse, pharmacist, or local reference.
- Monitor after medication.
- Document clearly.
- Avoid acting alone with high-risk medication decisions.

---

## 1. Medication Safety Mindset

Medication decisions in the ED happen under pressure, often before the full diagnosis is clear. The safest intern is not the one who memorizes the most drug names, but the one who pauses before harm happens.

Before any ED medication, ask:

- What exact problem am I treating?
- Is this medication necessary now?
- What patient factor makes this risky?
- What could go wrong after giving it?
- Who should confirm the plan?
- What monitoring is needed?

**Intern principle:**
A medication order is a clinical decision, not a clerical task.

## 2. The 30-Second Medication Pause

Before medication, use a fast structured pause.

### Patient checks

- Allergy history and reaction type.
- Pregnancy possibility when relevant.
- Age, frailty, and body habitus.
- Pediatric patient or weight-sensitive context.
- Kidney disease or dialysis.
- Liver disease.
- Current medications.
- Anticoagulant or antiplatelet use.
- Sedative, opioid, alcohol, or substance exposure.
- Baseline mental status.
- Baseline respiratory status.
- Prior adverse drug reaction.

### Medication checks

- Indication.
- Contraindication.
- Route.
- Monitoring need.
- Expected response.
- Major adverse effect to watch for.
- Senior confirmation if high-risk.
- Local formulary or protocol if required.

**JMC trap:**
The same symptom does not mean the same medication is safe in every patient.

## 3. High-Risk Drug Groups in the ED

These are not â€œforbidden medications.â€ They are medications that deserve extra attention because errors may cause serious harm.

### Opioids

**Why high-risk:**
Respiratory depression, sedation, hypotension, falls, worsening altered mental status, masking deterioration.

**Intern focus:**
Assess respiratory status, sedation risk, concurrent sedatives/alcohol, age/frailty, head injury concern, and reassess after administration.

### Sedatives and anxiolytics

**Why high-risk:**
Airway obstruction, hypoventilation, aspiration risk, paradoxical agitation, hypotension, prolonged sedation.

**Intern focus:**
Do not treat agitation as a medication problem until you think about hypoxia, hypoglycemia, sepsis, head injury, intoxication, withdrawal, psychiatric causes, pain, and safety.

### Paralytics and RSI-related drugs

**Why high-risk:**
They can remove respiratory effort and make airway failure immediate.

**Intern focus:**
Interns should not independently choose or administer paralytics/sedation for airway management. Understand the concept, prepare equipment, assist safely, and call senior/anesthesia early.

### Vasopressors and inotropes

**Why high-risk:**
They imply shock physiology and require monitoring, IV access considerations, senior-led resuscitation, and a clear disposition plan.

**Intern focus:**
If the patient may need vasoactive support, the case is not â€œroutine.â€ Escalate early.

### Anticoagulants and antiplatelets

**Why high-risk:**
Bleeding, trauma, procedures, renal impairment, drug interactions, and reversal decisions.

**Intern focus:**
Ask about bleeding, falls/trauma, planned procedures, renal disease, and interacting medications.

### Insulin and glucose-changing therapies

**Why high-risk:**
Hypoglycemia, recurrent hypoglycemia, missed monitoring, confusion between treatment response and true resolution.

**Intern focus:**
Any medication that changes glucose requires a monitoring plan and reassessment, not a one-time â€œfix.â€

### Potassium and electrolyte therapies

**Why high-risk:**
Arrhythmia risk, kidney disease, ECG changes, lab error, speed of change, and overtreatment.

**Intern focus:**
Do not treat the lab number alone. Think patient, ECG, kidney function, symptoms, and trend.

### Antiarrhythmics

**Why high-risk:**
Can worsen hypotension, conduction disease, drug toxicity, electrolyte-related rhythm problems, or proarrhythmia.

**Intern focus:**
Stability comes before rhythm labeling. Senior review is essential when the rhythm, stability, or cause is unclear.

### Antibiotics

**Why high-risk:**
Allergy, renal adjustment, local resistance, source mismatch, interactions, delayed source control, and missed sepsis.

**Intern focus:**
Think severity, source, allergy, kidney function, local guideline, cultures when relevant, and reassessment.

### Toxicology-related antidotes

**Why high-risk:**
Wrong antidote, wrong patient, wrong timing, missed co-ingestion, rebound toxicity, and false reassurance.

**Intern focus:**
Recognize possible poisoning, stabilize ABCDE, obtain key information, involve senior/poison center early, and avoid solo antidote decisions.

## 4. What Interns Should Not Do Alone

The intern should not independently make decisions involving medications that can rapidly alter:

- Airway
- Breathing
- Circulation
- Consciousness
- Cardiac rhythm
- Bleeding risk
- Electrolyte balance
- Toxicologic course

Call senior early before:

- Sedation for severe agitation.
- Paralytic or RSI-related planning.
- Vasopressors or inotropes.
- High-risk pediatric medication use.
- Anticoagulation reversal.
- Toxicology antidotes.
- Severe electrolyte correction.
- Antiarrhythmic therapy.
- Polypharmacy in frail older adults.
- Any medication when the patient is unstable or deteriorating.

**Strong rule:**
If a medication can destabilize the patient faster than you can fix the complication, do not manage it alone.

## 5. Medication Confirmation Workflow

Use a layered confirmation process.

### Confirm with the senior

Ask:

- What is the indication?
- What response are we expecting?
- What adverse effect should I watch for?
- What should trigger escalation?
- Is monitoring/disposition appropriate?

### Confirm with the nurse

Clarify:

- Patient identity.
- Allergy status.
- Route.
- Access.
- Timing.
- Monitoring.
- What change should be reported immediately.

### Confirm with pharmacy or local reference

Use when:

- High-risk medication.
- Pediatric or frail elderly patient.
- Renal/liver disease.
- Pregnancy/lactation.
- Multiple medications.
- Unfamiliar medication.
- Toxicology/antidote situation.

### Confirm with yourself

Ask:

- Does this medication match the working diagnosis?
- Could it mask deterioration?
- Could it interact with home meds or ED meds already given?
- Is this patient high-risk even if the diagnosis seems simple?

## 6. Monitoring After Medication

Giving a medication does not end the task. Medication safety includes reassessment.

Monitor depending on the medication and patient:

- Mental status.
- Respiratory effort.
- Oxygenation.
- Blood pressure and perfusion.
- Heart rate/rhythm.
- Pain or symptom response.
- Allergic reaction.
- Bleeding.
- Nausea/vomiting.
- Sedation level.
- Agitation or paradoxical reaction.
- Need for repeat senior review.

**Intern trap:**
â€œImprovedâ€ does not always mean â€œsafe.â€ Improvement may be temporary, medication-related, or misleading.

Document clearly:

- Medication name.
- Indication.
- Route.
- Time given.
- Allergy check.
- Senior discussion when relevant.
- Baseline status before medication.
- Response after medication.
- Adverse effects if present.
- Monitoring plan.
- Handover instructions.

## 8. Common JMC / Exam Traps

### Trap 1 â€” Treating the number instead of the patient

A lab abnormality matters, but the clinical picture, ECG, kidney function, trend, and symptoms matter too.

### Trap 2 â€” Ignoring allergy history

Clarify the reaction. Side effect, intolerance, and true allergy are not the same, but serious reactions must not be dismissed.

### Trap 3 â€” Forgetting renal or liver disease

Medication risk changes when clearance is impaired. Ask, check, and escalate.

### Trap 4 â€” Sedating the undifferentiated agitated patient

Agitation may be hypoxia, hypoglycemia, intoxication, withdrawal, sepsis, intracranial pathology, pain, or psychiatric illness.

### Trap 5 â€” Missing duplicate therapy

The patient may have received medications at home, from EMS, triage, another facility, or earlier in the ED.

### Trap 6 â€” Giving a high-risk medication without monitoring

Some medications require reassessment and observation. Giving and walking away is unsafe.

### Trap 7 â€” Treating toxicology like routine pharmacology

Overdose changes absorption, duration, recurrence, and co-ingestion risk. Escalate early.

### Trap 8 â€” Calling late

If you are uncertain, that is the signal to ask, not a reason to wait.

## 9. Quick Medication Safety Checklist

Before giving or prescribing:

- What is the indication?
- Is this medication necessary now?
- Are allergies checked?
- Any pregnancy concern?
- Any renal/liver disease?
- Any pediatric or frail elderly concern?
- Any anticoagulant or antiplatelet use?
- Any opioid/sedative/alcohol exposure?
- Any interacting home medication?
- Is this high-risk?
- Do I need senior/pharmacy confirmation?
- Is monitoring available?
- Did I communicate the plan?
- Did I document it?
- Did I reassess?

**Final takeaway:**
Safe ED medication practice is not memorizing every drug. It is risk recognition, deliberate checking, escalation, monitoring, and documentation.

# Pediatrics Intern/JMC Reviewed Source

## Intern Scope

Pediatrics content for Intern/JMC should focus on:

- recognizing the sick child
- identifying red flags
- escalating early
- communicating clearly with seniors
- avoiding false reassurance
- using structured handover

It should not include:

- medication amount tables
- weight-based independent treatment instructions
- detailed resuscitation algorithms
- procedure instructions
- independent disposition decisions
- local protocol replacement

## Core Rule

Children can compensate until late, then deteriorate quickly.  
An intern should prioritize appearance, breathing, circulation, hydration, mental state, caregiver concern, and early escalation.

## Universal Pediatric First Look

Use this mental checklist:

1. Is the child alert, interactive, or unusually quiet?
2. Is breathing comfortable or labored?
3. Is color/perfusion normal?
4. Is hydration adequate?
5. Are there danger signs?
6. Is the caregiver worried that the child is "not normal"?
7. Does this need senior review now?

## High-Yield Pediatric Syndromes

### Fever in a Child

Intern focus:

- identify toxic appearance
- consider age, immune risk, hydration, rash, breathing, and mental state
- avoid reassurance based on fever alone
- escalate fever with systemic red flags

Red flags:

- lethargy or poor interaction
- persistent irritability
- non-blanching rash
- neck stiffness or seizure
- poor feeding
- respiratory distress
- poor perfusion
- dehydration signs
- very young infant with fever

Common trap:

- assuming a child is safe because the temperature improved

Safe handover phrase:

"Fever with pediatric red flags. Needs senior review and monitored assessment."

### Respiratory Distress

Intern focus:

- look at work of breathing before relying on numbers
- recognize exhaustion and silent chest as danger signs
- consider bronchiolitis, asthma-like wheeze, pneumonia, foreign body, sepsis, and cardiac causes
- escalate early when breathing effort is high

Red flags:

- grunting
- severe retractions
- cyanosis
- inability to feed or speak appropriately for age
- drowsiness
- apnea
- persistent low oxygenation
- silent chest or exhaustion

Common trap:

- calling wheeze simple without considering severity and exhaustion

Safe handover phrase:

"Child has respiratory distress with red flags. Senior review needed now."

### Dehydration and Gastroenteritis Pattern

Intern focus:

- assess hydration and perfusion
- look for lethargy, poor intake, reduced urine, sunken eyes, dry mucosa, and shock features
- consider non-gastroenteritis causes of vomiting or diarrhea
- escalate severe dehydration or altered mental state

Red flags:

- lethargy
- shock features
- persistent vomiting
- blood in stool
- bilious vomiting
- severe abdominal pain
- very young age
- poor urine output
- caregiver reports worsening or not acting normally

Common trap:

- assuming vomiting and diarrhea are always benign gastroenteritis

Safe handover phrase:

"Possible dehydration or alternative serious diagnosis. Needs senior review."

### Seizure or Altered Consciousness

Intern focus:

- recognize prolonged or recurrent seizure as high-risk
- check bedside glucose early
- consider fever, infection, trauma, metabolic issue, intoxication, and neurological causes
- escalate persistent altered consciousness

Red flags:

- prolonged seizure
- repeated seizures
- focal signs
- persistent drowsiness
- meningism
- head trauma
- hypoglycemia concern
- first seizure with abnormal recovery

Common trap:

- ignoring persistent post-event abnormal mental state

Safe handover phrase:

"Child had seizure or altered consciousness with concerning features. Needs urgent senior review."

### Neonatal and Young Infant Concerns

Intern focus:

- treat young infants conservatively
- fever, poor feeding, lethargy, breathing difficulty, jaundice, abnormal cry, or temperature instability should trigger concern
- escalate early rather than observe casually

Red flags:

- poor feeding
- reduced activity
- temperature instability
- apnea
- cyanosis
- seizures
- jaundice with illness
- bulging fontanelle
- caregiver concern

Common trap:

- waiting for obvious signs; young infants may show subtle illness

Safe handover phrase:

"Young infant with concerning features. Needs senior pediatric review urgently."

### Pediatric Abdominal Pain

Intern focus:

- distinguish mild pain from surgical or systemic illness
- look for bilious vomiting, severe pain, guarding, distension, blood in stool, testicular pain, and dehydration
- escalate when pain is severe, localized, persistent, or associated with systemic signs

Red flags:

- bilious vomiting
- peritonism
- persistent severe pain
- distension
- blood in stool
- testicular pain
- shock features
- fever with worsening pain

Common trap:

- diagnosing constipation without checking for surgical red flags

Safe handover phrase:

"Child has abdominal pain with red flags. Needs senior review."

## JMC Exam Pattern

Common Pediatrics exam themes:

- recognize the sick child
- identify respiratory distress
- identify dehydration severity concern
- do not miss sepsis or meningitis clues
- treat young infants as higher risk
- escalate rather than over-reassure
- choose safe next step over detailed independent treatment

## SBAR Handover Template

Situation:
- age of child
- main concern
- why you are worried

Background:
- duration
- feeding and urine
- fever or respiratory symptoms
- relevant medical background

Assessment:
- appearance
- breathing effort
- perfusion
- hydration
- mental state
- red flags found

Recommendation:
- senior review needed
- urgency
- suspected syndrome

# Surgery Intern/JMC Reviewed Source

## Status

It is not a public page.  
It must not be linked from public navigation.  
It is intended to guide later Intern/JMC content creation.

## Intern Scope

Surgery content for Intern/JMC should focus on:

- recognizing surgical red flags
- identifying who needs urgent senior or surgical review
- safe assessment framing
- avoiding delayed escalation
- handover clarity

It should not include:

- operative steps
- procedure instructions
- independent perioperative management plans
- medication or fluid amount tables
- independent discharge decisions
- local protocol replacement

## Core Rule

The intern's safe role in surgery is early recognition, initial assessment, and timely escalation.  
Do not attempt to independently rule out high-risk surgical disease when red flags are present.

## Universal Surgical First Look

For any surgical presentation, ask:

1. Is the patient unstable?
2. Is there peritonism?
3. Is pain severe, sudden, worsening, or disproportionate?
4. Is there vomiting, distension, bleeding, ischemic pain, or sepsis concern?
5. Is there trauma?
6. Is there a vulnerable context: elderly, pregnant, anticoagulated, immunosuppressed?
7. Does this need urgent surgical review?

## High-Yield Surgical Syndromes

### Acute Abdomen

Intern focus:

- identify peritonism and obstruction patterns
- consider appendicitis, perforation, obstruction, pancreatitis, biliary disease, vascular causes, and gynecologic mimics
- escalate severe, progressive, or localized pain with systemic features

Red flags:

- guarding or rigidity
- hypotension or syncope
- persistent vomiting
- abdominal distension
- severe localized pain
- fever with abdominal signs
- blood in stool or vomit
- elderly patient with severe pain
- pain out of proportion

Common trap:

- labeling abdominal pain as gastritis without reassessment or senior review

Safe handover phrase:

"Acute abdominal presentation with surgical red flags. Needs senior or surgical review."

### Trauma

Intern focus:

- use structured primary survey thinking
- recognize mechanism of injury and hidden bleeding risk
- escalate major trauma, head injury concern, chest/abdominal injury, unstable vitals, or distracting injuries
- do not be reassured by early normal appearance after significant mechanism

Red flags:

- abnormal airway, breathing, or circulation
- altered mental status
- hypotension
- severe chest, abdominal, pelvic, or back pain
- neurological deficit
- anticoagulant use with head trauma
- high-energy mechanism
- multiple injuries

Common trap:

- focusing on visible wounds while missing internal injury risk

Safe handover phrase:

"Trauma patient has red flags or significant mechanism. Needs senior trauma review."

### Wounds and Soft Tissue Infection

Intern focus:

- assess severity, contamination, neurovascular status, and systemic illness
- recognize rapidly progressive infection or deep space involvement
- escalate wounds with tendon, joint, nerve, vessel, bite, foreign body, or severe infection concern

Red flags:

- rapidly spreading redness
- severe pain out of proportion
- crepitus
- systemic toxicity
- immunosuppression or diabetes context
- neurovascular deficit
- deep structure exposure
- hand, face, joint, or genital involvement

Common trap:

- treating a high-risk wound as simple skin infection

Safe handover phrase:

"Wound or soft tissue infection has high-risk features. Needs senior or surgical review."

### Vascular and Limb Threat

Intern focus:

- recognize acute limb ischemia and compartment concern
- assess pain, pallor, temperature, movement, sensation, and pulses
- escalate immediately when limb threat is possible

Red flags:

- sudden severe limb pain
- cold or pale limb
- weakness or sensory change
- absent or changed pulses
- pain with passive stretch
- tense compartment concern
- trauma with vascular concern

Common trap:

- waiting for complete signs before escalating limb threat

Safe handover phrase:

"Possible limb-threatening vascular or compartment problem. Needs urgent senior review."

### Urology Emergencies

Intern focus:

- recognize testicular torsion, urinary retention with systemic concern, obstructed infected system, and severe renal colic mimics
- escalate time-sensitive scrotal pain and infection with obstruction concern

Red flags:

- sudden testicular pain
- high-riding or abnormal testis
- severe flank pain with fever
- sepsis features
- anuria
- severe pain with instability
- urinary retention with renal or infection concern

Common trap:

- waiting for imaging when time-sensitive torsion is clinically concerning

Safe handover phrase:

"Urologic emergency cannot be excluded. Needs urgent senior or urology review."

### Post-Operative Patient

Intern focus:

- assess airway, breathing, circulation, pain, fever, wound, bleeding, urine output, and mental state
- escalate early when post-operative deterioration occurs
- do not assume fever or tachycardia is normal after surgery

Red flags:

- hypotension
- tachycardia with symptoms
- new breathlessness
- chest pain
- confusion
- bleeding
- severe pain
- reduced urine output
- wound concern
- sepsis features

Common trap:

- delaying review because the patient is "just post-op"

Safe handover phrase:

"Post-operative patient is deteriorating or has red flags. Needs senior surgical review."

## JMC Exam Pattern

Common Surgery exam themes:

- acute abdomen red flags
- surgical emergency recognition
- trauma priorities
- torsion and limb threat as time-sensitive diagnoses
- sepsis in surgical patients
- post-operative deterioration
- safe escalation over independent management

## SBAR Handover Template

Situation:
- patient and surgical concern
- unstable or stable
- why urgent

Background:
- onset
- operations or trauma
- comorbidities and anticoagulants
- key symptoms

Assessment:
- vitals
- abdomen or wound or limb findings
- red flags
- relevant bedside findings

Recommendation:
- urgent senior or surgical review
- specific concern to exclude

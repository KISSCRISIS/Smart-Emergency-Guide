# Internal Medicine Intern/JMC Reviewed Source

## Status

This is an unpublished reviewed source draft.

It is not a public page.  
It must not be linked from public navigation.  
It is intended to guide later Intern/JMC content creation.

## Scope for Intern Level

The intern version should focus on:

- recognizing dangerous presentations
- identifying what must not be missed
- early escalation to senior help
- safe handover
- exam-pattern thinking
- avoiding common traps

The intern version should not include:

- medication amount tables
- independent treatment pathways
- independent discharge decisions
- advanced procedure instructions
- local protocol replacement
- specialist-level management plans

## Core Intern Rule

An intern is not expected to independently manage high-risk Internal Medicine emergencies.  
The safe role is to recognize risk, start basic assessment, communicate clearly, and escalate early.

## Universal First Approach

Use this structure for any acute Internal Medicine presentation:

1. Check immediate danger.
2. Look for unstable vital signs.
3. Identify the chief syndrome.
4. Screen for red flags.
5. Review available ECG, bedside glucose, oxygenation, and focused history.
6. Call senior help early when red flags are present.
7. Give a concise SBAR handover.
8. Document what was found, who was informed, and the agreed plan.

## High-Yield Syndromes

### Chest Pain

Intern focus:

- rule out time-sensitive cardiac and vascular causes
- identify unstable patients quickly
- obtain and communicate ECG findings promptly
- avoid false reassurance from a single normal early test
- escalate persistent, exertional, crushing, radiating, or instability-associated pain

Red flags:

- hypotension, syncope, severe sweating, altered mental status
- new ECG abnormality
- ongoing pain with risk factors
- chest pain with breathlessness or collapse
- pain radiating to arm, jaw, back, or epigastrium

Common trap:

- treating young age or anxiety as proof that the pain is benign

Safe handover phrase:

"Patient with chest pain and red flags. ECG and initial assessment need senior review now."

### Shortness of Breath

Intern focus:

- assess work of breathing
- check oxygenation
- identify wheeze, crackles, silent chest, cyanosis, or exhaustion
- consider cardiac, pulmonary, infectious, and thromboembolic causes
- escalate severe respiratory distress early

Red flags:

- inability to speak full sentences
- low oxygenation
- drowsiness or exhaustion
- chest pain with dyspnea
- unilateral leg swelling with dyspnea
- sudden onset dyspnea
- abnormal chest findings with instability

Common trap:

- assuming all wheeze is simple asthma
- missing heart failure, pulmonary embolism, pneumonia, or pneumothorax

Safe handover phrase:

"Patient is breathless with red flags. Needs senior review and monitored assessment."

### Altered Mental Status

Intern focus:

- treat altered mental status as high-risk until proven otherwise
- check bedside glucose early
- consider infection, metabolic disturbance, seizure, stroke, intoxication, hypoxia, and shock
- document baseline mental state if known
- escalate urgently if reduced consciousness is new or worsening

Red flags:

- new confusion
- reduced consciousness
- seizure
- focal neurological deficit
- fever with neck stiffness or rash
- hypoxia
- head trauma
- severe dehydration or shock features

Common trap:

- labeling confusion as psychiatric before excluding medical causes

Safe handover phrase:

"New altered mental status. Bedside glucose and ABCDE assessment done; urgent senior review needed."

### Stroke-Like Symptoms

Intern focus:

- recognize sudden focal neurological deficits
- note exact time last known well when possible
- do not delay escalation for detailed workup
- communicate speech, face, arm, vision, gait, and consciousness findings

Red flags:

- sudden weakness
- facial droop
- speech disturbance
- visual loss
- severe sudden headache
- reduced consciousness
- new seizure with focal signs

Common trap:

- waiting for symptoms to improve before escalating

Safe handover phrase:

"Possible acute stroke syndrome. Last known well is documented or unknown. Needs urgent pathway review."

### Syncope and Collapse

Intern focus:

- distinguish fainting from seizure, arrhythmia, bleeding, shock, or pulmonary embolism
- check for injury, pregnancy possibility, cardiac symptoms, and abnormal ECG
- escalate exertional syncope, chest pain, palpitations, or family history of sudden death

Red flags:

- syncope during exertion
- chest pain or palpitations
- abnormal ECG
- hypotension
- shortness of breath
- neurological deficit
- significant bleeding
- recurrent episodes

Common trap:

- assuming vasovagal syncope without checking cardiac and bleeding risks

Safe handover phrase:

"Collapse with concerning features. Needs senior review before any disposition decision."

### Fever and Possible Sepsis

Intern focus:

- identify infection with instability
- recognize fever can be absent in vulnerable patients
- look for confusion, hypotension, tachypnea, mottling, oliguria, or severe weakness
- escalate early when systemic illness is suspected
- follow local sepsis pathway under supervision

Red flags:

- altered mental state
- hypotension or poor perfusion
- fast breathing
- severe rigors
- immunosuppression
- very young, elderly, pregnant, or frail patient
- suspected meningitis or non-blanching rash

Common trap:

- waiting for lab results before calling for help in an unstable patient

Safe handover phrase:

"Possible sepsis with red flags. Senior review and local pathway activation needed."

### Abdominal Pain and GI Bleeding

Intern focus:

- identify shock, peritonism, bleeding, obstruction, pancreatitis, and high-risk abdominal pain
- ask about vomiting blood, black stool, severe pain, pregnancy possibility, anticoagulant use, and syncope
- escalate early when bleeding or peritoneal signs exist

Red flags:

- hypotension or syncope
- hematemesis or melena
- rigid abdomen
- persistent severe pain
- fever with abdominal pain
- jaundice with sepsis features
- pregnancy possibility with lower abdominal pain or bleeding

Common trap:

- calling abdominal pain benign without reassessment or senior review when red flags exist

Safe handover phrase:

"Abdominal presentation has red flags. Needs senior review and urgent surgical or medical consideration."

### Diabetes and Glucose Emergencies

Intern focus:

- check bedside glucose in collapse, confusion, seizure, weakness, and altered behavior
- recognize dehydration, vomiting, abdominal pain, deep breathing, and ketone concern
- do not manage complex metabolic emergencies alone
- escalate suspected DKA, HHS, severe hypoglycemia, or electrolyte concern

Red flags:

- reduced consciousness
- vomiting with dehydration
- deep or labored breathing
- severe weakness
- suspected ketones
- infection trigger
- pregnancy
- renal disease

Common trap:

- correcting a number without understanding the whole patient condition

Safe handover phrase:

"Glucose-related emergency suspected. Patient has red flags and needs supervised pathway care."

### Renal and Electrolyte Presentations

Intern focus:

- suspect electrolyte problems in weakness, palpitations, confusion, renal failure, vomiting, diarrhea, and medication history
- review ECG when electrolyte abnormality is suspected
- escalate abnormal ECG, severe symptoms, renal impairment, or rapid changes
- do not attempt unsupervised correction plans

Red flags:

- arrhythmia or ECG changes
- paralysis or severe weakness
- confusion or seizure
- renal failure
- severe dehydration
- anuria or severe oliguria

Common trap:

- treating electrolyte values without clinical context or ECG review

Safe handover phrase:

"Electrolyte or renal emergency suspected. Symptoms or ECG concern present; senior review needed."

### Hypertension Presentations

Intern focus:

- separate uncomplicated high reading from emergency features
- look for chest pain, neurological deficit, confusion, dyspnea, visual symptoms, pregnancy, renal symptoms, or heart failure signs
- escalate suspected end-organ involvement

Red flags:

- neurological symptoms
- chest pain
- acute breathlessness
- confusion
- pregnancy or postpartum context
- renal failure features
- severe headache with neurological signs

Common trap:

- treating the number alone without looking for end-organ injury

Safe handover phrase:

"High blood pressure with possible end-organ features. Needs senior review."

### Anemia, Bleeding, and Anticoagulation Context

Intern focus:

- assess symptoms, hemodynamic stability, bleeding source, medication history, and comorbidities
- escalate active bleeding, syncope, chest pain, breathlessness, or instability
- avoid independent transfusion or reversal decisions

Red flags:

- active bleeding
- hypotension or syncope
- chest pain
- severe breathlessness
- black stool or vomiting blood
- anticoagulant use with bleeding or head injury

Common trap:

- focusing only on hemoglobin without assessing current bleeding and instability

Safe handover phrase:

"Bleeding or anemia with red flags. Needs senior review and monitored plan."

## JMC Exam Pattern

For Intern/JMC questions, the safest recurring pattern is:

- identify the dangerous diagnosis
- choose the next safe escalation step
- avoid premature reassurance
- avoid independent discharge when red flags exist
- prioritize unstable patients
- communicate clearly using SBAR

## SBAR Handover Template

Situation:
- who the patient is
- main problem
- why you are worried

Background:
- key history
- risk factors
- relevant medications or comorbidities

Assessment:
- vital signs concern
- examination red flags
- ECG, glucose, oxygenation, or key bedside findings

Recommendation:
- senior review needed
- urgency level
- immediate concern to rule out

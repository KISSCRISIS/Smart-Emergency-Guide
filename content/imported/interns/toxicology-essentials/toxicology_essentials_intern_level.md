## Page Purpose

This page teaches the intern how to recognize possible poisoning, stabilize first, collect useful information, identify major toxidrome patterns, escalate early, and avoid unsafe solo decisions.

It does **not** teach antidote dosing, decontamination procedures, or hospital-specific toxicology protocols.

The intern goal:

- Suspect poisoning early.
- Stabilize ABCDE.
- Check glucose and ECG when appropriate.
- Look for toxidrome patterns.
- Identify red flags.
- Call senior and poison center early.
- Avoid premature reassurance.
- Document exposure details and reassessment.

## 1. Toxicology Mindset

Toxicology in the ED is dangerous because the history may be incomplete, the patient may deteriorate later, and the exposure may involve multiple agents.

Do not treat toxicology as â€œnormal pharmacology.â€ Overdose changes:

- absorption
- duration
- recurrence
- metabolism
- co-ingestion risk
- monitoring needs
- disposition decisions

**Intern principle:**
A poisoned patient can look stable early and become unstable later.

## 2. First Approach â€” ABCDE Before the Substance

When the substance is unknown or the story is unclear, start with physiology.

### A â€” Airway

Ask:

- Can the patient protect the airway?
- Is there vomiting, aspiration risk, trauma, seizure, or reduced consciousness?
- Is the patient deteriorating?

### B â€” Breathing

Check:

- respiratory effort
- oxygenation
- hypoventilation
- bronchorrhea or wheeze
- respiratory depression or fatigue

### C â€” Circulation

Check:

- perfusion
- blood pressure trend
- heart rate and rhythm
- shock signs
- temperature and skin findings

### D â€” Disability

Check:

- glucose
- level of consciousness
- pupils
- seizure activity
- agitation, delirium, or coma

### E â€” Exposure

Look for:

- patches
- pills
- needle marks
- chemical exposure
- odors
- burns
- trauma
- hyperthermia or hypothermia

**Intern trap:**
Do not wait for the poison name before treating airway, breathing, circulation, hypoglycemia, seizure, or dangerous dysrhythmia concerns.

## 3. The Minimum Toxicology Information Set

Collect practical information without delaying stabilization.

Ask patient, family, EMS, police, nurse, or bring medication containers when available:

- What was taken?
- How much might be missing?
- When was it taken?
- Was it intentional, accidental, occupational, or recreational?
- Any co-ingestions?
- Alcohol or sedative use?
- Current medications?
- Past psychiatric history?
- Pregnancy possibility?
- Pediatric access or household exposure?
- Any vomiting, seizure, collapse, or trauma?
- What was found at the scene?
- What has already been given by EMS or another facility?

**Do not trust the first story completely.**
In overdose, the first history is often incomplete.

## 4. Toxidrome Thinking

A toxidrome is a pattern that helps you think before the exact drug is known.

### Opioid pattern

Think about:

- reduced consciousness
- respiratory depression
- small pupils
- recurrent sedation risk
- long-acting or mixed ingestion risk

**Intern focus:**
Airway and breathing first. If the patient improves, still reassess because recurrence is possible.

### Sympathomimetic pattern

Think about:

- agitation
- tachycardia
- hypertension
- sweating
- hyperthermia
- dilated pupils
- seizure risk

**Intern focus:**
Temperature, agitation, rhythm, and end-organ complications matter.

### Anticholinergic pattern

Think about:

- delirium
- dry skin and mucosa
- urinary retention
- decreased bowel sounds
- tachycardia
- dilated pupils

**Intern focus:**
Do not confuse all agitation with psychiatric disease. Look for dryness and retention.

### Cholinergic pattern

Think about:

- salivation
- lacrimation
- urination
- diarrhea
- vomiting
- bronchorrhea
- bronchospasm
- bradycardia
- weakness

**Intern focus:**
Secretions and breathing can kill. Escalate early.

### Sedative-hypnotic pattern

Think about:

- drowsiness
- slurred speech
- ataxia
- respiratory depression in severe cases
- co-ingestion risk

**Intern focus:**
Protect airway, check glucose, consider trauma, and reassess.

### Serotonergic pattern

Think about:

- agitation or confusion
- hyperthermia
- tremor
- clonus
- hyperreflexia
- autonomic instability

**Intern focus:**
Neuromuscular findings help distinguish it from many mimics.

### Cardiotoxic pattern

Think about:

- hypotension
- conduction delay
- wide-complex rhythm concern
- seizure
- shock
- abnormal ECG

**Intern focus:**
Any poisoning with ECG abnormalities is senior-level immediately.

## 5. High-Yield ED Tests in Suspected Toxicology

These tests do not replace clinical judgment, but they often help identify danger.

Common early checks:

- bedside glucose
- ECG
- basic electrolytes and acid-base pattern
- renal function
- pregnancy test when relevant
- acetaminophen level when ingestion is possible or unclear
- salicylate level when analgesic ingestion is possible or unclear
- targeted levels when clinically indicated
- temperature
- continuous monitoring for unstable or high-risk patients

**Intern trap:**
A negative urine drug screen does not rule out poisoning. Many important toxins are not detected, and many positive results do not explain the current danger.

---

## 6. When to Call Senior / Poison Center Immediately

Escalate immediately for:

- airway compromise
- respiratory depression
- recurrent sedation
- seizure
- severe agitation or delirium
- hyperthermia
- hypotension or shock
- bradycardia with poor perfusion
- dangerous ECG changes
- suspected cardiotoxic ingestion
- pediatric ingestion
- pregnancy
- intentional overdose
- unknown substance with symptoms
- multiple co-ingestions
- long-acting agent concern
- sustained-release or delayed absorption concern
- chemical or occupational exposure
- need for any antidote decision
- need for decontamination decision
- any patient you are unsure about

**Strong rule:**
If the decision involves antidotes, decontamination, ICU-level monitoring, or discharge after overdose, do not decide alone.

---

## 7. What Interns Should Not Do Alone

Do not independently decide:

- antidote use
- decontamination
- observation duration
- discharge after intentional overdose
- discharge after symptomatic poisoning
- discharge after pediatric ingestion
- management of cardiotoxic ingestion
- management of severe agitation/hyperthermia
- management of recurrent sedation
- management of toxic alcohol concern
- management of unknown mixed ingestion

**Intern role:**
Recognize, stabilize, gather data, monitor, document, and escalate.

---

## 8. Documentation Essentials

Document:

- suspected substance or unknown exposure
- time of exposure if known
- estimated amount if known
- source of history
- co-ingestions
- intent: accidental, recreational, occupational, self-harm, unclear
- baseline mental status
- vital signs and trends
- glucose and ECG when performed
- toxidrome features
- discussion with senior
- poison center or toxicology advice if obtained
- reassessment findings
- safety concerns and psychiatric risk when relevant
- handover plan

## 9. Common JMC / Exam Traps

### Trap 1 â€” Forgetting glucose

Altered mental status in intoxication still needs glucose consideration.

### Trap 2 â€” Believing the first history

The patient may not know, may not remember, may hide, or may have co-ingestions.

### Trap 3 â€” Reassurance after temporary improvement

Some agents outlast initial reversal or have delayed toxicity.

### Trap 4 â€” Missing ECG toxicity

Toxicology can present as arrhythmia, conduction delay, hypotension, or seizure.

### Trap 5 â€” Treating agitation as psychiatric first

Agitation with fever, autonomic findings, clonus, dryness, secretions, or abnormal vitals may be toxicologic or metabolic.

### Trap 6 â€” Ignoring acetaminophen/salicylate possibility

Patients may confuse over-the-counter analgesics. Serious occult toxicity can be missed if not considered.

### Trap 7 â€” Using urine drug screen as a rule-out test

It is not a comprehensive toxicology screen and does not reliably explain clinical severity.

### Trap 8 â€” Discharging too early

Intentional ingestion, pediatric ingestion, long-acting agents, sustained-release products, abnormal ECG, persistent symptoms, or unclear exposure require senior-led disposition.

## 10. Quick Toxicology Checklist

For suspected poisoning:

- Is ABCDE stable?
- Is glucose checked when mental status is altered?
- Is ECG needed or done?
- Is there a toxidrome pattern?
- What was taken?
- When was it taken?
- How much might be missing?
- Any co-ingestions?
- Any long-acting or sustained-release concern?
- Any pediatric or pregnancy concern?
- Is this intentional self-harm?
- Has senior been called?
- Is poison center/toxicology needed?
- Is monitoring/reassessment planned?
- Is disposition senior-approved?
- Is documentation complete?

**Final takeaway:**
Toxicology is pattern recognition plus physiology. Stabilize first, identify danger patterns, escalate early, and never let a temporary improvement create false safety.

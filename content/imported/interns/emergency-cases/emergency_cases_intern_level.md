## Page Purpose

This page teaches the intern how to think through common emergency presentations safely.

It does not give full protocols, medication dosing, or final disposition rules. Each case is designed to train:

- first impression
- immediate danger recognition
- focused assessment
- what to tell the senior
- what not to miss
- common exam traps
- handover thinking

**Intern-level goal:**
Recognize danger early, organize the story, escalate appropriately, and avoid false reassurance.

## How to Use These Cases

For every case, think in the same sequence:

1. Is the patient sick or unstable?
2. What immediate life threats must be considered?
3. What focused information changes risk?
4. What should be checked early?
5. What needs senior review now?
6. What is the common trap?
7. What must be handed over?

**Safety rule:**
If the patient looks worse than the diagnosis sounds, trust the patient, not the label.

# Case 1 â€” Chest Pain

## Presentation

A patient presents with chest discomfort, sweating, shortness of breath, or vague upper body discomfort. The story may be classic, atypical, or unclear.

## What the intern should notice

- abnormal vital signs
- diaphoresis or distress
- shortness of breath
- syncope or near-syncope
- older age or major risk factors
- radiation to arm, jaw, back, or epigastrium
- new ECG concern
- persistent or worsening symptoms

## Immediate thinking

Chest pain is not only acute coronary syndrome. Think:

- ACS / MI
- pulmonary embolism
- aortic dissection
- pneumothorax
- pericarditis/myocarditis
- esophageal rupture
- pneumonia
- chest wall pain only after danger is considered

## Call senior early if

- abnormal ECG
- unstable vitals
- ongoing severe pain
- syncope
- suspicion of dissection or PE
- new heart failure signs
- diagnostic uncertainty

## JMC / exam trap

Calling a patient â€œlow riskâ€ before ECG and senior review.

## Handover line

â€œChest pain patient with [key risk], [current stability], ECG status, working differential, and what is pending.â€

# Case 2 â€” Shortness of Breath

## Presentation

A patient presents with dyspnea, increased work of breathing, wheeze, hypoxia, chest tightness, cough, or inability to speak comfortably.

## What the intern should notice

- work of breathing
- oxygenation
- ability to speak
- mental status
- silent chest
- cyanosis
- hypotension
- chest asymmetry
- fever or sepsis concern
- known asthma/COPD/heart failure
- sudden onset

## Immediate thinking

Dyspnea can be airway, lung, heart, blood, metabolic, toxicologic, or anxiety â€” but anxiety is a diagnosis of exclusion.

Think:

- asthma/COPD exacerbation
- pneumonia
- pulmonary edema
- pulmonary embolism
- pneumothorax
- anaphylaxis
- sepsis
- metabolic acidosis
- toxic exposure
- ACS presenting as dyspnea

## Call senior early if

- severe respiratory distress
- hypoxia not improving
- altered mental status
- silent chest
- hypotension
- exhaustion
- suspected anaphylaxis
- need for ventilatory support discussion

## JMC / exam trap

Reassuring yourself because the chest is â€œwheezy.â€ Wheeze can coexist with anaphylaxis, pulmonary edema, PE, or foreign body.

## Handover line

â€œDyspneic patient with current oxygenation/work of breathing, suspected cause, response so far, and escalation concern.â€

# Case 3 â€” Altered Mental Status

## Presentation

A patient is confused, drowsy, agitated, unresponsive, behaving abnormally, or not at baseline.

## What the intern should notice

- airway risk
- breathing pattern
- glucose concern
- fever or hypothermia
- trauma signs
- focal neurology
- pupil abnormalities
- seizure/postictal state
- intoxication or withdrawal
- medication exposure
- sepsis concern

## Immediate thinking

Use a broad differential:

- hypoglycemia or metabolic disturbance
- sepsis
- stroke or intracranial bleed
- seizure/postictal state
- intoxication/overdose
- hypoxia/hypercapnia
- head injury
- psychiatric cause only after medical danger is assessed

## Call senior early if

- reduced consciousness
- airway concern
- seizure
- new focal deficit
- fever with confusion
- suspected overdose
- agitation with abnormal vitals
- unclear cause

## JMC / exam trap

Labeling the patient as psychiatric before checking medical causes.

## Handover line

â€œAltered patient with baseline, current mental status, glucose/ECG status if relevant, key neuro findings, suspected causes, and safety concerns.â€

# Case 4 â€” Abdominal Pain

## Presentation

A patient presents with abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, diarrhea, constipation, GI bleeding, pelvic pain, or vague weakness.

## What the intern should notice

- shock signs
- peritonitis
- severe pain out of proportion
- GI bleeding
- pregnancy possibility
- elderly or immunocompromised patient
- persistent vomiting
- syncope
- fever/sepsis concern
- surgical history
- anticoagulant use

## Immediate thinking

Do not treat abdominal pain as â€œsimpleâ€ too early.

Consider:

- appendicitis
- biliary disease
- pancreatitis
- bowel obstruction
- perforation
- mesenteric ischemia
- AAA in appropriate patient
- ectopic pregnancy
- ovarian/testicular emergency
- DKA or metabolic cause
- sepsis

## Call senior early if

- peritonitis
- hypotension
- severe persistent pain
- elderly with concerning presentation
- pregnancy concern
- GI bleeding
- suspected surgical abdomen
- pain out of proportion
- unclear diagnosis with abnormal vitals

## JMC / exam trap

Giving symptom treatment and forgetting reassessment.

## Handover line

â€œAbdominal pain patient with location, onset, peritoneal signs, pregnancy/bleeding risk, vitals, and surgical concern.â€

# Case 5 â€” Fever / Sepsis Concern

## Presentation

A patient presents with fever, rigors, confusion, hypotension, tachycardia, respiratory symptoms, urinary symptoms, abdominal symptoms, wound infection, or nonspecific weakness.

## What the intern should notice

- abnormal vital signs
- altered mental status
- hypotension or poor perfusion
- respiratory distress
- immunosuppression
- elderly or frail patient
- pregnancy
- indwelling lines or devices
- recent surgery
- rash or meningism
- source not obvious

## Immediate thinking

Fever is not the only sign of sepsis. Some high-risk patients may not mount fever.

Think:

- source
- severity
- perfusion
- organ dysfunction clues
- need for senior-led escalation
- need for timely investigation and treatment according to local policy

## Call senior early if

- hypotension
- altered mental status
- respiratory distress
- suspected meningitis
- immunocompromised patient
- persistent abnormal vitals
- unclear source with deterioration
- concern for shock

## JMC / exam trap

Waiting for all results before escalating a clinically deteriorating patient.

## Handover line

â€œPossible sepsis with suspected source, current perfusion, mental status, respiratory status, investigations pending, and escalation needs.â€

# Case 6 â€” Trauma

## Presentation

A patient arrives after fall, road traffic crash, assault, penetrating injury, crush injury, head injury, or unknown mechanism.

## What the intern should notice

- mechanism of injury
- airway or breathing concern
- hypotension
- altered mental status
- external bleeding
- chest/abdominal/pelvic injury signs
- anticoagulant use
- spinal concern
- distracting injury
- intoxication
- pediatric or elderly patient

## Immediate thinking

Trauma is physiology plus mechanism. Do not let a normal-looking wound distract from internal injury.

Think:

- primary survey
- bleeding
- chest injury
- head injury
- spinal risk
- abdominal/pelvic injury
- missed injuries
- reassessment after initial stability

## Call senior early if

- abnormal airway/breathing/circulation/disability
- significant mechanism
- hypotension
- chest/abdominal/pelvic concern
- altered mental status
- penetrating injury
- anticoagulant use with head injury
- pediatric or elderly major trauma

## JMC / exam trap

Focusing on the obvious limb injury while missing shock or head injury.

## Handover line

â€œTrauma patient with mechanism, primary survey status, key injuries suspected, anticoagulant status, and what needs urgent reassessment.â€

# Case 7 â€” Seizure

## Presentation

A patient presents during seizure, after seizure, with recurrent events, altered mental status, injury, pregnancy, fever, known epilepsy, or possible toxin/metabolic cause.

## What the intern should notice

- ongoing seizure
- airway and breathing
- hypoxia
- trauma
- glucose concern
- fever/meningitis concern
- pregnancy/postpartum
- toxin or withdrawal concern
- focal deficit after event
- failure to return to baseline

## Immediate thinking

Seizure is not always â€œknown epilepsy.â€

Think:

- hypoglycemia
- head injury
- CNS infection
- stroke/bleed
- toxicology
- withdrawal
- pregnancy-related emergency
- electrolyte/metabolic disturbance

## Call senior early if

- ongoing or recurrent seizure
- airway concern
- failure to return to baseline
- new focal deficit
- pregnancy/postpartum
- fever/meningism
- trauma
- suspected toxicology
- first seizure

## JMC / exam trap

Assuming all seizures in an epileptic patient are benign.

## Handover line

â€œSeizure patient with event duration/recurrence, current mental status, glucose status, injuries, fever/pregnancy/toxin concerns, and baseline recovery.â€

# Case 8 â€” Anaphylaxis / Severe Allergic Reaction

## Presentation

A patient presents with rash, swelling, wheeze, vomiting, hypotension, collapse, or symptoms after food, medication, sting, latex, contrast, or unknown exposure.

## What the intern should notice

- airway swelling
- voice change
- stridor
- wheeze
- hypotension
- collapse
- vomiting/abdominal symptoms
- rapid progression
- exposure timing
- prior anaphylaxis history

## Immediate thinking

Anaphylaxis is a clinical emergency. Skin findings may be absent.

Think:

- airway
- breathing
- circulation
- exposure
- progression
- recurrence risk
- senior review

## Call senior early if

- airway symptoms
- respiratory distress
- hypotension
- syncope/collapse
- unclear severe allergic reaction
- poor response
- recurrent symptoms
- high-risk comorbidity

## JMC / exam trap

Waiting for rash to appear before treating the syndrome as serious.

## Handover line

â€œAllergic reaction/anaphylaxis concern with exposure, airway/breathing/circulation status, progression, treatment response, and recurrence monitoring.â€

# Case 9 â€” Pediatric Fever / Dehydration

## Presentation

A child presents with fever, poor intake, vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, irritability, reduced urine output, rash, or parental concern.

## What the intern should notice

- age
- appearance
- work of breathing
- hydration signs
- urine output
- mental status
- rash
- neck stiffness
- persistent vomiting
- immunization status
- caregiver concern
- high-risk age group
- chronic disease

## Immediate thinking

Children compensate until they do not. A quiet or lethargic child can be more concerning than a crying child.

Think:

- sepsis
- meningitis
- pneumonia
- UTI
- dehydration
- DKA/metabolic issue
- non-accidental injury when context suggests
- surgical abdomen

## Call senior early if

- toxic appearance
- lethargy
- poor perfusion
- respiratory distress
- non-blanching rash
- persistent vomiting
- dehydration concern
- very young infant
- parental concern that something is seriously wrong
- unclear diagnosis

## JMC / exam trap

Using adult reassurance patterns in children.

## Handover line

â€œPediatric fever/dehydration patient with age, appearance, hydration, breathing, perfusion, urine output, and caregiver concern.â€

# Case 10 â€” Overdose / Unknown Ingestion

## Presentation

A patient presents after overdose, unknown ingestion, found down, altered mental status, intentional self-harm, recreational substance use, or unclear exposure.

## What the intern should notice

- airway/breathing/circulation
- glucose
- ECG concern
- mental status
- pupils
- temperature
- seizure
- agitation
- co-ingestion
- time of exposure
- long-acting or sustained-release concern
- self-harm risk
- medication containers

## Immediate thinking

Overdose can be delayed, recurrent, mixed, or misleading.

Think:

- ABCDE
- glucose
- ECG
- toxidrome
- acetaminophen/salicylate possibility when relevant
- co-ingestion
- suicide risk
- poison center/senior review

## Call senior early if

- abnormal ABCDE
- unknown substance with symptoms
- intentional overdose
- pediatric ingestion
- abnormal ECG
- seizure
- hyperthermia
- hypotension
- recurrent sedation
- long-acting/sustained-release concern
- need for antidote or decontamination decision

## JMC / exam trap

Trusting a normal early appearance after a potentially serious ingestion.

## Handover line

â€œOverdose/unknown ingestion with substance/time/amount if known, ABCDE status, glucose/ECG status, toxidrome features, and poison center/senior plan.â€

## Cross-Case Intern Red Flags

Across any ED case, call senior early if you see:

- airway compromise
- severe respiratory distress
- hypotension or shock
- reduced consciousness
- active or recurrent seizure
- new focal neurological deficit
- severe pain out of proportion
- abnormal ECG with symptoms
- major trauma
- anaphylaxis concern
- pediatric toxic appearance
- pregnancy with concerning symptoms
- any patient you are unsure about

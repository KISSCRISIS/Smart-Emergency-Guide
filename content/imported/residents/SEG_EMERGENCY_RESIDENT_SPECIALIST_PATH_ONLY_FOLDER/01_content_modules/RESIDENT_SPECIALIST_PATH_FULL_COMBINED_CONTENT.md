# Resident ED Leadership Mindset

## Purpose

Define the resident-level role in the emergency department.

## Why It Matters

Residents are often the link between:
- unstable patient
- intern/student team
- nursing team
- consultant
- ICU/OR/cath lab/stroke team
- family communication
- disposition decision

## Resident First-Pass Questions

For every patient, ask:

- Is this patient sick or not sick?
- What kills them first?
- What is my leading dangerous differential?
- What information changes disposition?
- Who needs to know now?
- What can wait?
- What must be reassessed?
- What did the junior miss?

## Resident Responsibilities

- prioritize unstable patients
- lead structured assessment
- prevent premature closure
- coordinate resources
- communicate clearly
- supervise junior doctors
- confirm high-risk discharges
- document reasoning
- escalate early when needed

## Dangerous Mindset Errors

- acting like a protocol machine
- ignoring nursing concern
- failing to reassess
- accepting incomplete handover
- leaving disposition vague
- overconfidence after one normal test
- delegating without closing the loop

## Exam Trap

Board-style emergency medicine questions often test the next safest decision, not the most sophisticated diagnosis.

## Arabic Pearl

القائد في الطوارئ ليس من يعرف كل شيء، بل من يرتب الخطر ويغلق الدائرة.

# Resuscitation Room Leadership

## Purpose

Give residents a structure for the first minutes of a critical patient.

## Why It Matters

The resus room fails when tasks happen without shared priorities.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Start with:

- announce role and team structure
- identify immediate life threats
- assign tasks
- request vital trends
- ask for history source
- confirm monitoring/access/support
- plan reassessment points
- call consultants early when appropriate

## Team Communication

Use:

- closed-loop communication
- task assignment by name/role
- periodic summary
- clear escalation language
- “what are we missing?”
- “what is the next decision point?”

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

Depends on presentation, but always consider:

- shock
- airway compromise
- respiratory failure
- ACS/arrhythmia
- sepsis
- stroke/seizure
- hemorrhage/trauma
- anaphylaxis
- toxicology
- pregnancy-related catastrophe

## Disposition Implications

Resus room patients commonly need:
- ICU
- OR
- cath lab/stroke pathway
- transfer
- close observation
- consultant-led decision

## Cognitive Trap

Doing many interventions without naming the working problem.

## Exam Trap

The safest answer may be “stabilize and call for help/consultation,” not ordering a rare test first.

## Arabic Pearl

في غرفة الإنعاش، الكلام المنظم علاج بحد ذاته.

# Undifferentiated Shock

## Purpose

Help residents approach shock by physiology, not guesswork.

## Why It Matters

Shock is a syndrome. Waiting for the final diagnosis can be dangerous.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Think in categories:

- distributive
- hypovolemic
- cardiogenic
- obstructive
- mixed shock

Ask:

- Is the patient perfusing?
- What is the likely shock category?
- What bedside data can narrow this quickly?
- What can kill first?
- What consultation/disposition is needed?

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- septic shock
- hemorrhage
- ACS/cardiogenic shock
- pulmonary embolism
- tamponade
- tension pneumothorax
- ruptured AAA
- anaphylaxis
- severe dehydration/metabolic shock
- toxicologic shock

## Decision Points

- airway/breathing risk
- volume responsiveness vs overload risk
- pressor/inotrope concept under local protocol
- urgent imaging vs immediate intervention
- POCUS utility
- blood product/surgical pathway in hemorrhage
- ICU/OR/cath lab need

## Consultation Triggers

- persistent shock
- suspected surgical/trauma bleed
- cardiogenic shock
- massive PE concern
- tamponade
- severe sepsis with organ dysfunction
- pregnancy/postpartum shock
- pediatric shock

## Cognitive Traps

- labeling all hypotension as dehydration
- missing obstructive shock
- not reassessing after interventions
- delaying ICU/consultant communication
- ignoring narrow pulse pressure, mental status, urine output context

## Exam Trap

Shock questions often hinge on recognizing physiology before definitive tests.

## Arabic Pearl

الصدمة ليست رقم ضغط فقط؛ هي فشل تروية حتى يثبت العكس.

# Airway Strategy & Post-Intubation Risk

## Purpose

Give residents a high-level airway decision framework without procedural steps or drug doses.

## Why It Matters

The dangerous airway decision is not only “intubate or not,” but:
- why now?
- who should do it?
- what could go wrong?
- what happens after?

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- Is airway failure present or impending?
- Is oxygenation failing?
- Is ventilation failing?
- Is mental status unsafe?
- Is aspiration risk high?
- Is this a physiologically difficult airway?
- Is this an anatomically difficult airway?
- What is my backup plan?
- Who needs to be in the room?

## High-Risk Airway Contexts

- severe hypoxia
- shock
- metabolic acidosis
- severe asthma/COPD
- massive GI bleed
- trauma
- pregnancy
- pediatrics
- obesity
- facial/neck pathology
- toxicology/overdose
- agitation/delirium
- airway swelling/burns

## Post-Intubation Risk

After airway intervention, residents must anticipate:

- hypotension
- worsening hypoxia
- ventilation mismatch
- tube displacement
- pneumothorax
- aspiration
- need for sedation/analgesia strategy under local protocol
- ventilator mismatch
- missed original diagnosis

## Decision Points

- call anesthesia/ICU/senior early when high risk
- prepare team roles
- anticipate hemodynamic collapse
- plan post-airway reassessment
- confirm disposition

## Cognitive Traps

- focusing only on laryngoscopy, not physiology
- intubating severe metabolic acidosis without planning ventilation consequences
- underestimating post-intubation hypotension
- not calling help early
- no backup plan

## Exam Trap

Airway questions may test physiologic risk rather than equipment choice.

## Arabic Pearl

الأنبوب لا ينهي الخطر؛ أحيانًا يبدأ الخطر الحقيقي بعده.

# Respiratory Failure & Ventilator Thinking

## Purpose

Help residents think beyond “dyspnea” into oxygenation, ventilation, and work of breathing.

## Why It Matters

Different respiratory failures require different priorities and consultation.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Classify:

- oxygenation problem
- ventilation problem
- airway problem
- work-of-breathing failure
- mixed failure

Ask:

- Can the patient protect airway?
- Is oxygenation failing?
- Is CO2/ventilation likely failing?
- Is fatigue present?
- Is noninvasive support appropriate under local policy?
- Is invasive ventilation likely?
- Is shock or altered mental status present?

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- severe asthma/COPD
- pneumonia/sepsis
- pulmonary edema
- PE
- pneumothorax
- ARDS
- toxicologic respiratory depression
- neuromuscular weakness
- anaphylaxis
- metabolic acidosis driving respiratory compensation

## Ventilator Thinking

Resident should understand concepts:

- oxygenation vs ventilation
- lung-protective mindset
- obstructive physiology
- metabolic acidosis ventilation demand
- sedation/comfort under local protocol
- alarms as patient-ventilator information
- post-intubation reassessment

No fixed settings should be presented in this general SEG page.

## Disposition

High-risk respiratory failure commonly needs:
- resus bed
- ICU
- respiratory/critical care consultation
- close reassessment
- transfer depending on resources

## Cognitive Traps

- treating hypoxia without diagnosing work of breathing
- using noninvasive support in unsafe mental status
- missing PE with clear chest
- missing metabolic acidosis
- ignoring fatigue

## Exam Trap

The key may be identifying impending respiratory failure before saturation collapses.

## Arabic Pearl

ضيق النفس ليس تشخيصًا؛ افصل بين الأكسجة والتهوية والتعب.

# Chest Pain, ACS & OMI Thinking

## Purpose

Give residents a risk-focused approach to chest pain and ischemia.

## Why It Matters

Chest pain decisions carry high risk: missed ACS, missed dissection, missed PE, unsafe discharge, delayed activation.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- Is the patient unstable?
- Is this ischemic until proven otherwise?
- Are there STEMI or STEMI-equivalent concerns?
- Does the ECG match symptoms?
- What dangerous non-ACS causes fit?
- What serial data are needed?
- What disposition risk remains after initial tests?

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- ACS/OMI
- aortic dissection
- pulmonary embolism
- pneumothorax
- pericarditis/tamponade
- myocarditis
- esophageal rupture
- severe arrhythmia
- pneumonia/sepsis

## Decision Points

- ECG interpretation and repeat ECGs when appropriate
- symptom timeline
- risk factors and atypical presentations
- troponin concept and serial testing according to local protocol
- consultant/cath lab discussion when indicated
- observation vs admission vs discharge risk

## Disposition Implications

Disposition depends on:
- symptoms
- ECG evolution
- biomarkers under local protocol
- risk features
- comorbidities
- alternative dangerous diagnoses
- response and reassessment

## Cognitive Traps

- normal first ECG = safe
- young patient = safe
- pleuritic pain = not cardiac
- reproducible pain = no ACS
- one negative test = discharge
- anchoring on reflux/anxiety

## Exam Trap

Board-style chest pain questions often test the next action when initial ECG is nondiagnostic but suspicion remains high.

## Arabic Pearl

ألم الصدر لا ينتهي بتخطيط واحد؛ ينتهي بتقييم خطر متكامل.

# ECG Danger & Advanced Pattern Recognition

## Purpose

Prepare residents for high-risk ECG pattern recognition.

## Why It Matters

Emergency ECG interpretation is time-sensitive and error-prone.

## Resident First-Pass ECG System

1. rate
2. rhythm
3. axis
4. intervals
5. QRS width
6. ST segment
7. T waves
8. QT
9. comparison with old ECG
10. match with symptoms

## Dangerous ECG Patterns

- STEMI and STEMI equivalents
- posterior MI
- RV MI
- Wellens
- De Winter
- hyperacute T waves
- left main/aVR concern
- wide complex tachycardia
- complete heart block
- Brugada/WPW/long QT in syncope
- hyperkalemia
- TCA/sodium channel blocker concern
- PE clues
- pericarditis vs MI uncertainty

## Decision Points

- repeat ECG
- consult cardiology/senior
- avoid false reassurance
- correlate with symptoms
- consider toxic/metabolic causes
- document uncertainty and escalation

## Cognitive Traps

- reading ECG without the patient
- over-relying on automated interpretation
- missing posterior MI
- mislabeling VT as SVT
- missing hyperkalemia/toxicology patterns
- dismissing syncope ECG abnormalities

## Exam Trap

The board often tests the “pattern that should not be missed” more than the full ECG interpretation.

## Arabic Pearl

التخطيط في الطوارئ ليس رسمًا للحفظ؛ هو إنذار خطر يجب ربطه بالمريض.

# POCUS for Resident Decision-Making

## Purpose

Define how residents should use POCUS as a decision-support tool.

## Why It Matters

POCUS can rapidly answer focused questions, but overconfidence can harm.

## Resident POCUS Questions

- Is there pericardial effusion/tamponade concern?
- Is the LV grossly poor or hyperdynamic?
- Is RV strain possible?
- Is there free fluid in trauma?
- Is there pneumothorax concern?
- Is there pulmonary edema pattern?
- Is there AAA concern?
- Is there DVT concern?
- Is IVC data meaningful in this context?

## Decision Points

- POCUS supports bedside decisions
- POCUS does not replace comprehensive imaging when needed
- image quality matters
- operator skill matters
- clinical context matters
- document limitations

## Dangerous Uses

- ruling out PE with POCUS alone
- ruling out dissection with limited scan
- overcalling tamponade without physiology
- over-relying on IVC
- missing poor windows
- using POCUS to delay definitive care

## Disposition Implications

POCUS may accelerate:
- resuscitation
- consultant call
- ICU/OR decisions
- further imaging
- transfer decisions

## Cognitive Trap

Believing a negative limited scan equals no disease.

## Exam Trap

POCUS questions often test what the scan can and cannot answer.

## Arabic Pearl

السونار السريري يجيب سؤالًا محددًا؛ لا يحل محل التفكير السريري.

# Sepsis & Severe Infection

## Purpose

Help residents manage suspected severe infection as a risk syndrome.

## Why It Matters

Sepsis is time-sensitive, but patients vary widely. Residents must identify danger, source, and disposition.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- Is the patient toxic or in shock?
- Is organ dysfunction suspected?
- What is the likely source?
- Is source control needed?
- What comorbidity increases risk?
- Is the patient immunocompromised?
- What disposition is safest?
- Who needs early consultation?

## Dangerous Sources

- pneumonia
- pyelonephritis/urosepsis
- meningitis/encephalitis
- abdominal sepsis
- necrotizing infection
- septic arthritis
- line/device infection
- postpartum infection
- febrile neutropenia
- pediatric serious infection

## Decision Points

- resuscitation priority
- empiric therapy according to local protocol
- cultures/labs/imaging according to local pathway
- source control
- ICU need
- reassessment after interventions
- consultant involvement

## Cognitive Traps

- waiting for high fever
- missing sepsis in elderly
- missing immunocompromised risk
- focusing on labs over appearance
- delaying source control discussion
- underestimating normal-looking early sepsis

## Exam Trap

Questions often test early recognition of severe infection and escalation rather than naming the organism.

## Arabic Pearl

الإنتان قرار خطر قبل أن يكون نتيجة مزرعة.

Give residents a structured approach to time-sensitive neurologic emergencies.

- focal deficit?
- seizure or postictal state?
- glucose/metabolic mimic?
- anticoagulant use?
- trauma?
- headache pattern?
- infection/meningitis concern?
- pregnancy/postpartum?
- disposition pathway?

- ischemic stroke
- intracranial hemorrhage
- subarachnoid hemorrhage
- status epilepticus
- meningitis/encephalitis
- cerebral venous thrombosis
- spinal cord compression

## Decision Points

- stroke pathway under local protocol

- neurologist/neurosurgeon consult

# Toxicology & Antidote Reasoning

## Purpose

Give residents a structured toxicology approach without antidote dosing.

## Why It Matters

Toxicology is high-risk because early appearance may be misleading and histories are incomplete.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- airway/breathing/circulation stable?
- toxidrome?
- substance/time/amount?
- intentional vs accidental?
- co-ingestions?
- sustained-release/long-acting?
- pediatric/pregnancy?
- ECG danger?
- glucose/temperature/mental status?
- poison center/toxicologist discussion?

## Toxidrome Categories

Recognize:
- opioid/sedative
- sympathomimetic
- anticholinergic
- cholinergic
- serotonin syndrome concern
- neuroleptic malignant syndrome concern
- withdrawal states
- caustic/chemical exposure
- carbon monoxide/cyanide/methemoglobinemia concepts

## Antidote Reasoning

For each antidote, residents should know conceptually:

- indication
- mechanism
- timing importance
- monitoring need
- complications
- consultation need
- local dosing protocol source

Do not include dosing in this general page.

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- acetaminophen toxicity
- salicylate toxicity
- TCA/sodium channel blocker toxicity
- opioid toxicity
- beta-blocker/CCB toxicity
- digoxin toxicity
- toxic alcohols
- CO poisoning
- organophosphate exposure
- cyanide/methemoglobinemia concern
- pediatric ingestion

## Cognitive Traps

- believing first history
- reassuring early asymptomatic patient
- missing ECG toxicity
- missing acetaminophen in mixed overdose
- delayed poison center call
- forgetting self-harm safety

## Exam Trap

Toxicology exams often test toxidrome recognition and antidote indication, not rare mechanisms alone.

## Arabic Pearl

في التسمم، القصة ناقصة حتى يثبت العكس، والتخطيط قد يكشف الخطر قبل الأعراض.

# Board / Exam Trap Framework

## Purpose

Help residents prepare for emergency medicine board-style reasoning without copying copyrighted questions.

## Source Use

Use Tintinalli, Rosen Q&A, and Rosh Rapid Review as conceptual foundations for:
- topic coverage
- high-yield differentials
- pattern recognition
- common traps
- emergency priorities

Do not copy questions, answer choices, images, tables, or explanations.

## Common Exam Patterns

### 1. “Most appropriate next step”

Usually tests:
- stabilization
- dangerous diagnosis
- consultation
- imaging choice
- disposition

### 2. “Do not miss”

Usually tests:
- atypical presentation
- red flag
- hidden complication
- contraindication

### 3. “Pattern recognition”

Usually tests:
- ECG
- toxidrome
- rash
- pediatric age clue
- trauma mechanism
- electrolyte pattern

### 4. “Safe disposition”

Usually tests:
- who cannot go home
- who needs observation
- who needs ICU/consult
- who needs reassessment

## Resident Study Method

For every topic, write:

- core emergency
- dangerous mimic
- first stabilization priority
- key diagnostic clue
- common exam trap
- disposition risk
- consultant trigger

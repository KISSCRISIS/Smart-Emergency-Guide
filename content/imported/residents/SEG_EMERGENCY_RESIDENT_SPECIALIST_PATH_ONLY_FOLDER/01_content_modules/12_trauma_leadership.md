# Trauma Leadership

## Purpose

Help residents lead trauma evaluation safely.

## Why It Matters

Trauma requires parallel processing: physiology, mechanism, anatomy, resources, and disposition.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- is the patient unstable?
- what is the mechanism?
- primary survey threats?
- hidden bleeding?
- head/spine concern?
- chest/abdomen/pelvis concern?
- anticoagulants?
- pregnancy?
- pediatric/elderly special risk?
- trauma team/OR/transfer need?

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- tension pneumothorax
- massive hemorrhage
- tamponade
- pelvic hemorrhage
- intracranial bleed
- spinal cord injury
- hollow viscus injury
- blunt aortic injury
- compartment syndrome
- nonaccidental injury/safeguarding

## Decision Points

- trauma activation/local pathway
- imaging vs immediate OR/resus priorities
- eFAST role and limits
- blood product concept under local protocol
- consultant involvement
- transfer need
- serial exam

## Cognitive Traps

- stable initially = safe
- visible injury distracts from internal injury
- normal eFAST = no injury
- elderly fall = minor
- anticoagulated head injury minimized
- not reassessing abdomen

## Exam Trap

Trauma questions often test primary survey priority and hidden injury.

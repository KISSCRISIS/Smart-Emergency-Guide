# Airway Strategy & Post-Intubation Risk

## Purpose

Give residents a high-level airway decision framework without procedural steps or drug doses.

## Why It Matters

The dangerous airway decision is not only “intubate or not,” but:
- why now?
- who should do it?
- what could go wrong?
- what happens after?

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- Is airway failure present or impending?
- Is oxygenation failing?
- Is ventilation failing?
- Is mental status unsafe?
- Is aspiration risk high?
- Is this a physiologically difficult airway?
- Is this an anatomically difficult airway?
- What is my backup plan?
- Who needs to be in the room?

## High-Risk Airway Contexts

- severe hypoxia
- shock
- metabolic acidosis
- severe asthma/COPD
- massive GI bleed
- trauma
- pregnancy
- pediatrics
- obesity
- facial/neck pathology
- toxicology/overdose
- agitation/delirium
- airway swelling/burns

## Post-Intubation Risk

After airway intervention, residents must anticipate:

- hypotension
- worsening hypoxia
- ventilation mismatch
- tube displacement
- pneumothorax
- aspiration
- need for sedation/analgesia strategy under local protocol
- ventilator mismatch
- missed original diagnosis

## Decision Points

- call anesthesia/ICU/senior early when high risk
- prepare team roles
- anticipate hemodynamic collapse
- plan post-airway reassessment
- confirm disposition

## Cognitive Traps

- focusing only on laryngoscopy, not physiology
- intubating severe metabolic acidosis without planning ventilation consequences
- underestimating post-intubation hypotension
- not calling help early
- no backup plan

## Exam Trap

Airway questions may test physiologic risk rather than equipment choice.

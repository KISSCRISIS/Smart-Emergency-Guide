# Behavioral, Agitation & Self-Harm Risk

## Purpose

Help residents manage behavioral emergencies while avoiding missed medical causes.

## Resident First-Pass Thinking

Ask:

- is the scene safe?
- is the patient medically unstable?
- delirium vs psychiatric illness?
- intoxication/withdrawal?
- hypoxia/glucose/sepsis/head injury?
- self-harm risk?
- violence risk?
- capacity?
- safeguarding?
- need security/psychiatry/social work?

## Dangerous Diagnoses Not To Miss

- delirium
- hypoglycemia
- hypoxia/hypercapnia
- sepsis
- intracranial pathology
- toxidrome/withdrawal
- serotonin syndrome/NMS concern
- self-harm/overdose
- psychosis with unsafe behavior

## Decision Points

- safety for staff/patient
- medical evaluation before psychiatric anchoring
- capacity and consent
- involuntary hold/local legal pathways
- observation and reassessment
- psychiatry consultation
- safe disposition

## Cognitive Traps

- “psych patient” label
- ignoring abnormal vitals
- no collateral history
- letting high-risk self-harm leave
- not documenting risk assessment
- underestimating intoxication

## Exam Trap

Behavioral emergency questions often hide medical causes or safety/legal disposition issues.

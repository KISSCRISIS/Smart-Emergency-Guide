# Jordan Medical Student Roadmap

## Learning Goal

Understand how the Medical Students Path fits the Jordanian public medical education environment.

## Jordan Context

Jordanian medical students in public universities commonly move through:

- preclinical sciences
- integrated body systems
- introductory clinical skills
- clinical rotations
- hospital and primary-care exposure
- elective or field training
- transition toward internship and residency pathways

Different universities may organize years and courses differently, so SEG should not claim to be an official curriculum.

## SEG Student Stages

### Stage 1 — Preclinical Foundation

For years focused on:
- anatomy
- physiology
- biochemistry
- pathology
- microbiology
- pharmacology
- basic systems integration

SEG focus:
- connect systems to symptoms
- learn red-flag vocabulary
- basic clinical orientation
- avoid memorization without clinical meaning

### Stage 2 — Clinical Entry

For first clinical exposure and early clerkships.

SEG focus:
- history
- exam
- OSCE performance
- patient communication
- case presentation
- supervised clinical reasoning

### Stage 3 — Senior Clinical / Internship Bridge

For students near graduation.

SEG focus:
- emergency recognition
- ED observation
- handover
- patient safety
- referral thinking
- internship readiness

## What Students Should Not Do

Students should not:
- independently prescribe
- independently discharge
- perform procedures without supervision
- give medication orders
- communicate final decisions without supervisor approval
- interpret high-risk ECGs alone
- reassure high-risk patients alone

## Arabic Pearl

المسار لا يستبدل خطة الجامعة؛ هو جسر يساعدك تفهم كيف تتحول المعلومة إلى قرار آمن.

# Preclinical-to-Clinical Bridge

## Learning Goal

Help students connect basic sciences with clinical presentations.

## Why It Matters

Many students study systems separately, then feel lost when patients present with symptoms instead of textbook chapter titles.

## How to Think

Convert basic science into clinical questions:

### Cardiovascular

Basic science:
- heart pump
- coronary circulation
- conduction system
- blood pressure regulation

Clinical symptoms:
- chest pain
- dyspnea
- syncope
- palpitations
- edema

Danger patterns:
- ACS
- arrhythmia
- shock
- heart failure

### Respiratory

Basic science:
- oxygenation
- ventilation
- airway resistance
- gas exchange

Clinical symptoms:
- dyspnea
- wheeze
- cough
- hypoxia
- chest tightness

Danger patterns:
- severe asthma/COPD
- pneumonia/sepsis
- PE
- pneumothorax
- respiratory failure

### Neurology

Basic science:
- motor pathways
- cranial nerves
- consciousness
- seizures

Clinical symptoms:
- weakness
- confusion
- seizure
- headache
- dizziness

Danger patterns:
- stroke
- intracranial bleed
- meningitis
- status seizure
- raised intracranial pressure

## Student Exercise

For every disease you study, write:

- common symptom
- dangerous symptom
- exam clue
- first test often discussed
- red flag
- what must be reported to supervisor

## Common Student Trap

Learning mechanisms without knowing what the patient looks like.

## Arabic Pearl

اربط كل جهاز بسؤال: كيف سيظهر هذا المرض على المريض أمامي؟

# Hospital & MOH Clinical Environment

## Learning Goal

Prepare students to behave safely in hospitals, Ministry of Health settings, university hospitals, and primary-care/community rotations.

## Why It Matters

Jordanian students may train across:
- university hospitals
- Ministry of Health hospitals
- primary health centers
- Royal Medical Services or partner clinical sites
- community and family medicine settings

The student must understand the environment before trying to act.

## Student Role

Students may:
- observe
- take supervised histories
- perform supervised examinations
- present to doctors
- follow ward/clinic/ED workflow
- learn documentation standards
- ask questions respectfully
- recognize red flags and report them

Students must not:
- make independent decisions
- give orders
- promise treatment
- discuss final plans without supervisor
- use patient data outside rules
- take photos or recordings without clear permission/policy

## In Emergency Areas

Students should focus on:

- patient appearance
- vital signs
- ABCDE thinking
- red flags
- what the team is prioritizing
- communication and handover
- how decisions are escalated

## In Primary Care / MOH Settings

Students should focus on:

- common complaints
- chronic disease follow-up
- preventive care
- vaccination/public health context
- referral decisions
- safety-netting
- documentation

## Common Student Trap

Trying to impress by acting beyond your role.

## Arabic Pearl

في المستشفى، السلامة قبل الحماس؛ تعلّم دورك وحدودك أولًا.

# Professionalism, Consent & Patient Safety

## Learning Goal

Teach students safe behavior around patients, families, staff, and confidential information.

## Core Principles

- introduce yourself as a medical student
- ask permission before history or examination
- respect privacy and dignity
- use a chaperone when appropriate according to local rules
- do not expose more than needed
- do not discuss patients in public spaces
- do not photograph patient data
- do not post clinical material online
- report concerns to supervisors
- ask if unsure

## Consent Language

Useful phrase:

“Hello, I am a medical student working with the team. Is it okay if I ask you some questions and examine you under supervision?”

## Patient Safety

Report immediately if you notice:

- patient deterioration
- abnormal vital signs
- severe pain
- confusion
- breathing difficulty
- fall risk
- medication allergy concern
- wrong patient/label concern
- infection control issue
- safeguarding concern

## Common Student Trap

Thinking professionalism means only being polite. It also means protecting privacy, safety, and trust.

## Arabic Pearl

عرّف نفسك كطالب طب دائمًا؛ الصراحة جزء من الأمانة المهنية.

# History Taking Framework

## Learning Goal

Teach a student-friendly structure for clinical history.

## Core Structure

1. Patient identification
2. Presenting complaint
3. History of presenting complaint
4. Associated symptoms
5. Red flags
6. Past medical history
7. Medications
8. Allergies
9. Family history
10. Social history
11. Systems review
12. Summary and concern

## HPI Tools

Use:
- onset
- duration
- progression
- location
- character
- severity
- triggers
- relieving factors
- associated symptoms
- previous episodes
- impact on function
- red flags

## Emergency Red-Flag Questions

Always consider asking about:

- chest pain
- shortness of breath
- syncope
- fever with deterioration
- confusion
- weakness/numbness/speech problems
- severe headache
- bleeding
- pregnancy possibility
- self-harm/overdose when relevant

## Student Presentation Summary

“One-sentence summary:  
This is a [age] [sex] with [main complaint] for [duration], associated with [key positives], without/with [red flags], background of [key PMH], currently concerning for [top differentials].”

## Common Student Trap

Taking a long history without knowing the main problem or red flags.

## Arabic Pearl

التاريخ المرضي الجيد ليس أسئلة كثيرة؛ هو أسئلة مرتبة تكشف الخطر.

# Physical Examination Framework

## Learning Goal

Teach students how to examine safely and present findings.

## Before Exam

- wash hands
- introduce yourself
- confirm consent
- ensure privacy
- expose only needed area
- ask about pain
- use chaperone when appropriate
- position patient safely

## General Inspection

Look for:

- distress
- breathing effort
- color
- sweating
- confusion
- pain behavior
- hydration
- mobility
- lines/tubes/devices
- rash/bruising/wounds

## Vitals Matter

Students should recognize that vital signs are not numbers to copy. They are clinical clues.

Danger if vitals suggest:
- shock
- respiratory distress
- fever with toxicity
- severe pain
- altered mental status
- hypoxia according to local policy

## System Exam

For each system:

- inspect
- palpate
- percuss when relevant
- auscultate when relevant
- assess function
- compare sides
- report abnormal findings

## What To Report Immediately

- severe distress
- abnormal consciousness
- breathing difficulty
- cyanosis
- hypotension/shock appearance
- new neurologic deficit
- severe abdominal tenderness/peritonism
- non-blanching rash
- signs of abuse/safeguarding concern

## Common Student Trap

Performing a full textbook exam when the patient needs urgent senior attention.

## Arabic Pearl

الفحص السريري يبدأ من لحظة رؤية المريض، وليس من لحظة لمس السماعة.

# Vitals & ABCDE for Students

## Learning Goal

Teach students a safe recognition framework, not independent resuscitation.

## ABCDE Meaning

### A — Airway

Look for:
- speaking ability
- obstruction sounds
- vomiting/secretions
- reduced consciousness

### B — Breathing

Look for:
- work of breathing
- respiratory rate pattern
- cyanosis
- chest movement
- wheeze/silent chest
- oxygenation if available

### C — Circulation

Look for:
- color
- sweating
- pulse quality
- capillary refill context
- bleeding
- shock appearance

### D — Disability

Look for:
- consciousness
- glucose concern
- pupils
- seizure
- focal deficits

### E — Exposure

Look for:
- rash
- trauma
- temperature
- hidden bleeding
- infection signs

## Student Role

Students should:
- recognize ABCDE danger
- call the team/supervisor
- communicate findings clearly
- observe interventions
- document only under rules

Students should not:
- run resuscitation alone
- give medications
- perform procedures without supervision

## Common Student Trap

Trying to name the final diagnosis before checking if the patient is unstable.

## Arabic Pearl

قبل التشخيص التفصيلي اسأل: هل المريض آمن الآن؟

# Red Flags Every Medical Student Must Know

## Learning Goal

Give students a compact safety list across common presentations.

## Universal Red Flags

Report urgently if you encounter:

- airway concern
- severe shortness of breath
- cyanosis
- shock appearance
- severe chest pain
- syncope with ongoing symptoms
- reduced consciousness
- new neurologic deficit
- new seizure
- severe sudden headache
- severe abdominal pain with collapse or peritonism
- major trauma
- suspected sepsis
- suspected anaphylaxis
- pregnancy/postpartum danger symptoms
- toxic child appearance
- self-harm or overdose
- rapidly worsening symptoms

## Red Flags by Complaint

### Chest pain

- sweating
- dyspnea
- syncope
- radiation
- abnormal ECG
- ongoing severe pain

### Dyspnea

- cannot speak
- exhaustion
- silent chest
- cyanosis
- confusion
- chest pain/syncope

### Headache

- thunderclap
- fever/neck stiffness
- neuro deficit
- seizure
- pregnancy/postpartum
- cancer/immunosuppression

### Abdominal pain

- peritonism
- shock
- pregnancy
- GI bleeding
- severe pain out of proportion
- persistent vomiting

## Common Student Trap

Waiting until you are certain before calling for help.

## Arabic Pearl

في التدريب السريري، الشك بالخطر يكفي لتبلّغ الطبيب المسؤول.

# OSCE Survival for Medical Students

## Learning Goal

Teach students a safe, repeatable OSCE structure.

## Universal OSCE Opening

- wash hands
- introduce yourself
- confirm patient identity if appropriate
- explain task
- ask consent
- ensure privacy
- ask about pain
- position patient
- expose appropriately
- thank patient
- summarize findings
- mention red flags/escalation

## History OSCE

Structure:
- presenting complaint
- HPI
- associated symptoms
- red flags
- PMH
- medications
- allergies
- family/social history
- systems review
- summary

## Examination OSCE

Structure:
- inspection
- focused exam
- relevant special tests
- function
- finish with what you would do next

## Emergency OSCE

Start with:
- ABCDE
- call for help
- monitor/vitals
- focused danger recognition
- communicate clearly

## Common OSCE Traps

- forgetting consent
- no hand hygiene
- no pain question
- no red flags
- no summary
- over-talking and under-structuring
- pretending to do actions not allowed

## Arabic Pearl

# Case Presentation & Oral Handover

## Learning Goal

Help students present patients clearly.

## One-Minute Presentation

Use:

1. patient identity and setting
2. chief complaint and duration
3. key positives
4. key negatives/red flags
5. relevant background
6. exam/vitals
7. top differential
8. what you are worried about
9. what is pending

## Example Structure

“This is a [age]-year-old [sex] presenting with [complaint] for [duration]. The concerning features are [red flags]. Relevant background includes [PMH/meds]. On exam, [key findings]. I am most concerned about [differential], and I would like senior review because [reason].”

## Handover Priorities

Include:
- unstable features
- pending tests
- required reassessment
- safety issues
- escalation plan

## Common Student Trap

Presenting every detail equally without highlighting danger.

## Arabic Pearl

العرض السريري الجيد لا يروي القصة كاملة فقط؛ يوضح ما الذي يقلقك.

# Clinical Reasoning Basics

## Learning Goal

Teach students how to move from symptom to differential safely.

## The Three-Layer Differential

### 1. Most dangerous

What kills or disables if missed?

### 2. Most likely

What fits best with the story?

### 3. Must confirm/exclude

What tests/exam findings would change the plan?

## Illness Script

For each disease, know:

- who gets it
- how it presents
- key risk factors
- red flags
- exam clues
- key tests
- common mimics
- emergency consequence if missed

## Student Reasoning Phrase

“My leading diagnosis is [...], but I am also worried about [...] because [...].”

## Cognitive Errors

Avoid:
- anchoring
- premature closure
- ignoring vital signs
- ignoring patient appearance
- assuming young patients are safe
- dismissing caregiver concern

## Common Student Trap

Choosing the most likely diagnosis before considering the dangerous one.

## Arabic Pearl

ابدأ بالتفريق بين: الأكثر احتمالًا، والأكثر خطورة إن فاتك.

# Labs, ECG & Imaging Basics for Students

## Learning Goal

Teach students how to interpret tests in clinical context.

## Core Principle

Tests support clinical reasoning. They do not replace the patient.

## Labs

Students should understand:
- why the test was ordered
- what abnormality would be dangerous
- whether the result fits the patient
- what needs urgent reporting

Common high-yield labs:
- CBC
- electrolytes
- renal function
- liver tests
- glucose
- blood gas concept
- inflammatory markers
- pregnancy test when relevant
- troponin concept
- urinalysis concept

## ECG

Student focus:
- rate
- rhythm
- QRS width
- obvious ST/T danger
- heart block concern
- hyperkalemia/toxicology concern
- match ECG with symptoms
- report uncertainty

Do not overclaim ECG interpretation.

## Imaging

Student focus:
- why imaging is ordered
- what emergency question it answers
- limitations
- clinical correlation
- do not reassure based only on one test if symptoms are high-risk

## Common Student Trap

Interpreting tests without knowing the clinical question.

## Arabic Pearl

اسأل دائمًا: ما السؤال السريري الذي يجيب عنه هذا الفحص؟

# Medication Safety for Medical Students

## Learning Goal

Teach students medication awareness without prescribing.

## Student Role

Students may learn:
- medication class
- indication
- contraindication concept
- adverse effects
- allergy checking
- monitoring concept
- patient-specific risk

Students must not:
- prescribe independently
- suggest doses to patients
- give medication orders
- administer medications unless explicitly allowed and supervised
- change medication plans

## High-Risk Medication Groups

Know these as high-risk:
- opioids
- sedatives
- anticoagulants
- insulin/glucose-changing therapy
- potassium/electrolytes
- vasopressors/inotropes
- antiarrhythmics
- antibiotics in severe infection
- antidotes/toxicology medications

## Questions Students Should Ask

- Why is this medication used?
- What risk does this patient have?
- What should be monitored?
- What allergy matters?
- What could go wrong?
- Who confirms the plan?

## Common Student Trap

Thinking medication learning means memorizing doses only.

## Arabic Pearl

طالب الطب يتعلم لماذا الدواء خطير أو مفيد قبل أن يتعلم كيف يكتبه.

Teach students to recognize urgent mental health safety concerns.

## Red Flags

Report immediately if:
- suicidal thoughts with plan/intent
- recent self-harm
- overdose
- psychosis with unsafe behavior
- severe agitation
- risk to others
- unsafe home situation
- intoxication plus self-harm risk
- safeguarding concern
- inability to care for self

Students may:
- listen respectfully
- ask direct safety questions under supervision
- report concerns immediately
- avoid judgment
- protect privacy

- promise secrecy

## Common Student Trap

Avoiding suicide questions because they feel uncomfortable.

# Research & Evidence-Based Medicine for Students

## Learning Goal

Help students develop evidence-based thinking.

## Why It Matters

Jordanian medical faculties increasingly emphasize research, quality, and evidence-based learning. Students should learn how to ask clinical questions and read evidence safely.

## Basic EBM Framework

Use PICO:

- Patient/problem
- Intervention/exposure
- Comparison
- Outcome

## Student Research Skills

- formulate question
- search literature
- read abstracts critically
- understand study design
- identify bias
- summarize evidence
- cite sources
- respect research ethics
- avoid plagiarism

## Clinical Evidence Habit

For any clinical question, ask:

- what is the patient problem?
- what evidence supports this?
- what are the limitations?
- does it apply to Jordan/local setting?
- what does local policy say?

## Common Student Trap

Copying information without judging quality or relevance.

## Arabic Pearl

البحث الطبي ليس تجميع مراجع؛ هو سؤال واضح ودليل مناسب وتفكير نقدي.

# Internship Readiness

## Learning Goal

Prepare senior students for safer transition into internship.

## Intern-Ready Skills

Before internship, a student should practice under supervision:

- focused history
- focused exam
- vital sign interpretation
- ABCDE recognition
- red flag reporting
- case presentation
- handover
- documentation basics
- medication safety thinking
- ECG danger recognition
- lab/imaging clinical context
- referral communication
- asking for help early

## First Intern Mindset

You do not need to know everything. You need to:

- recognize sick patients
- communicate clearly
- avoid unsafe solo decisions
- reassess
- document
- escalate early

## Student-to-Intern Checklist

- Can I present a case in one minute?
- Can I recognize shock appearance?
- Can I identify respiratory distress?
- Can I list chest pain red flags?
- Can I explain why altered mental status is dangerous?
- Can I call senior with a clear ISBAR?
- Can I avoid prescribing beyond competence?
- Can I document safety-netting?

## Common Student Trap

Thinking internship readiness means memorizing more facts, not practicing safer structure.

## Arabic Pearl

بداية الامتياز تحتاج نظام تفكير آمن أكثر من حاجتها للثقة الزائدة.

# Jordan Public Medical Schools Alignment

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help SEG connect the Medical Students Path to the shared realities of Jordanian public medical education without claiming to replace any university curriculum.

## Why It Matters

Jordanian medical students may study in different public universities, but they usually face similar clinical expectations: safe bedside behavior, history and examination, OSCE performance, supervised rotations, patient communication, and transition toward internship. SEG should support those shared skills while directing students back to their own official study plan.

## Public University Alignment Cards

### University of Jordan — School of Medicine

Use SEG to support clinical reasoning, communication, OSCE structure, and safe emergency recognition. Do not claim SEG is the UJ official plan. Always link students to the official School of Medicine study plan pages.

### Jordan University of Science and Technology — Faculty of Medicine

Use SEG as a companion for staged study across preclinical and clinical years. JUST publishes study plan and syllabus pages by year, so SEG should avoid duplicating them and instead provide practical student-facing structure.

### Mutah University — Faculty of Medicine

Use SEG to help students in the southern clinical environment connect core medicine to supervised bedside observation and patient safety.

### Hashemite University — Faculty of Medicine

Use SEG to reinforce the transition from biomedical sciences to clinical rotations, including medicine, surgery, pediatrics, OB/GYN, emergency medicine exposure, family medicine, and elective rotations where applicable.

### Yarmouk University — Faculty of Medicine

Use SEG to support year-by-year clinical preparation and OSCE readiness while keeping official syllabus requirements separate.

### Al-Balqa Applied University — Faculty of Medicine

Use SEG to strengthen clerkship performance, primary-care awareness, emergency exposure, case presentation, and supervised clinical reasoning.

## What SEG Should Do

- Show common competencies across Jordanian medical training.
- Provide student-safe red-flag recognition.
- Offer OSCE and clerkship tools.
- Link forward to the Intern/JMC path.
- Keep official curriculum links in a source map.

## What SEG Must Not Do

- Claim Ministry of Health endorsement.
- Claim equivalence to any university study plan.
- Copy university syllabi or exam questions.
- Provide unsupervised management instructions.
- Give medication doses or procedural protocols.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

A student may know a disease definition but fail the station by not introducing themselves, not asking permission, not checking patient safety, or not escalating red flags.

## Arabic Pearl

# Year-by-Year Jordan Medical Student Roadmap

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Give students a practical map from early basic sciences to senior clinical readiness and internship preparation.

## Year 1–2: Basic Sciences With Clinical Meaning

### Main Focus

- Anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, histology, and basic systems.
- Learning medical language in English while speaking to patients in Arabic.
- Building disciplined study habits.

### SEG Role

- Connect organ systems to symptoms.
- Introduce red-flag vocabulary.
- Teach safe observation language: “I need to inform my supervisor.”

### Common Mistake

Memorizing lists without knowing how the concept appears in a real patient.

## Year 3: Pathology, Pharmacology, Microbiology, and Clinical Bridge

### Main Focus

- Mechanisms of disease.
- Why signs and symptoms happen.
- Medication safety concepts without prescribing.

### SEG Role

- Teach clinical reasoning basics.
- Build a symptom-to-system map.
- Prepare for history, exam, labs, ECG, and imaging interpretation under supervision.

## Year 4: First Real Clinical Exposure

### Main Focus

- Professional behavior on wards and clinics.
- History, examination, case presentation, and OSCE.
- Understanding hospital workflow.

### SEG Role

- Clerkship survival maps.
- Patient phrases in Arabic and English.
- Safe handover and presentation templates.

## Year 5: Major Clerkships

### Main Focus

- Medicine, surgery, pediatrics, OB/GYN, and specialty rotations.
- Recognizing unstable patients.
- Learning what to report urgently.

### SEG Role

- Clerkship maps by specialty.
- Red flags and common pitfalls.
- OSCE station bank.

## Year 6: Senior Student and Internship Bridge

### Main Focus

- Clinical maturity.
- Internship readiness.
- Emergency recognition and escalation.
- JMC-style thinking foundation.

### SEG Role

- ED observation logbook.
- Student-to-intern bridge.
- Safe communication, documentation, and escalation.

## What Students Should Track Each Year

- Cases observed.
- Histories taken under supervision.
- Examinations observed or performed under supervision.
- Presentations given.
- Feedback received.
- Red flags encountered.
- Personal gaps to review.

# Internal Medicine Clerkship Map

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help students survive internal medicine rotations by focusing on structured thinking, safe observation, and clear case presentation.

## Why It Matters in Jordanian Training

Internal medicine exposure may occur in university hospitals, Ministry of Health hospitals, Royal Medical Services hospitals, and outpatient clinics depending on university agreements. Students need a consistent approach even when the clinical site changes.

## What the Student Should Know

- How to present chronic illness clearly.
- How to recognize acute deterioration.
- How to connect symptoms to systems.
- Basic lab interpretation patterns.
- Difference between stable ward review and urgent senior review.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Take complete histories.
- Perform focused examinations.
- Prepare problem lists.
- Present data in order: patient, complaint, vitals, exam, labs, impression, concerns.
- Ask about medications, allergies, comorbidities, and follow-up.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Change medications.
- Reassure high-risk symptoms.
- Interpret critical labs without senior review.
- Decide discharge or admission.

## High-Yield Presentations

- Chest pain.
- Shortness of breath.
- Fever.
- Confusion.
- Syncope.
- Abdominal pain.
- Weakness.
- Hyperglycemia or hypoglycemia symptoms.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Failing to state the patient is unstable when abnormal vitals are present. The examiner often wants escalation, not a long differential.

## Arabic Pearl

في الباطني، الترتيب أهم من كثرة الكلام: الشكوى، العلامات الحيوية، الفحص، التحاليل، ثم الخطر.

# Surgery Clerkship Map

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Train students to observe surgical patients safely, recognize deterioration, and present focused surgical problems.

## Why It Matters

Surgery rotations expose students to wards, clinics, emergency surgical referrals, operating rooms, and postoperative care. The student role is not to operate or decide management, but to recognize risk and communicate clearly.

## What the Student Should Know

- Acute abdomen red flags.
- Preoperative and postoperative basics.
- Wound observation language.
- Pain, fever, vomiting, bleeding, and sepsis concern after surgery.
- Difference between observation and intervention.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Present surgical history in time order.
- Ask about onset, progression, vomiting, bowel symptoms, urinary symptoms, fever, and prior surgery.
- Observe abdominal examination technique.
- Learn sterile behavior and OR etiquette.
- Document what was observed, not what was assumed.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Remove drains, sutures, or dressings unless directly supervised.
- Give procedural consent independently.
- Reassure severe abdominal pain.
- Interpret postoperative complications alone.

## Red Flags

- Rigid abdomen.
- Shock signs.
- Persistent vomiting with dehydration.
- Postoperative fever with tachycardia.
- Wound bleeding or dehiscence.
- Testicular pain.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Jumping to a diagnosis before assessing stability and surgical red flags.

## Arabic Pearl

في الجراحة، لا تبحث عن اسم المرض فقط؛ ابحث عن الخطر الذي يحتاج إبلاغًا سريعًا.

# Pediatrics Clerkship Map

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help students approach children safely by focusing on caregiver history, hydration, breathing, fever red flags, and escalation.

## Why It Matters

Pediatrics requires a different mindset: the history often comes from a caregiver, the examination must be gentle, and small changes in appearance, feeding, hydration, or breathing may matter.

## What the Student Should Know

- Child appearance matters: alertness, work of breathing, color, hydration.
- Fever in young infants is high risk.
- Poor feeding and lethargy are danger signs.
- Parent/caregiver communication must be respectful and clear.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Ask age, weight if available, immunization status, feeding, urine output, vomiting, diarrhea, fever duration, medications already given, and chronic disease.
- Observe pediatric respiratory effort.
- Present the child and caregiver concerns clearly.
- Ask a senior early when the child looks unwell.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Reassure an ill-looking child.
- Give medication advice or dosing.
- Decide discharge.
- Ignore caregiver concern.

## Red Flags

- Lethargy.
- Cyanosis.
- Severe work of breathing.
- Dehydration signs.
- Seizure.
- Non-blanching rash.
- Fever in very young infants.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Forgetting to ask feeding and urine output in a sick child.

## Arabic Pearl

في الأطفال، شكل الطفل وسلوكه أهم من كلام طويل؛ الطفل الخامل يحتاج انتباهًا سريعًا.

# OB/GYN Clerkship Map

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Prepare students to recognize pregnancy and gynecologic red flags while respecting privacy, consent, and supervision.

## Why It Matters

OB/GYN rotations require professionalism, privacy, chaperone awareness, and careful communication. Students must know when symptoms may represent risk to the mother, fetus, or both.

## What the Student Should Know

- Always consider pregnancy status when relevant.
- Bleeding, severe pain, syncope, hypertension symptoms, fever, and reduced fetal movement require escalation.
- Consent and chaperone practice are essential.
- Sensitive history must be taken respectfully.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Introduce themselves clearly as a medical student.
- Ask permission before sensitive questions.
- Use respectful language.
- Present gestational age, symptoms, vitals, and red flags.
- Observe supervised examination only when appropriate.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Perform intimate examination without explicit supervision, consent, and chaperone process.
- Reassure pregnancy bleeding or severe pain.
- Give medication advice.
- Communicate final diagnosis alone.

## Red Flags

- Vaginal bleeding in pregnancy.
- Severe abdominal pain.
- Syncope.
- Severe headache or visual symptoms in pregnancy.
- Fever postpartum.
- Reduced fetal movement.
- Seizure.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Not asking pregnancy status or last menstrual period when clinically relevant.

## Arabic Pearl

في النساء والولادة، الاحترام والخصوصية جزء من العلامة، وليس مجرد أخلاق عامة.

# Emergency Medicine Exposure Map for Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Prepare students to observe the emergency department safely and understand what they should learn without acting beyond their role.

## Why It Matters

Emergency exposure teaches prioritization, communication, handover, and red-flag recognition. It should not push students into independent management.

## What the Student Should Know

- ED thinking starts with safety and stability.
- Abnormal vitals matter.
- ABCDE is a recognition framework for students.
- Handover must be brief and structured.
- Students should ask before touching equipment or entering high-risk situations.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Observe triage flow.
- Track time course of presentation.
- Practice one-minute case summaries.
- Identify red flags and tell a supervisor.
- Reflect after cases: What made this dangerous? What did the team do first?

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Triage patients.
- Give medication, discharge, or procedural advice.
- Interrupt resuscitation unless asked.
- Document decisions they did not make.

## ED Observation Checklist

- Chief complaint.
- Initial appearance.
- Vitals.
- Immediate concern.
- Team roles.
- Investigations ordered by the team.
- Disposition decision by clinician.
- Learning point.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Starting a long history while the patient is unstable.

## Arabic Pearl

في الطوارئ، الطالب الذكي لا يتصرف وحده؛ يلاحظ الخطر ويبلّغ بسرعة وباختصار.

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help students approach mental health presentations safely, respectfully, and without minimizing risk.

## Why It Matters

Mental health presentations can appear in clinics, wards, emergency departments, and primary care. Students must learn confidentiality, stigma-sensitive communication, and escalation for safety concerns.

## What the Student Should Know

- Self-harm and suicidal thoughts require immediate senior awareness.
- Agitation may be medical, psychiatric, substance-related, or environmental.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Introduce themselves calmly.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Decide capacity or discharge.

## Red Flags

- Suicidal intent.
- Homicidal intent.
- Severe agitation.
- Confusion or altered mental status.

Avoiding direct questions about self-harm because they feel uncomfortable.

# Orthopedics and Trauma Basics for Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Teach students to recognize musculoskeletal and trauma danger signs and present them clearly.

## Why It Matters

Students often see falls, fractures, wounds, back pain, and trauma mechanisms. Their job is to recognize danger, not perform independent reduction or procedures.

## What the Student Should Know

- Mechanism of injury matters.
- Neurovascular status is essential in limb injuries.
- Pain out of proportion can be dangerous.
- Back pain with neurologic or bladder symptoms is not routine.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Ask mechanism, time, pain location, function, numbness, bleeding, and previous injury.
- Observe distal pulses, color, movement, and sensation when supervised.
- Present mechanism + injury + neurovascular concern.
- Learn immobilization principles by observation.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Reduce dislocations or fractures.
- Remove helmets or spinal protection without instruction.
- Ignore anticoagulant use or head injury risk.
- Reassure severe back pain with neurologic symptoms.

## Red Flags

- Open fracture.
- Absent pulse or limb pallor.
- Severe swelling with pain out of proportion.
- Head injury with vomiting, confusion, seizure, or anticoagulant use.
- Back pain with weakness, saddle anesthesia, or urinary retention.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Forgetting neurovascular assessment in a limb injury.

## Arabic Pearl

في الكسور والإصابات، اسأل دائمًا: هل الدم والأعصاب بخير؟

# ENT and Ophthalmology Red Flags for Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Give students a simple red-flag framework for ENT and eye complaints without turning them into specialists.

## Why It Matters

ENT and eye complaints can look minor but occasionally threaten airway, vision, or intracranial spread. Students must know when to escalate.

## What the Student Should Know

### Eye Red Flags

- Sudden vision loss.
- Eye trauma.
- Severe eye pain.
- Chemical exposure.
- Red eye with photophobia or reduced vision.

### ENT Red Flags

- Airway symptoms.
- Stridor.
- Drooling with toxic appearance.
- Neck swelling.
- Severe headache with ear/sinus infection.
- Facial weakness.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Ask onset, trauma, pain, vision change, fever, swallowing difficulty, breathing difficulty, and immune risk.
- Avoid pressuring the eye.
- Report airway, vision, or neurologic concerns immediately.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Remove foreign bodies.
- Prescribe eye drops.
- Reassure vision loss.
- Examine aggressive airway-risk patients unsupervised.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Calling every red eye “conjunctivitis” without checking pain, photophobia, and visual change.

## Arabic Pearl

العين والأذن والأنف قد تبدو بسيطة، لكن فقدان النظر أو صعوبة التنفس لا ينتظر.

# Radiology Basics for Medical Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Teach students how to approach imaging safely as observers and learners, not as final interpreters.

## Why It Matters

Students see X-rays, CT, ultrasound reports, and imaging requests during rotations. They need structure, language, and humility.

## What the Student Should Know

- Imaging is interpreted in clinical context.
- The report matters, but the patient matters more.
- Some findings are time-sensitive.
- Radiation, pregnancy, contrast, and renal function are safety issues decided by clinicians.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Learn to identify patient name, date, modality, and body area.
- Ask why the image was requested.
- Compare with the clinical question.
- Present: “The imaging was requested because..., the report says..., clinically the concern is...”

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Give final imaging interpretation.
- Tell a patient their scan is normal.
- Decide imaging is unnecessary.
- Ignore discordance between patient condition and report.

## High-Yield Imaging Awareness

- Chest X-ray: pneumonia, pneumothorax, pulmonary edema patterns.
- Trauma imaging: mechanism and instability matter.
- CT head: altered mental status, trauma, neurologic deficit context.
- Ultrasound: operator and indication matter.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Describing an image without connecting it to the patient’s symptoms and stability.

## Arabic Pearl

الصورة لا تعالج المريض وحدها؛ اربطها دائمًا بالقصة والفحص والعلامات الحيوية.

# ICU and Anesthesia Exposure for Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Prepare students to observe critical care and anesthesia environments safely and professionally.

## Why It Matters

Students may rotate through ICU, OR, recovery areas, or anesthesia exposure. These settings teach airway, monitoring, physiology, teamwork, and safety—but they also carry high risk.

## What the Student Should Know

- Monitoring is continuous for a reason.
- Airway and ventilation are supervised skills.
- Sedation, analgesia, and vasopressors are high-risk medication areas.
- Infection prevention matters around lines, tubes, and devices.

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Observe monitors and ask what each parameter means.
- Learn how teams communicate during induction, intubation, transfer, and deterioration.
- Record learning points, not patient identifiers.
- Respect sterile fields and equipment boundaries.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Touch ventilator settings.
- Adjust infusions.
- Handle airway devices.
- Silence alarms without permission.
- Enter sterile fields unknowingly.

## Red Flags to Report

- Sudden desaturation.
- Hypotension.
- Agitation or decreased consciousness.
- Alarm with patient change.
- Line disconnection or bleeding.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Thinking ICU is only machines; it is physiology, teamwork, and patient safety.

## Arabic Pearl

في العناية والتخدير، لا تلمس شيئًا لا تعرفه؛ راقب، اسأل، وتعلم تحت إشراف.

# OSCE Station Bank for Jordanian Medical Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Provide reusable OSCE station templates that support Jordanian medical students across clinical years without copying any university exam.

## Station Format

Each station should include:

- Task.
- Student opening line.
- Required consent or permission.
- Key questions or actions.
- Red flags.
- What the examiner wants.
- Common failure.
- Arabic patient phrase.

## Station Templates

### 1. Chest Pain History

Task: Take a focused chest pain history.

Student opening: “Hello, I am a medical student. May I ask you some questions about your pain?”

Key items: onset, character, radiation, severity, duration, triggers, associated shortness of breath, sweating, syncope, risk factors, medications, allergies.

Red flags: ongoing severe pain, syncope, abnormal vitals, shortness of breath.

Common failure: asking risk factors before checking if the patient is unstable.

Arabic phrase: “متى بدأ الألم؟ وهل ينتقل إلى الكتف أو الفك؟”

### 2. Shortness of Breath History

Key items: onset, exertion, cough, fever, chest pain, wheeze, leg swelling, past asthma/COPD/heart disease.

Red flags: cyanosis, inability to speak, confusion, severe work of breathing.

Arabic phrase: “هل ضيق النفس بدأ فجأة أم تدريجيًا؟”

### 3. Abdominal Pain History

Key items: site, onset, migration, vomiting, bowel habits, urinary symptoms, fever, pregnancy possibility when relevant.

Red flags: rigid abdomen, shock, pregnancy bleeding, severe persistent pain.

Arabic phrase: “أين بدأ الوجع؟ وهل انتقل لمكان آخر؟”

### 4. Fever in a Child

Key items: age, duration, feeding, urine output, activity, rash, breathing, vomiting, immunization, medications given.

Red flags: lethargy, poor feeding, dehydration, seizure, breathing difficulty.

Arabic phrase: “هل الطفل يرضع أو يأكل كالمعتاد؟ كم مرة تبول اليوم؟”

### 5. Pregnancy Bleeding

Key items: gestational age, amount of bleeding, pain, dizziness, fetal movement if relevant, previous pregnancy history.

Red flags: syncope, severe pain, heavy bleeding, reduced fetal movement.

Arabic phrase: “كم شهر الحمل؟ وهل يوجد ألم أو دوخة مع النزيف؟”

### 6. Neurologic Weakness

Key items: exact time last known well, weakness, speech, face droop, vision, seizure, anticoagulants, glucose check by team.

Red flags: acute focal deficit, altered consciousness, severe headache.

Arabic phrase: “متى كانت آخر مرة كان فيها طبيعيًا تمامًا؟”

### 7. Mental Health Safety

Key items: mood, sleep, stressors, self-harm thoughts, plan, support, substances.

Red flags: active suicidal intent, plan, psychosis, severe agitation.

Arabic phrase: “هل خطرت ببالك أفكار أن تؤذي نفسك؟”

### 8. Consent for Examination

Key items: introduce role, explain exam, ask permission, offer chaperone when appropriate, respect refusal.

Common failure: proceeding without permission.

Arabic phrase: “أنا طالب طب، هل تسمح لي أن أفحصك تحت إشراف الطبيب؟”

### 9. Case Presentation

Task: Present a patient in one minute.

Structure: identity, complaint, duration, relevant background, vitals, key exam, concern, request for review.

Common failure: unstructured storytelling.

### 10. Data Interpretation

Task: Interpret basic labs or ECG context under supervision.

Expected behavior: state abnormality, connect to patient, ask for senior review if high risk.

Common failure: giving final diagnosis confidently without context.

## Arabic Pearl

الـ OSCE ليس حفظ جمل فقط؛ هو أمان، ترتيب، احترام، وتصعيد الخطر في الوقت المناسب.

# Clinical Logbook and Portfolio for Medical Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Give students a structured way to document learning across rotations without storing patient-identifiable information.

## Why It Matters

A logbook turns random clinical exposure into deliberate progress. It also helps students prepare for OSCE, oral presentations, and internship.

## Privacy Rule

Never record patient name, national number, phone number, address, exact date of birth, or identifiable story details.

## Case Log Template

- Date or week number.
- Rotation/site type: ward, clinic, ED, OR, ICU, primary care.
- Presentation observed.
- Student role: observed / took history / examined under supervision / presented / reflected.
- Key red flag.
- What the team did first.
- What I learned.
- What I need to review.
- Supervisor feedback.

## Skills Log

### Communication

- Introduced myself as a student.
- Asked permission.
- Used Arabic patient-friendly language.
- Summarized the case.

### Clinical Skills

- History under supervision.

# Arabic–English Bedside Communication for Medical Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help students translate medical structure into respectful Arabic bedside communication while keeping English academic terms available.

## Core Opening

English: “Hello, I am a medical student working with the team. May I ask you some questions?”

Arabic: “مرحبًا، أنا طالب طب مع الفريق الطبي. هل تسمح لي أسألك بعض الأسئلة؟”

## Permission and Role Boundaries

English: “I will discuss what you tell me with the supervising doctor.”

Arabic: “سأنقل ما تخبرني به للطبيب المسؤول.”

English: “I cannot make the final decision, but I will inform the doctor.”

Arabic: “لا أستطيع إعطاء القرار النهائي، لكن سأبلغ الطبيب المسؤول.”

## Pain History

- “Where is the pain?” / “أين مكان الألم؟”
- “When did it start?” / “متى بدأ؟”
- “Does it move anywhere?” / “هل ينتقل لمكان آخر؟”
- “What makes it worse or better?” / “ما الذي يزيده أو يخففه؟”

## Chest Pain

- “Do you feel short of breath?” / “هل لديك ضيق نفس؟”
- “Any sweating, nausea, or fainting?” / “هل يوجد تعرّق أو غثيان أو إغماء؟”
- “Does it go to the jaw, shoulder, or back?” / “هل ينتقل للفك أو الكتف أو الظهر؟”

## Shortness of Breath

- “Did it start suddenly or gradually?” / “هل بدأ فجأة أم تدريجيًا؟”
- “Can you speak full sentences?” / “هل تستطيع الكلام بجمل كاملة؟”
- “Do you have chest pain or fever?” / “هل يوجد ألم صدر أو حرارة؟”

## Abdominal Pain

- “Any vomiting or diarrhea?” / “هل يوجد استفراغ أو إسهال؟”
- “Any urinary symptoms?” / “هل يوجد حرقان بول أو تغير في البول؟”
- “Could you be pregnant?” / “هل يوجد احتمال حمل؟”

## Pediatrics Caregiver Phrases

- “Is the child feeding normally?” / “هل الطفل يرضع أو يأكل كالمعتاد؟”
- “How many wet diapers or urine times today?” / “كم مرة تبول اليوم؟”
- “Is the child less active than usual?” / “هل نشاطه أقل من المعتاد؟”

## Sensitive Questions

- “I need to ask some personal questions because they may affect your health.” / “أحتاج أسأل بعض الأسئلة الشخصية لأنها قد تؤثر على صحتك.”
- “Would you prefer a chaperone or another person to be present?” / “هل تفضل وجود مرافق أو شخص آخر أثناء الفحص؟”

## Asking for Senior Help

English: “This patient has red flags. Could you review them now?”

Arabic to colleague/supervisor: “المريض عنده علامات خطورة، ممكن تراجعه الآن؟”

## Arabic Pearl

الطالب الممتاز لا يترجم الكلمات فقط؛ يترجم الاحترام والوضوح وحدود دوره.

# Student-to-Intern Bridge in Jordan

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Prepare senior medical students for the mindset shift from supervised learner to new intern while keeping boundaries clear.

## What Changes After Graduation

A student mainly observes, presents, and learns under direct supervision. An intern may be expected to participate more actively in documentation, calls, follow-up, initial assessment, and team communication—but still needs supervision and local policy awareness.

## What Students Should Master Before Internship

- Introduce themselves clearly.
- Take a focused history.
- Perform basic examination under supervision.
- Interpret vital signs as normal or concerning.
- Present a case in one minute.
- Recognize red flags.
- Know when to call for help.
- Document accurately without inventing.

## Internship-Readiness Checklist

### Communication

- Can I explain my role to patients?
- Can I summarize a patient to a senior?
- Can I ask for help without hiding uncertainty?

### Clinical Safety

- Can I spot abnormal vitals?
- Can I recognize chest pain, dyspnea, altered mental status, sepsis concern, trauma, pregnancy bleeding, pediatric danger signs, and self-harm risk?

### Professionalism

- Do I respect consent and confidentiality?
- Do I avoid acting beyond my role?
- Do I ask before doing procedures?

## Link to SEG Intern/JMC Path

After finishing this student path, the learner should move to:

- Intern Overview.
- First Night Shift.
- ED Workflow.
- Handover.
- Airway Basics.
- Drug Essentials.
- Toxicology Essentials.
- Emergency Cases.
- JMC Practice.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

The student who pretends to know everything is unsafe. A safe student recognizes limits and escalates.

# MOH Hospital Workflow and Referral Observation for Students

> Safety boundary: This page is for supervised medical student learning. It is not an official Ministry of Health curriculum, not an official university syllabus, and not a treatment protocol.

## Learning Goal

Help students understand how patients move through Jordanian public healthcare environments and how a student can observe that process safely.

## Why It Matters

Jordanian students may encounter Ministry of Health hospitals, comprehensive health centers, primary health centers, university hospitals, Royal Medical Services sites, and private facilities. SEG should teach the student to understand workflow, referral logic, and communication without claiming operational authority.

## What the Student Should Observe

### Entry Point

- Emergency department.
- Outpatient clinic.
- Primary health center.
- Comprehensive health center.
- Referral from another facility.

### Initial Assessment

- Who receives the patient?
- What information is collected first?
- Are vital signs obtained?
- What makes the team act urgently?

### Referral Logic

- Why is this patient staying here or being referred?
- What information must travel with the patient?
- Who communicates with whom?

### Documentation

- What was observed?
- What was decided by the clinician?
- What follow-up or referral was arranged?

## What the Student Should Do Under Supervision

- Ask how the referral pathway works at that site.
- Learn the difference between clinic, ED, ward, ICU, and referral center.
- Practice concise case summaries.
- Understand that local policy controls operational decisions.

## What the Student Must Not Do Alone

- Decide referral destination.
- Promise admission or discharge.
- Give final instructions.
- Sign or write official referral decisions unless explicitly supervised and allowed.

## OSCE / Exam Trap

Forgetting that safe care is not only diagnosis—it is also communication, documentation, and correct escalation.

## Arabic Pearl

فهم النظام الصحي جزء من الطب؛ المريض لا يحتاج معلومة فقط، يحتاج طريقًا آمنًا داخل النظام.

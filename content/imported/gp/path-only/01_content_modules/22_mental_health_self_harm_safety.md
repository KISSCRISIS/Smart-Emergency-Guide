# Mental Health & Self-Harm Safety for GP

## Purpose

Help GP identify mental health presentations that need urgent emergency or crisis referral.

## High-risk presentations

- suicidal ideation with plan, intent, or means
- recent self-harm
- overdose or poisoning
- psychosis with unsafe behavior
- severe agitation
- severe depression with inability to function
- mania with risk-taking or lack of sleep
- intoxication with unsafe behavior
- safeguarding/domestic violence concern
- risk to others
- inability to care for self
- confused/agitated patient where medical cause is possible

## Red flags

Urgent referral/crisis escalation if:

- active suicidal plan or intent
- recent suicide attempt
- overdose/unknown ingestion
- access to lethal means
- command hallucinations or severe psychosis
- severe agitation or violence risk
- intoxication plus self-harm risk
- child/adolescent safeguarding risk
- domestic violence with immediate danger
- patient lacks safe supervision
- medical cause of behavior not excluded

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask directly and calmly:

- thoughts of self-harm
- plan
- intent
- access to means
- previous attempts
- substance use
- protective factors
- support/supervision
- psychotic symptoms
- medical symptoms or overdose

Check:

- medical stability
- intoxication
- injury
- overdose concern
- immediate safety
- support person availability

## Common GP traps

- Avoiding direct questions about suicide.
- Letting an unsafe patient leave alone.
- Assuming agitation is psychiatric before checking medical causes.
- Missing overdose in a distressed patient.

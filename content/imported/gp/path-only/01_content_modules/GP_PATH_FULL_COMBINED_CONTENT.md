# GP Emergency Red Flags

## Purpose

Teach a simple danger-first mindset for any patient in clinic, urgent care, telehealth, or home visit.

## Universal red flags

Urgent ED/senior escalation if the patient has:

- airway concern
- severe shortness of breath
- cyanosis
- shock appearance
- severe chest pain
- syncope with ongoing symptoms
- reduced consciousness
- new focal neurologic deficit
- new seizure
- severe sudden headache
- severe abdominal pain with peritonism or collapse
- major trauma
- suspected sepsis
- suspected anaphylaxis
- pregnancy/postpartum red flags
- pediatric toxic appearance
- self-harm or unsafe mental state
- rapidly worsening symptoms
- clinician concern that the patient is â€œnot rightâ€

## High-risk groups

Lower threshold for referral in:

- infants
- elderly/frail patients
- pregnancy/postpartum
- immunocompromised patients
- anticoagulated patients
- dialysis/advanced kidney disease
- significant cardiac disease
- diabetes with systemic illness
- cancer/chemotherapy
- recent surgery
- serious psychiatric risk

## GP action

- Pause routine clinic flow.
- Assess ABCDE.
- Decide if ED/ambulance is needed.
- Do not let unstable/high-risk patients self-drive.
- Give a clear referral reason.
- Document red flags and advice.

## Common GP trap

Reassuring because the patient arrived walking.

# Chest Pain in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP recognize chest pain that needs urgent ED assessment.

## Dangerous causes to consider

- acute coronary syndrome
- aortic dissection
- pulmonary embolism
- pneumothorax
- pericarditis/tamponade concern
- esophageal rupture
- pneumonia/sepsis
- arrhythmia presenting with chest discomfort

## Red flags

Urgent referral if chest pain is associated with:

- sweating
- shortness of breath
- syncope or near-syncope
- radiation to arm, jaw, back, or epigastrium
- tearing chest/back pain
- neurologic symptom
- hypotension or shock appearance
- persistent/worsening pain
- abnormal ECG if available
- known coronary disease
- diabetes/elderly with atypical symptoms
- pregnancy/postpartum with concerning symptoms
- stimulant use with severe symptoms or abnormal vitals

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- onset and progression
- character and radiation
- exertional component
- dyspnea/syncope/sweating/nausea
- cardiac history and risk factors
- PE risk factors
- dissection clues
- recent infection/pericarditis clues
- stimulant use
- pregnancy/postpartum status when relevant

Check:

- appearance
- vital signs if available
- heart and lung exam
- pulse symmetry if dissection concern
- ECG if available and it does not delay referral

## Communicate to ED

â€œI am referring a chest pain patient with high-risk features. Concern includes ACS/dissection/PE/pneumothorax/pericarditis. Current status is [...]. Onset was [...]. ECG is [.../not available].â€

## Common GP traps

- Calling indigestion benign too early.
- Assuming a normal ECG excludes danger.
- Missing atypical ACS in elderly/diabetic patients.
- Reassuring pleuritic pain without considering PE, pneumothorax, pericarditis, or pneumonia.

# Shortness of Breath in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP identify dyspnea that should not stay in primary care.

## Dangerous causes

- severe asthma/COPD exacerbation
- pneumonia with sepsis
- pulmonary embolism
- pulmonary edema/heart failure
- pneumothorax
- anaphylaxis
- metabolic acidosis
- ACS or arrhythmia
- airway obstruction
- toxic exposure

## Red flags

Urgent ED referral if:

- severe work of breathing
- cyanosis
- confusion or drowsiness
- inability to speak comfortably
- silent chest
- hypotension/shock appearance
- chest pain or syncope
- hemoptysis with dyspnea
- suspected PE
- suspected anaphylaxis
- deterioration despite initial clinic measures
- child with exhaustion, poor feeding, or altered behavior

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- sudden vs gradual onset
- chest pain
- wheeze/cough/fever
- PE risks
- heart failure history
- asthma/COPD history
- allergen/medication exposure
- toxic inhalation
- pregnancy/postpartum

Check:

- ability to speak
- work of breathing
- mental status
- color
- chest auscultation
- pulse/perfusion
- temperature

## Common GP traps

- Labeling dyspnea as anxiety before excluding danger.
- Treating wheeze as asthma only.
- Missing PE when chest exam is normal.
- Missing ACS presenting mainly as dyspnea.

# Headache & Neurologic Red Flags

## Purpose

Help GP separate common headache from dangerous headache or neurologic emergency.

## Dangerous causes

- subarachnoid hemorrhage
- meningitis/encephalitis
- stroke/TIA
- intracranial mass/raised pressure
- cerebral venous thrombosis
- temporal arteritis in appropriate patients
- acute glaucoma/eye emergency
- hypertensive emergency concern
- pregnancy/postpartum neurologic emergency

## Red flags

Urgent referral if:

- thunderclap headache
- worst headache of life
- new neurologic deficit
- seizure
- fever with neck stiffness or confusion
- reduced consciousness
- papilledema concern
- new headache in pregnancy/postpartum
- new headache with cancer/immunosuppression
- new headache in older patient
- headache after trauma, especially anticoagulated
- acute visual loss or painful red eye
- progressive worsening pattern

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- onset speed
- time to maximum intensity
- previous similar headaches
- fever/neck stiffness
- neurologic symptoms
- trauma
- pregnancy/postpartum
- anticoagulants
- cancer/immunosuppression
- visual symptoms

Check:

- mental status
- cranial nerves
- limb power/sensation
- speech
- gait if safe
- neck stiffness
- eye/red-flag visual signs

## Common GP traps

- Treating thunderclap headache as migraine.
- Ignoring fever/confusion with headache.
- Missing pregnancy/postpartum risk.
- Reassuring after temporary analgesic response.

# Fever / Sepsis Recognition in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP recognize serious infection early.

## Dangerous causes

- sepsis
- pneumonia with systemic illness
- meningitis
- pyelonephritis/urosepsis
- abdominal sepsis
- cellulitis/necrotizing infection concern
- infected device/line
- postpartum infection
- febrile neutropenia concern
- pediatric serious bacterial infection

## Red flags

Urgent ED referral if infection concern plus:

- confusion
- shock appearance
- severe breathlessness
- cyanosis
- reduced urine output
- non-blanching rash
- neck stiffness
- severe pain out of proportion
- immunocompromise
- chemotherapy/neutropenia concern
- pregnancy/postpartum
- very young infant
- elderly/frail deterioration
- persistent abnormal vitals
- inability to maintain oral intake with concerning appearance

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- source symptoms
- duration and progression
- rigors
- confusion/weakness
- urine output
- respiratory symptoms
- rash
- immunosuppression/chemotherapy
- pregnancy/postpartum
- recent surgery/procedure
- travel/exposure if relevant

Check:

- appearance
- mental status
- breathing
- circulation/perfusion
- rash
- focused source exam

## Common GP traps

- Waiting for fever to be high before worrying.
- Missing sepsis in elderly/immunocompromised patients.
- Treating infection as minor when the patient looks toxic.
- Delaying referral for labs when the clinical picture is concerning.

# Pregnancy & Postpartum Red Flags

## Purpose

Help GP recognize high-risk pregnancy/postpartum presentations.

- ectopic pregnancy
- miscarriage with instability

- postpartum hemorrhage
- stroke/neurologic emergency

- pregnancy status/possibility

- domestic safety

- appearance
- perfusion
- mental status
- focused abdomen/pelvis context
- vitals if available
- urine/pregnancy test if relevant and available, without delaying referral if unstable

## Common GP traps

- Forgetting ectopic pregnancy.
- Underestimating postpartum risk.

# ECG Danger Recognition for GP

## Purpose

Teach GP-level ECG danger recognition, not advanced interpretation.

## ECG findings needing urgent ED/senior review

- ST elevation or STEMI-equivalent concern
- new ischemic changes with symptoms
- wide complex tachycardia
- complete heart block
- severe bradycardia with symptoms
- rapid irregular rhythm with instability
- long QT with syncope/seizure concern
- Brugada/WPW pattern with syncope or palpitations
- hyperkalemia pattern concern
- toxicology-related ECG abnormality concern

## GP ECG principles

- ECG must match the patient, not be read alone.
- A normal ECG does not automatically make chest pain safe.
- Compare old ECG if available.
- If uncertain and the patient is symptomatic, escalate.
- Do not delay transfer for perfect interpretation.

## Common GP traps

- Reassuring chest pain after one ECG.
- Calling wide complex tachycardia benign.
- Missing heart block in dizziness/syncope.
- Ignoring ECG changes in overdose.

# Mental Health & Self-Harm Safety for GP

Help GP identify mental health presentations that need urgent emergency or crisis referral.

- suicidal ideation with plan, intent, or means

- overdose concern

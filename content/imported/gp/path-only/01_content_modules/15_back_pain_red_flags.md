# Back Pain Red Flags for GP

## Purpose

Help GP recognize back pain that may represent neurologic, vascular, infectious, malignant, or traumatic danger.

## Dangerous causes

- cauda equina syndrome
- spinal cord compression
- spinal infection
- malignancy/metastasis
- fracture
- AAA in appropriate patient
- renal/abdominal referred pain
- inflammatory or systemic disease when severe/systemic

## Red flags

Urgent referral if:

- urinary retention or new incontinence
- saddle anesthesia
- progressive leg weakness
- bilateral neurologic symptoms
- fever with spinal pain
- immunosuppression or IV drug use with back pain
- cancer history with new severe pain
- significant trauma
- elderly/frail with trauma or severe new pain
- severe abdominal/back pain with collapse or vascular risk
- night pain with systemic symptoms
- unexplained weight loss with concerning pattern

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- bladder/bowel symptoms
- saddle sensation
- leg weakness/numbness
- trauma
- fever
- cancer history
- immunosuppression
- IV drug use
- steroid use
- vascular symptoms

Check:

- gait if safe
- leg power/sensation/reflex context
- spinal tenderness
- abdominal/vascular context when indicated
- fever/toxic appearance

## Common GP traps

- Not asking bladder/bowel and saddle symptoms.
- Treating new weakness as simple sciatica.
- Missing infection/malignancy risk.
- Reassuring severe back pain in older patient without red-flag review.

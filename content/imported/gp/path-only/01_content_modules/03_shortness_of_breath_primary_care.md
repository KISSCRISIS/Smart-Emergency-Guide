# Shortness of Breath in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP identify dyspnea that should not stay in primary care.

## Dangerous causes

- severe asthma/COPD exacerbation
- pneumonia with sepsis
- pulmonary embolism
- pulmonary edema/heart failure
- pneumothorax
- anaphylaxis
- metabolic acidosis
- ACS or arrhythmia
- airway obstruction
- toxic exposure

## Red flags

Urgent ED referral if:

- severe work of breathing
- cyanosis
- confusion or drowsiness
- inability to speak comfortably
- silent chest
- hypotension/shock appearance
- chest pain or syncope
- hemoptysis with dyspnea
- suspected PE
- suspected anaphylaxis
- deterioration despite initial clinic measures
- child with exhaustion, poor feeding, or altered behavior

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- sudden vs gradual onset
- chest pain
- wheeze/cough/fever
- PE risks
- heart failure history
- asthma/COPD history
- allergen/medication exposure
- toxic inhalation
- pregnancy/postpartum

Check:

- ability to speak
- work of breathing
- mental status
- color
- chest auscultation
- pulse/perfusion
- temperature

## Common GP traps

- Labeling dyspnea as anxiety before excluding danger.
- Treating wheeze as asthma only.
- Missing PE when chest exam is normal.
- Missing ACS presenting mainly as dyspnea.

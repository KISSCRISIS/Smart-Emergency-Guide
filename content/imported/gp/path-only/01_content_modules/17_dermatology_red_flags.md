# Dermatology Red Flags for GP

## Purpose

Help GP recognize skin presentations that may represent emergency infection, allergy, bleeding, or systemic illness.

## Dangerous causes

- necrotizing soft tissue infection
- meningococcemia/sepsis rash concern
- anaphylaxis/angioedema
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome/toxic epidermal necrolysis concern
- severe cellulitis with systemic illness
- purpura/petechiae with fever
- abscess with sepsis or high-risk patient
- shingles involving eye/face concern
- burns/chemical injury

## Red flags

Urgent referral if:

- non-blanching rash with fever or toxic appearance
- rapidly spreading redness with severe pain
- pain out of proportion
- bullae, skin sloughing, mucosal involvement
- facial/lip/tongue swelling
- breathing symptoms with rash
- immunocompromised patient with significant infection
- fever with cellulitis and systemic symptoms
- eye involvement in shingles
- chemical skin exposure
- severe burn concern

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- onset and speed of spread
- fever/systemic symptoms
- pain severity
- medication exposure
- mucosal symptoms
- allergy/anaphylaxis symptoms
- immunosuppression
- travel/exposure/insect bite
- eye involvement

Check:

- appearance
- vitals if available
- blanching vs non-blanching rash
- mucosa
- skin tenderness
- spread/extent
- airway/breathing when swelling or allergy present

## Common GP traps

- Missing non-blanching rash.
- Treating pain-out-of-proportion cellulitis as routine.
- Missing mucosal involvement in severe drug reaction.
- Missing anaphylaxis when rash is not dramatic.

# Syncope & Palpitations in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP recognize dangerous syncope or palpitations.

## Dangerous causes

- arrhythmia
- ACS
- PE
- structural heart disease
- GI bleed/hypovolemia
- seizure mimic
- ectopic pregnancy
- medication/toxicology
- neurologic event when focal symptoms exist

## Red flags

Urgent referral if:

- syncope during exertion
- syncope while supine
- chest pain or dyspnea
- palpitations before syncope
- family history of sudden death
- known structural heart disease
- abnormal ECG if available
- persistent hypotension or tachycardia
- injury/head trauma/anticoagulant use
- pregnancy possibility
- elderly/frail patient
- recurrent episodes
- neurologic deficit
- GI bleeding symptoms

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- before/during/after event
- exertion or supine event
- palpitations
- chest pain/dyspnea
- injury
- seizure features
- medications
- pregnancy possibility
- cardiac history/family sudden death

Check:

- pulse/rhythm
- cardiac exam
- neurologic screen
- ECG if available and does not delay referral

## Common GP traps

- Calling syncope vasovagal too early.
- Missing exertional syncope.
- Ignoring palpitations before collapse.
- Ignoring abnormal ECG.

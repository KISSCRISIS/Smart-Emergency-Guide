# Trauma Referral Basics for GP

## Purpose

Help GP decide when injury needs ED/trauma assessment.

## Red flags

Urgent referral if:

- airway/breathing/circulation concern
- major mechanism
- head injury with reduced consciousness, vomiting, seizure, focal deficit, anticoagulant use, or worsening headache
- neck/back pain with neurologic symptoms
- chest trauma with breathlessness
- abdominal trauma with pain, tenderness, syncope, or shock appearance
- pelvic trauma
- open fracture or deformity
- neurovascular compromise
- crush injury
- significant burn concern
- non-accidental injury/safeguarding concern
- elderly fall with concerning symptoms or anticoagulant use

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- mechanism
- timing
- loss of consciousness
- vomiting/seizure/headache
- anticoagulants
- neck/back pain
- chest/abdominal symptoms
- limb sensation/movement
- safeguarding context

Check:

- ABCDE
- mental status
- bleeding
- neurovascular status
- pain out of proportion
- signs of chest/abdominal injury

## Common GP traps

- Focusing on the visible wound and missing internal injury.
- Underestimating elderly falls.
- Missing anticoagulant head injury risk.
- Missing safeguarding concerns.

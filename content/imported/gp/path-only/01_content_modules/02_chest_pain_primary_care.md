# Chest Pain in Primary Care

## Purpose

Help GP recognize chest pain that needs urgent ED assessment.

## Dangerous causes to consider

- acute coronary syndrome
- aortic dissection
- pulmonary embolism
- pneumothorax
- pericarditis/tamponade concern
- esophageal rupture
- pneumonia/sepsis
- arrhythmia presenting with chest discomfort

## Red flags

Urgent referral if chest pain is associated with:

- sweating
- shortness of breath
- syncope or near-syncope
- radiation to arm, jaw, back, or epigastrium
- tearing chest/back pain
- neurologic symptom
- hypotension or shock appearance
- persistent/worsening pain
- abnormal ECG if available
- known coronary disease
- diabetes/elderly with atypical symptoms
- pregnancy/postpartum with concerning symptoms
- stimulant use with severe symptoms or abnormal vitals

## GP first-contact assessment

Ask:

- onset and progression
- character and radiation
- exertional component
- dyspnea/syncope/sweating/nausea
- cardiac history and risk factors
- PE risk factors
- dissection clues
- recent infection/pericarditis clues
- stimulant use
- pregnancy/postpartum status when relevant

Check:

- appearance
- vital signs if available
- heart and lung exam
- pulse symmetry if dissection concern
- ECG if available and it does not delay referral

## Common GP traps

- Calling indigestion benign too early.
- Assuming a normal ECG excludes danger.
- Missing atypical ACS in elderly/diabetic patients.
- Reassuring pleuritic pain without considering PE, pneumothorax, pericarditis, or pneumonia.

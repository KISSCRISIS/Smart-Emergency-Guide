# GP Emergency Red Flags

## Purpose

Teach a simple danger-first mindset for any patient in clinic, urgent care, telehealth, or home visit.

## Universal red flags

Urgent ED/senior escalation if the patient has:

- airway concern
- severe shortness of breath
- cyanosis
- shock appearance
- severe chest pain
- syncope with ongoing symptoms
- reduced consciousness
- new focal neurologic deficit
- new seizure
- severe sudden headache
- severe abdominal pain with peritonism or collapse
- major trauma
- suspected sepsis
- suspected anaphylaxis
- pregnancy/postpartum red flags
- pediatric toxic appearance
- self-harm or unsafe mental state
- rapidly worsening symptoms
- clinician concern that the patient is â€œnot rightâ€

## High-risk groups

Lower threshold for referral in:

- infants
- elderly/frail patients
- pregnancy/postpartum
- immunocompromised patients
- anticoagulated patients
- dialysis/advanced kidney disease
- significant cardiac disease
- diabetes with systemic illness
- cancer/chemotherapy
- recent surgery
- serious psychiatric risk

## GP action

- Pause routine clinic flow.
- Assess ABCDE.
- Decide if ED/ambulance is needed.
- Do not let unstable/high-risk patients self-drive.
- Give a clear referral reason.
- Document red flags and advice.

## Common GP trap

Reassuring because the patient arrived walking.
